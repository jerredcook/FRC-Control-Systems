Part 5 · 02 The Drivetrain
COTS Drivetrains

Roll fast,
turn easy.

Most teams don't design a drivetrain from scratch, they buy a proven kit or build a standard six-wheel tank drive. The catch with six wheels is turning: they all want to grip and go straight. A clever trick called drop center fixes it.

A six-wheel tank turns by scrubbing.

A tank drive steers like a tank: spin the sides at different speeds and the wheels skid sideways to rotate the robot. With all six wheels flat on the floor that scrub is huge, the robot fights every turn. Drop center lowers the middle wheels slightly so the robot rocks onto them and pivots cleanly. Toggle it and turn.

Predict first
Before you run it: you turn the Drop center OFF, so all six wheels touch the floor flat, then press Turn. How does the robot turn?
With every wheel gripping flat, the front and back wheels have to skid sideways for the robot to rotate, which is scrub. That drag fights the turn, so it grinds around slowly and leaves skid marks. Lowering the center wheels (drop center) lets the robot pivot on them with little scrub. Run the sim with drop center off, press Turn, and watch the scrub meter jump to high and the skid marks appear.

Get a drivetrain working first, always

A robot that can't drive can't do anything, so the drivetrain is the first thing teams build and the last thing they want to be debugging at a competition. That's why kits are so popular: they're proven, they go together fast, and they free your time for the mechanisms that actually score. Rookie teams especially should start with a kit drive and a drop center, then spend their energy on the game-specific parts from Part 4.

Going further · optional

The drop is a real number teams tune: too little and the robot still fights its turns, too much and it rocks like a seesaw and loses straight-line traction because fewer wheels touch down hard. A common starting point is a small drop of about an eighth of an inch on the center wheels. Here is the question to sit with: a longer robot needs a bigger drop to turn the same way, so why does stretching the wheelbase make turning harder?

Show the thinking

A longer wheelbase puts the front and rear wheels farther from the pivot, so to rotate the robot those end wheels have to scrub through a bigger sideways arc, which means more scrub force to fight. A drop center beats that scrub by rocking the robot onto the center wheels and lifting the ends clear of the carpet, but the longer the frame, the more it tips for the same drop, so you need a bigger drop to lift the far ends enough. The tradeoff is that more drop also rocks the robot more, hurting straight-line traction, which is why long robots are harder to turn cleanly with a tank drive.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
How does a six-wheel tank drive turn?
Tank (skid-steer) drives turn by running the two sides at different speeds. The wheels have to scrub sideways against the floor to rotate the robot, there's no steering.
Question 2
What does dropping the center wheels do?
A drop center lowers the middle wheels slightly, so the robot tips onto them and turns about its center. That shortens the effective wheelbase and dramatically reduces scrub.
Question 3
Why do most teams start with a COTS or kit drivetrain?
A drivetrain has to just work, so teams lean on proven kits rather than reinventing one. That gets the robot driving quickly and saves the build time for the parts that score.

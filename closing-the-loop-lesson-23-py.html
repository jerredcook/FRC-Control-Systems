Vision-Assisted Control

Close the loop
on what it sees.

A camera hands you one number - how far off-target you are. Drive that number to zero and you've got auto-aim. It's the very first controller you ever built, fed by a new sensor.

Vision isn't a new kind of control. It's a new kind of sensor.

A camera - a Limelight or PhotonVision running on a coprocessor - spots an AprilTag or target and reports tx: the horizontal angle, in degrees, between where the robot is pointed and the target. That's an error. And you know exactly what to do with an error:

turn rate = −kP · tx setpoint = 0 (centered) · measurement = tx from the camera · it's a P controller

Drive tx to zero and the robot is aimed dead at the target. Lesson 2's proportional control, three years of build season later, pointed at a camera instead of an encoder.

Aim it

Auto-aim, live

The camera sees a target up the field. Move the target and watch tx - the offset - change. Flip auto-aim on and the robot rotates to drive tx to zero and lock on. Crank kP too high and the aim overshoots and hunts, exactly like an over-tuned PID.

In code

Read the offset, turn toward it

The aim loop is a few lines. Ask the camera for the target's yaw, multiply by a gain, and command a turn. When the target's centered, tx is ~0 and the turn stops:

Auto-aim Python · PhotonVision
result = self.camera.getLatestResult()
if result.hasTargets():
    tx = result.getBestTarget().getYaw()   # degrees off bore-sight
    omega = -kP * tx                          # omega = turn rate; drive tx -> 0
    self.drive.drive(0, 0, omega)              # rotate in place (swerve!)

And vision does something odometry can't: it gives an absolute fix. Odometry drifts as wheels slip; a known AprilTag tells the robot exactly where it is on the field. You fold that into the same pose estimator from the swerve lesson:

Vision corrects odometry drift Python · WPILib
# every loop, blend the camera's field-position estimate into odometry:
self.pose_estimator.addVisionMeasurement(estimated_pose, timestamp_seconds)
Two cautions the camera adds

Latency: a camera frame is tens of milliseconds old, so you time-stamp measurements and let the estimator account for the delay. Trust: a single noisy frame shouldn't yank the robot - you reject wild jumps and weight vision against odometry. The control idea stays simple; the plumbing is where the care goes.

Going further · optional

Pure proportional auto-aim hides a flaw that only shows up against a moving target: because the turn rate is only kP times the current offset, the robot is always chasing where the target was a frame ago, not where it is now, so it forever lags a hair behind. The camera's tens-of-milliseconds delay makes that lag worse. One fix is to aim at where the target is heading rather than where it sits, the way you lead a thrown ball with your glove instead of reaching for where it already passed. Worth chewing on: against a target drifting sideways at a steady speed, would a plain P loop ever truly settle on zero offset, or hold a small steady miss?

Show the thinking

It holds a small steady miss, never true zero. A P loop only turns when there is an offset, so to keep pace with a target moving at constant speed it needs a constant turn rate, which means it must keep a nonzero offset to generate that rate. That leftover gap is a steady-state error, and it grows with the target's speed and shrinks with higher kP, but it never reaches zero. The honest fixes are to add the target's velocity as a feedforward, lead the aim point, or add an I term, rather than chasing it with kP alone.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does the camera's tx represent in control terms?
tx is the angle between bore-sight and the target. The setpoint is 0 (centered), so tx is the error. Multiply it by kP and you have a turn command - proportional control with a camera for a sensor.
Question 2
Crank the aim kP way up. What happens, and why?
It's the exact same proportional loop from Lesson 2. Too much gain overreacts to the error, swings past center, and oscillates. Vision doesn't change the control rules one bit.
Question 3
Besides aiming, what can vision do that wheel odometry can't?
Odometry accumulates error as wheels slip. A recognized AprilTag has a known field location, so it pins down where the robot truly is - you blend it in with addVisionMeasurement to keep the pose honest.

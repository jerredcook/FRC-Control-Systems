Wiring Motors & Controllers

Right wire,
right fuse.

Each motor runs through its own controller on a fused branch off the PDH. The wire has to be thick enough to carry the current without overheating, and the breaker has to protect that wire while still letting the motor pull its normal load.

Match the wire and the breaker to the current.

A bigger motor pulls more amps, so it needs thicker wire (a lower AWG number) and a bigger breaker. Too thin and the wire overheats; too small a breaker and it trips every time the motor works hard; too big and it won't protect the wire. Size both for each motor below.

A thin wire (18 AWG) is fine for a little motor but melts under a drive motor's current. The breaker should sit just above the motor's normal draw and at or below what the wire can handle, that's the sweet spot.

Power is only half the wiring

Two heavy wires carry power to each motor controller, but a third, lighter wire carries data: the CAN bus that tells the controller what to do (that's the next lesson). And mind polarity, swapping the two power wires just makes the motor spin backward, which you can fix in code, but a reversed battery connection can destroy electronics. Color-code red for positive, black for negative, and check before you connect the battery.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
A motor pulls more current than your wire can carry. What happens?
Too much current in too thin a wire turns into heat, which can melt insulation or start a fire. A bigger motor needs a thicker wire (lower AWG number).
Question 2
How should you size the branch breaker?
The breaker should let the motor pull its normal current without nuisance-tripping, but still trip to protect the wire from a fault. Too small trips constantly; too big leaves the wire unprotected.
Question 3
Which wire is thicker and carries more current?
Wire gauge runs backward: the smaller the AWG number, the thicker the wire and the more current it carries. 10 AWG is much thicker than 18 AWG.

Plate, Sheet & Polycarbonate

Flat stock,
and what bends.

Tubes are the skeleton; flat stock is everything else, gearbox plates, panels, and clear guards. The biggest gotcha is plastic: polycarbonate bends and bounces back, but its look-alike, acrylic, shatters.

Same shape, very different behavior.

Clamp a flat panel at one end and push down on the free end. Polycarbonate flexes far and springs back. Acrylic, which looks identical, snaps. Thin aluminum bends and stays bent. Pick a material and load it up.

Push the load up on each. Polycarbonate just keeps flexing and recovers. Acrylic reaches its limit and shatters. Aluminum bends past a point and takes a permanent set. This is why guards and bellypans are polycarbonate, never acrylic.

Polycarbonate, never acrylic

This trips up every new team. The two clear plastics look identical on the shelf, but acrylic is brittle and cracks the first time the robot takes a hit, while polycarbonate (Lexan) flexes and absorbs the impact. For anything on a robot that might get bumped, a guard, a bellypan, a panel over electronics, use polycarbonate. Acrylic belongs in a picture frame, not a match.

Going further · optional

Polycarbonate has one quiet trap: drill a hole too close to the edge or leave a sharp inside corner, and a crack can start there and run, even in this tough plastic. Teams fix this by rounding inside corners and keeping holes back from the edge so stress has nowhere to concentrate. A design challenge to try on paper: sketch a polycarbonate bracket with a slot in it, then redraw it so every corner is a smooth curve instead of a sharp 90 degrees, and think about why the curved version survives more hits.

Show the thinking

A sharp inside corner concentrates stress, meaning the force from a hit piles up into that one tiny point instead of spreading out, and that is where a crack starts and runs. Rounding the corner with a fillet gives the load a wider path to flow around, so the peak stress drops and the part shrugs off the impact. A good redrawn bracket rounds every inside corner and the ends of the slot, and keeps holes set back from the edge. You see the same trick everywhere stress matters, like the rounded corners of a phone screen or an airplane window instead of square ones.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
You need a clear guard that can take a hit. Which plastic?
Polycarbonate (Lexan) bends and bounces back instead of shattering. Acrylic looks identical but is brittle and cracks on impact, exactly what you don't want in a match.
Question 2
What's aluminum plate (1/8" to 1/4") mainly used for?
Thick aluminum plate is the structural flat stock, it carries real loads as gearbox plates, gussets, and mounts. Thin sheet is for light panels and covers.
Question 3
You bend a thin aluminum panel hard and let go. What happens?
Past its yield point, aluminum deforms permanently and keeps the bend. That's useful when you want to form a bracket, but a surprise if you expected it to spring back like polycarbonate.

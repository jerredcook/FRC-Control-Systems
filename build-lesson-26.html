Bumpers

Wrap it in
foam.

Bumpers are the least glamorous part of the robot and one of the few the rules absolutely require. They protect your robot and everyone else's in a contact sport, and they have to be built a specific way and sit in a specific height zone, or you fail inspection.

The bumper has to live in a legal zone.

The rules define a height range, the bumper zone, that your bumpers must sit within so robots hit each other consistently and safely. Slide the bumper up and down and find the legal band.

Predict first
Before you run it: you slide the Bumper position all the way to the top, well above the green zone. What does inspection say?
The rule fixes a narrow band where every robot's bumper has to sit, so they all bump at the same height. Mount it above that band and it fails as too high; below it and it fails as too low, no matter how good the rest of the build is. Run the sim, push the bumper to the top, and watch the inspection readout flip to too high.

In the shop

Building legal bumpers

A bumper is simple to make but every detail is governed by the rules. The standard build is the same across nearly every team:

🪵
Wood backingA plywood board bolted hard to the frame, the bumper's structure.
🌭
Pool noodlesTwo stacked round noodles, the soft part that absorbs hits.
🧵
Fabric coverTight red or blue cloth, swappable for whichever alliance you're on.
🔢
Team numbersYour number on each side, sized and colored to the rules.
Bumpers are an inspection gate, build them early

You can't compete until an inspector signs off on your bumpers: right height, right construction, hard mounting, covering the whole frame perimeter, and a clean red and blue set. Teams that leave bumpers for the last night regret it, a failed bumper keeps the whole robot off the field. Build them early, make both colors, and mount them so they swap in seconds between matches. It's the unglamorous detail that quietly decides whether you get to play.

Going further · optional

Bumpers are not pure dead weight, smart teams make them earn their place. The wood backing can be cut to hug the frame and the noodle stack tuned so the bumper sits at the very top or bottom of the legal zone, which changes how your robot meets others: ride low and you can wedge under an opponent, ride high and you are harder to tip up. The rules also fuss over corners and gaps because a bumper that leaves a frame edge exposed can hook another robot. If you were defending, would you want your bumper riding high or low, and why?

Show the thinking

For defense, a low bumper is usually the better pick. Riding low lets you meet the opponent down near their wheels and even wedge under their frame, which lifts their drive wheels and kills their traction so they cannot push back or get away. It also keeps your contact point low, which makes you harder to tip and steadier when you slam into someone. The case for riding high is mostly to stay out from under a tall opponent who could ride up over you, so the right answer depends on what robots you expect to defend against.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Why does FRC require bumpers?
Robots collide constantly, so bumpers cushion the hits and keep frames from damaging each other. The rules standardize them so contact is fair and safe.
Question 2
A bumper is built perfectly but mounted too high. What happens at inspection?
Height is a hard rule. Even a well-built bumper outside the legal zone fails inspection, and the robot can't play until it's fixed.
Question 3
What's the standard bumper construction?
Wood backing for structure, stacked pool noodles for cushioning, a tight red or blue fabric cover, and your team number, the rules-defined recipe nearly every team follows.

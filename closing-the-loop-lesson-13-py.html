Motion Profiles

Give the move
a plan.

Stop snapping the setpoint and hoping. A motion profile maps out a smooth ramp-cruise-slow - and assembles the entire gold-standard controller in code.

Until now, every move has been a jump.

We've set the target to 0.7 and let the loop scramble to catch up. That works, but it's harsh: a huge error appears instantly, the controller slams full voltage, and the mechanism lurches. A motion profile fixes this by handing the controller a plan - a setpoint that eases up to speed, cruises, and gently slows into the target, never exceeding limits you choose.

And there's a bonus. A profile knows the intended velocity and acceleration at every instant - exactly what feedforward's kV and kA have been waiting for. Profile + feedforward + feedback is the complete controller from Part I, Lesson 8 - now in real code.

The whole controller

Everything, in code

WPILib bundles the profile and the PID into one class: ProfiledPIDController. You give it gains and motion limits; it generates the trapezoid plan and runs feedback against it. Pair it with an ElevatorFeedforward fed the profile's velocity, and you've built the gold standard:

elevator.py - the complete mechanism controller Python · WPILib
self.ff = ElevatorFeedforward(kS, kG, kV, kA)
self.pid = ProfiledPIDController(
    kP, 0, kD,
    TrapezoidProfile.Constraints(max_vel, max_accel),
)

def set_goal(self, meters):
    self.pid.setGoal(meters)

def periodic(self):
    measured = self.get_height_meters()
    fb_volts = self.pid.calculate(measured)                       # rides the profile
    ff_volts = self.ff.calculate(self.pid.getSetpoint().velocity)  # FF along the plan
    self.motor.setVoltage(ff_volts + fb_volts)

Eight lessons of intuition and five of code collapse into this. pid.getSetpoint().velocity is the profile's planned speed right now - hand it to feedforward and kV/kA do the heavy lifting, while feedback nudges out the rest.

This is the recipe - for everything

An arm swaps in ArmFeedforward and an angle; a flywheel uses velocity control instead of position. But the shape never changes: a planned motion, feedforward to predict it, feedback to correct it. You now have the controller behind essentially every FRC mechanism.

Going further · optional

A trapezoid profile speeds up at a steady rate, cruises, then slows at a steady rate, which means acceleration jumps instantly from zero to full at the very start. That sudden jolt, the change in acceleration, is called jerk, and you feel it as a thunk in a tall mechanism. WPILib also offers an ExponentialProfile and other tools soften the corners. Here is the question: a short hop might never reach max velocity at all. What shape does the speed plan take then, and is it still a trapezoid?

Show the thinking

The speed plan becomes a triangle: it speeds up until it is halfway there, then slows back down, with no flat cruise in the middle. You can think of it as a trapezoid whose cruise segment shrank to zero length, so it is the same plan handling a move too short to ever hit max velocity. TrapezoidProfile produces this on its own when the distance is small, so the code does not change.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does a motion profile give you that a bare setpoint doesn't?
A raw setpoint just says "be there now," creating a huge instant error. A profile lays out a smooth ramp-cruise-slow - and its velocity/acceleration are exactly what feedforward needs to keep error tiny throughout the move.
Question 2
A ProfiledPIDController combines which two things?
It generates the trapezoid motion profile and runs PID feedback against that moving setpoint - all in one object. You still add feedforward separately.
Question 3
In the gold-standard loop, what velocity do you pass to ElevatorFeedforward?
Feedforward needs to know how fast the plan says to be moving right now, so you feed it pid.getSetpoint().velocity. That lets kV and kA supply the voltage for the planned speed-up, cruise, and slow-down.

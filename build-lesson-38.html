Assembling the Robot

Build it in
order.

All the subsystems are done; now they become one machine. Assembly order matters more than it looks, because each layer you add covers up the one beneath it. Put the wiring in after the arm blocks it and you'll be fishing for connectors with tweezers.

Stack the robot from the base up.

Add the subsystems in the order that keeps everything reachable: structure first, then the parts that move it, then the wiring while you can still get to it, then the mechanisms, and bumpers last. Click them in the right order and watch the robot come together.

Design for assembly and maintenance from the start

The smartest teams think about this back in CAD: can a hand reach the battery to swap it? Can you pull a dead motor without removing the whole arm? A robot you can't service quickly will lose matches in the pit. Leave access panels, route wiring along reachable paths, and order the build so nothing important gets buried. It ties back to design for manufacture, the best design isn't just buildable, it's serviceable.

Going further · optional

Pros take "can you reach it later?" one step further and check tool access in CAD, not the part, the wrench. A bolt you can touch with a finger is useless if no socket can swing onto its head, so designers leave clearance for the actual driver and even pick fastener types (a hex bolt, a captive nut) for the space they have. Here is a challenge: pick the one part on a robot most likely to break mid-event, then trace in your head every tool and motion needed to swap it. If that takes more than a couple of minutes, the fix is a design change, not a faster pit crew.

Show the thinking

The usual suspects are the parts that take the hardest hits or run hottest: a drive motor, a gearbox, a wheel, or an intake mechanism out front where collisions happen. Trace the full swap and you often find the trap is access, not the part: the bolts are buried, a socket cannot swing onto the head, or you have to remove a second mechanism to reach the first. The fix is a design change you make now in CAD, like leaving wrench clearance, using captive nuts, or putting the part on an access panel, so the swap stays under a couple of minutes when it breaks at an event.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What gets assembled first?
The frame is the foundation. Every other subsystem mounts to it, so it has to come first.
Question 2
Why wire the electrical before adding the mechanisms?
Once an arm or intake fills the space, the wiring is buried. Run it while the inside is still open and reachable.
Question 3
What's the real reason assembly order matters?
Order is about access. Build so that nothing you'll need to reach later gets buried under something added earlier.

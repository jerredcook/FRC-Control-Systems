Gears & Gear Ratios

Trade speed
for torque.

A motor spins fast and weak. A robot needs slow and strong. Gears make the trade: a small gear driving a big one slows the output down and multiplies its force by the same amount. That ratio is the heart of every mechanism.

Count the teeth, and you know the ratio.

Two meshed gears turn in opposite directions, and their speeds are set by their tooth counts. Drive a 12-tooth gear into a 36-tooth gear and the output spins one-third the speed with three times the torque, a 3:1 reduction. Change the teeth below and watch the ratio.

In the shop

The one formula, and a few rules

Everything about gears comes from one idea: the ratio is the driven teeth divided by the driver teeth. From there:

➗
Ratio = driven ÷ driver36 driven / 12 driver = 3:1. Bigger driven = bigger reduction.
⚖️
Speed down, torque upA 3:1 reduction is 1/3 the speed and 3× the torque. They always trade.
🔁
Meshed gears reverseTwo gears spin opposite ways. Need the same direction? Add a third (idler).
📐
Same module to meshGears must share a tooth size (module / pitch) or they won't mesh.
Why you almost always reduce

A Kraken motor free-spins around 6000 rpm, way too fast and far too weak to move a robot or lift an arm. So nearly every mechanism gears down: a drivetrain might be 6:1, an arm 100:1 or more. The reduction turns the motor's speed into the force the job needs. Getting that ratio right, fast enough but strong enough, is one of the central design decisions on any mechanism.

Going further · optional

One pair of gears can only reduce so much before the big gear gets huge, so teams chain reductions in stages: gear down 4:1, then 4:1 again, and the ratios multiply to 16:1 in a compact gearbox. Try the math on a target: if an arm needs about 100:1, how would you split that across two or three stages with reasonable tooth counts? And a real trade-off to chew on, a bigger reduction gives more torque but also makes the output slower and adds friction at every mesh, so why might a team deliberately pick a little less reduction than the maximum they could fit?

Show the thinking

For about 100:1 in two stages you want each stage near the square root of 100, so roughly 10:1 and 10:1; a clean version is a 12-tooth driving a 60-tooth twice, which is 5:1 times 5:1 for 25:1, or split it 4:1 then 25:1, and the stages multiply. Three stages let you use smaller, easier-to-fit gears, like 4:1, 5:1, and 5:1 for 100:1. A team picks less than the maximum reduction when the mechanism is fast enough already and they would rather keep speed, save weight and space, or cut the friction that every extra mesh adds. More reduction is not free, it trades away speed and efficiency for torque you may not need.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
A 12-tooth gear drives a 48-tooth gear. What's the ratio?
Ratio is driven teeth over driver teeth: 48 / 12 = 4, a 4:1 reduction. The output spins a quarter the speed with four times the torque.
Question 2
A 3:1 reduction does what to speed and torque?
Reduction trades speed for torque, one for one. A 3:1 output spins at 1/3 the input speed and delivers 3× the torque. You never get both; you choose.
Question 3
Why do most FRC mechanisms gear the motor down, not up?
A motor free-spins around 6000 rpm with little torque. Reduction slows it to a useful speed and multiplies the force, which is what a drivetrain or arm actually needs.

Battery Science

The robot's
heartbeat.

The whole robot runs on one 12-volt battery. The reason a robot browns out and freezes mid-match lives inside that battery, in a part you cannot see from the outside.

One battery feeds everything.

Every motor, the radio, the roboRIO, every light, all of it pulls from a single 12-volt sealed lead-acid battery, often called an SLA. It holds about 18 amp-hours of charge and weighs roughly 12 to 13 pounds, so it is one of the heaviest single parts on the robot. Sitting on a shelf with nothing pulling on it, a fully charged one rests around 12.8 to 13.2 volts. That resting number is called the open-circuit voltage, and it is comforting, but it hides the real story.

Think of amp-hours as the size of the tank. About 18 amp-hours means the battery could hand out 18 amps steadily for an hour, or much bigger bursts for a much shorter time. An FRC match is only about 2.5 minutes long, and the draw is bursty, not steady: a drivetrain slams from a crawl to a sprint, then stops, then does it again. So the question is never only "how much charge is left." The real question is "how hard can it pour right now."

The water tank picture

Picture the battery as a water tank with a pipe at the bottom. The resting voltage is how full the tank is. The internal resistance is how narrow that pipe is. A fresh battery is a fat pipe: open the valve hard and water still pours out with pressure to spare. A tired battery is a narrow pipe: the tank can be completely full, but ask it to pour fast and the pressure at the spout drops away. Hold that picture, it is the whole lesson.

The key idea

Voltage sags under load

Here is the part most people miss. Every battery has a small resistance hiding inside it, called internal resistance. When current flows out, some voltage gets eaten up crossing that internal resistance before it ever reaches the wires. So the voltage you actually get at the terminals drops the moment the robot pulls hard. The rule is short:

terminal voltage = resting voltage - (current x internal resistance)

A healthy FRC battery has about 10 to 15 milliohms of internal resistance. A milliohm is a thousandth of an ohm, a tiny number, but the currents in FRC are huge, so it adds up fast. A worn or old battery climbs to 20 to 30 or more milliohms, and that higher number is exactly the narrow pipe from the tank picture.

Work the numbers

Pull 100 amps through 15 milliohms and you lose 100 x 0.015 = 1.5 volts. So a battery resting at 12.8 volts sags to about 11.3 volts while that current flows. Still fine. But a hard stall surge of 250 amps sags it far more and drags it down toward the brownout zone, which is the next lesson's whole topic. Now swap in a tired battery at 30 milliohms: that same 100 amps eats 3 volts, and the surge is brutal. Same robot, same demand, but the tired battery sags roughly twice as far.

In the pit

Resting voltage lies

This is the trap that bites teams every season. Age, heat, and deep discharges permanently raise a battery's internal resistance, and once it is up, it never comes back down. The cruel part is that a tired battery can still read a perfectly healthy 12.8 volts at rest. The tank is full. But the pipe has narrowed, and you cannot see that until you ask it to pour. So a resting-voltage check tells you the tank is full, and tells you nothing about whether the battery can survive a match.

The real test happens under load

Because resting voltage cannot reveal health, the only honest test is to make the battery work and watch how far it sags. A fresh battery holds its terminal voltage up; a tired one collapses for the same current. That is exactly what the next two lessons set up: how the amps add up into a power budget, and how to actually rank your batteries by how well they hold up under a real load. For now, the takeaway is one sentence: never trust a battery on its resting number alone.

Going further · optional

There is a wrinkle in that tidy "18 amp-hours" rating. The number is measured at a gentle, steady draw, but FRC batteries get hammered with short, hard bursts of hundreds of amps. The harder and faster you pull, the less total charge you actually get out, so a battery that should last on paper can fade early under brutal use. Engineers call this the Peukert effect, and in plain words it means heavy bursty draws "waste" capacity that a slow draw would have kept. Here is one to chew on with a mentor: if two robots both drain the same battery, but one sprints in hard stop-and-go bursts and the other cruises smoothly, which one runs out of usable battery first, and why?

Show the thinking

The hard stop-and-go sprinter runs out of usable battery first. That is the Peukert effect: the harder and faster you pull current, the less of the rated 18 amp-hours you actually get, so big bursts "waste" capacity that a gentle, steady draw would have kept. The bursts also drag the terminal voltage down hard each time, since the loss across the battery's internal resistance grows with current, so you hit the brownout floor sooner even with charge still left. The smooth cruiser pulls fewer peak amps, stays closer to the rated capacity, and holds its voltage up longer.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
A battery rests at 12.8 volts. The robot pulls a big current. What happens to the voltage at the terminals?
Terminal voltage = resting voltage minus (current times internal resistance). The harder the robot pulls, the more voltage gets eaten inside the battery, so the terminals sag. That sag is the whole reason robots brown out.
Question 2
A battery reads a healthy 12.8 volts at rest. What does that prove?
Resting voltage shows how full the tank is, not how narrow the pipe has become. A worn battery with high internal resistance can still rest at 12.8 volts. You only see health by testing under load.
Question 3
Why does a tired, higher-resistance battery brown out sooner than a fresh one?
The drop is current times internal resistance. Double the resistance and the same current causes roughly double the sag, so a tired battery dives toward the brownout zone on bursts a fresh one would shrug off, even when both rest at the same voltage.

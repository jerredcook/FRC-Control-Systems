Question 1
A 12 tooth gear drives a 48 tooth gear, with the motor spinning the small one at 6000 rpm. What is the ratio and the output?
Ratio is driven divided by driver, 48 over 12 equals 4:1. A reduction divides speed by 4 (6000 to 1500 rpm) and multiplies torque by about 4. Speed and torque always trade inversely.
Question 2
A chain on an arm keeps skipping teeth under load. The most likely cause and fix is:
Chain that skips under load is almost always too loose, and new chain goes slightly slack over a season. Add a tensioner, a movable mount, or a half-link to take up the slack. Too tight wears it fast, but it is the rarer problem here.
Question 3
You need a 25:1 gearbox reduction from stackable planetary cartridges that come in 3:1, 4:1, and 5:1. Which stack works, and why does it work?
Gearbox stages multiply, so total ratio is stage one times stage two. Two 5:1 cartridges give 5 times 5 equals 25:1. They do not add, and 4 times 4 is only 16:1, not close enough.
Question 4
A gear sits on the very end of a shaft with one bearing back near the gearbox. The shaft visibly bows and wobbles as it spins. The cleanest fix is to:
One bearing leaves the gear cantilevered like a diving board, so it bows and wobbles (runout). A bearing on each side of the load runs the shaft dead straight. A fatter shaft also helps, since stiffness climbs sharply with diameter.
Question 5
Connecting back to picking the right size: you must lift a 12 lb load in under one second and you size it in ReCalc. You pick a huge reduction. ReCalc shows plenty of force but the lift crawls and current climbs toward the 120 A breaker. What do you do?
Too much reduction has force to spare but is too slow and drags current up toward the breaker. Too little cannot make the lift at all. The sizing loop is to adjust the ratio and re-check in ReCalc until speed, force, and current all sit in a safe band, then cut metal.

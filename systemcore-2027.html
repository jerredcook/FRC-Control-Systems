The roboRIO is
retiring.

Starting in the 2027 season, a new board called SystemCore replaces the roboRIO and the radio. Here is what is changing and what stays the same.

2027 Preview Read this first

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing as of 2026. The official details and software are not final. Exact specs and wiring will change before the 2027 kickoff.

This page is a concept preview, not a build guide. Before you rely on any specific number, port, or step here, confirm it against the official WPILib and FIRST sources linked at the bottom of this page. Treat everything below as "the plan so far," not "the final word."

The big picture

What's changing

For about a decade, two boxes have sat at the heart of every FRC robot: the roboRIO, the brain that runs your code, and a separate wireless radio that talks to the driver station over Wi-Fi. Starting in the 2027 season, both of those get replaced by a single new board called SystemCore.

This is a hard cutover, not a slow transition. Teams will not run a roboRIO alongside SystemCore in 2027. Every team gets a SystemCore in the 2027 Kit of Parts, and that becomes the brain of the robot. SystemCore is built by Limelight, the same company behind the popular vision cameras, and it is aimed to cost less than a roboRIO did.

The hardware

What SystemCore is

In plain words, SystemCore is a small but powerful Linux computer with robot wiring built into it. It is about the size of a large phone. Inside, it is built around a Raspberry Pi Compute Module 5 (CM5), which has a quad-core ARM Cortex-A76 processor and 4 GB of RAM, running real-time Linux. Alongside that sits a Raspberry Pi RP2350 chip that handles the reconfigurable I/O, the pins that read sensors and drive outputs.

A water-tank way to picture it

Think of the old setup like a house where the water heater, the pump, and the water meter all sat in separate boxes wired together. SystemCore is like buying one appliance that has the heater, the pump, and the meter built into a single unit. You still get the same water, but now you have fewer boxes to plumb together and less wiring to get wrong. More of the computer lives in one box.

A lot is built right in that used to be separate parts you bolted on:

🧭
Onboard IMUA built-in gyro and accelerometer for heading and motion.
👁️
Limelight visionPlug in a webcam and every team gets vision processing.
📶
Wi-Fi radio insideA 2.4 and 5 GHz radio is built in. No separate radio box.
💡
Status LEDs + displayLights and a small screen to read what the board is doing.

For connecting everything to the robot, SystemCore brings the ports you would expect plus some new room to grow:

  • CAN, I2C, USB, and Ethernet for motor controllers, sensors, and accessories.
  • Reconfigurable I/O for PWM, digital I/O, and analog input, the same kinds of pins the roboRIO had.
  • An M.2 slot that can take a Hailo-8 AI accelerator, a small add-on chip for teams pushing into machine learning.
For the build team

What it means if you build the robot

The biggest day-to-day change is fewer separate boxes on the robot. Because the radio lives inside SystemCore, there is no separate radio to mount, power, and run an Ethernet cable to. That is one whole subsystem gone from your wiring diagram. The built-in IMU and the built-in vision mean a couple more parts you used to add yourself are already there.

The flip side is that there is new wiring to learn. The ports, the layout, and the way you lay out the board on the robot will look different from a roboRIO, so plan to relearn that part when the official wiring guides come out.

Your power and battery knowledge still holds

Everything you learned about the battery, the main breaker, fuses, voltage drop, and brownouts is fundamental electricity, and it does not change because the brain changed. A 12 volt battery still powers the robot, current still flows through your wiring the same way, and good power management still keeps the robot from browning out. SystemCore is a new box to feed, but the rules of feeding it are the rules you already know.

For the programmers

What it means if you write the code

The most visible change for coders is a brand-new Driver Station app. The old one only ran on Windows. The new one runs on Windows, macOS, and Linux, so your team is not locked to a single kind of laptop anymore. Gamepads also get an upgrade: finer 16-bit axes for smoother stick reads, and up to 64 buttons. One catch to remember: only SystemCore uses this new Driver Station, so it goes hand in hand with the new board.

Under the hood, your code still lives in WPILib, and WPILib 2027 will bring a unified software experience across FRC and the FIRST Tech Challenge. The big ideas you have been learning carry straight over: command-based structure, your control theory, PID, feedforward, and so on all still apply. What changes is the exact setup and deploy steps, the project templates, and how you push code to the board. Expect to relearn the plumbing, not the concepts.

Concepts carry, the buttons move

Picture moving from one phone to a newer phone. Your contacts, your habits, and the apps you know all come along; the menus sit in slightly different places. Going from roboRIO to SystemCore feels similar for code. What you understand stays valuable. You mostly relearn where things live.

Don't panic

What stays the same

Most of this academy does not change. The brain of the robot is new, but the body and the thinking around it are not. Here is what carries over untouched:

📐
Control theoryPID, feedforward, and motion still work the same way.
💻

⚙️
Mechanisms and motorsGears, drivetrains, and motor behavior are unchanged.
🔌
CAN devicesMotor controllers and sensors on the CAN bus still apply.
🔋
Power managementBattery, breakers, and brownout sense all hold.
🛠️
How you buildThe design process and shop skills do not change.

So if you are early in the academy, keep going. Almost everything you are learning now is exactly as useful in 2027 as it is today.

Honest gaps

What we don't know yet

SystemCore has already run in over 1,000 offseason matches with positive feedback, which is a strong sign. But the software and the docs are still early, alpha and beta, and several important details are not locked down. As of 2026 we do not yet have final answers on:

  • Exact electrical specs, such as the input voltage range and the brownout threshold.
  • Final wiring, meaning the official port layout and connection diagrams.
  • The final software workflow, including how you create projects and deploy code.
  • Vendor library details from the companies that make your motor controllers and sensors.

These land closer to the 2027 kickoff. When they do, the official sources below are where the real numbers will live, so check there before you wire or program anything for real.

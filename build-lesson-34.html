Part 7 · 03 Design & CAD
Prototyping

Test it
cheap first.

A drawing can look perfect and still fail in real life. Smart teams build a rough, throwaway version out of cardboard or wood to find the problems while they're free to fix, then climb up to metal only once the cheap mockup has answered the hard questions.

Climb the fidelity ladder.

The same bracket can be made in cardboard in minutes, plywood in an hour, or aluminum in an afternoon. Each step up costs more time and money but gets you closer to the real thing. Slide through the materials and watch the trade between speed and realism.

Cardboard gets you a dozen quick tries to nail the shape and motion; aluminum gives you maybe two and answers "is it match-ready?" Use the cheap rungs to settle the cheap questions, and only spend metal once you're confident.

In the shop

Why rough beats perfect (at first)

A prototype isn't a worse robot, it's a question you're asking quickly and cheaply:

⚡
Fail fast, fail cheapFind the flaw in cardboard, not in finished aluminum.
🔁
IterateCheap means many tries; each one teaches you something.
🤝
Test the real questionWill it reach? Will it grab? Build just enough to find out.
🪜
Match fidelity to the questionCardboard for shape, wood for motion, metal for the real load.
Twenty minutes of cardboard saves a week of aluminum

The most expensive mistake is the one you discover after machining the final part. A taped-together cardboard intake that proves the geometry is wrong has just saved you days of rework, for free. Prototyping pairs perfectly with CAD: model it, mock it up, learn, update the model, then commit. The teams that win aren't the ones who guess right the first time, they're the ones who cheaply tried the most ideas.

Going further · optional

The sharpest teams do not prototype the whole mechanism, they prototype the one thing they are least sure about. If you do not know whether your intake can grab the game piece, build only the grabbing surface and the geometry around it, in cardboard, and ignore the rest. Naming the riskiest unknown out loud first ("will it reach?" or "will it hold under load?") tells you both what to build and which rung of the fidelity ladder actually answers it: a shape question is cheap cardboard, but a real-load question may need metal. Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Why prototype before building the final part?
A rough mockup surfaces flaws early, when fixing them costs minutes and cardboard instead of days and aluminum.
Question 2
What should you usually prototype with first?
Start at the bottom of the ladder. Cheap materials let you try many ideas quickly before committing real time and money.
Question 3
When should you commit to the final metal version?
Climb the ladder: once cardboard and wood have proven the shape, fit, and motion, you commit to metal with confidence.

Elevators & Lifts

Go straight
up.

When you need clean vertical reach, an elevator beats an arm. And with the right rigging, a multi-stage elevator does something surprising: the carriage climbs faster than the cable you're pulling, reaching twice as high for the same motor turn.

Stages telescope, and the rigging multiplies travel.

An elevator is one or more stages that slide up out of each other. How you route the cable, the rigging, decides how the stages move. Cascade rigging makes each stage drive the next, so the carriage moves several times faster than the cable. Continuous rigging moves them one-to-one. Extend it both ways.

Predict first
Before you run it: with the rigging set to Cascade, you drag the slider so the cable is pulled 50 percent of the way. How high does the carriage go?
In a two-stage cascade each moving stage adds the cable's motion, so the carriage travels twice the cable. Pull 50 percent and the carriage hits the full 100 percent reach. Continuous rigging is the one-to-one case. Run the sim, leave it on Cascade, set the slider to 50 percent, and watch the carriage land on the dashed max-reach line.

In the shop

Cascade vs continuous

Both rig the same stages; they just route the cable differently. The choice trades reach against the load on your rigging:

⬆️
CascadeEach stage drives the next, so the carriage moves N× the cable speed.
📏
More reach per turnA 2-stage cascade doubles travel for the same motor rotation.
🪢
ContinuousStages move together, one-to-one with the cable. Simpler, even loading.
⚖️
The tradeoffCascade's speed comes with more tension on the cable, and less lifting force.
Speed and force always trade, even here

Cascade rigging moves the carriage twice as fast, but by the same rule as gears, it also has half the lifting force and puts more tension on the cable and pulleys. Continuous rigging is slower but stronger and easier on the hardware. Pick cascade when you need height and speed and the load is light; pick continuous when you're lifting something heavy and want it simple and reliable. It's the speed-versus-torque trade from Part 3, wearing a different hat.

Going further · optional

The same speed-versus-force trade is hiding in your gearbox choice too, so you get to pick where the multiplication happens. A 3-stage cascade triples carriage speed but the motor must supply triple the force, so you might add gear reduction to get that force back, which slows it down again. Try reasoning through it: if you need a tall reach quickly, is it smarter to add an elevator stage or to gear the motor for more pulling power? Often the cleanest answer is a 2-stage cascade with a sensible ratio, not the most aggressive rigging you can build.

Show the thinking

Adding cascade stages and adding gear reduction push the same trade in opposite directions, so the smart move is to decide your needed height first, then size the ratio to that height. Stages buy you reach and carriage speed but multiply the force the motor must hold, while reduction buys back force and holding power at the cost of speed. Pick the fewest stages that physically reach the target, since each extra stage adds rigging, friction, and a heavier moving mass, then gear the motor so it can both lift the load and stop it without stalling or slamming. A 2-stage cascade with a moderate ratio usually wins because it reaches high enough, keeps the carriage controllable, and leaves margin so the motor is not pinned at its limit the whole match.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
When is an elevator a better choice than an arm?
An elevator moves its carriage in a straight vertical line. When the job is pure height with no need to swing, that's simpler and more predictable than an arm's arc.
Question 2
In a 2-stage cascade elevator, how fast does the carriage move vs the cable?
Cascade rigging stacks the stage motions: with two moving stages the carriage travels twice the cable, reaching higher per motor turn. Continuous rigging is one-to-one.
Question 3
What's the downside of cascade's extra speed?
Speed and force trade. Cascade moves twice as fast but lifts with half the force and loads the rigging harder. For heavy lifts, continuous is the safer, stronger choice.

Lesson 01 Foundations
Feedback & Control

The one idea
everything is built on.

Before any code. Before any math. Just the loop you've been running your whole life - made deliberate.

You already know how to do this.

Every time you lean your bike back upright when it starts to tip, nudge the shower knob because the water's too cold, or ease off as your cup fills near the top, you're running a control loop. You look at where things are, compare it to where you want them, and adjust. Then you do it again. And again. Without ever thinking about it.

This whole course is about teaching a robot to do that same thing - on purpose, precisely, in code. But the idea underneath it is something you've done since you learned to walk. So let's not start with code. Let's start by feeling it.

What just happened

That was feedback control

You watched the gap between where the marker was and where you wanted it (the line). You thrust harder when it sank, eased off when it rose. Pull that apart and you get the three words this entire field is built on:

Setpoint

Where you want to be. The dashed line - your goal.

Measurement

Where you actually are. The marker - what's really happening.

Error

The gap between them. Setpoint minus measurement.

A controller is the thing that looks at the error and decides what to do about it - over and over. You were the controller just now. For the rest of the course, we hand that job to the robot.

The big distinction

Two ways to control anything

Open loop is "set it and hope." You pick an action up front, commit to it, and never check the result. Like microwaving popcorn for exactly 3:00 and walking away - if your microwave runs hot, you burn it, because nothing is watching.

Closed loop is "watch and correct." You keep measuring the result and adjust as you go. That feedback - the loop running from measurement back to action - is what makes control survive surprises.

Same thruster below, run both ways. It's tuned perfectly for dead-calm air. Now drag the disturbance slider to add a gust, and watch what each one does.

The shape of it

The control loop

Every controller in this course - from the one line you'll write next lesson to the state-space math at the end - is this same loop, running about 50 times a second:

Measure. Compare to the setpoint. Compute the error. Act. Repeat. Master this shape and everything else is just choosing a smarter box in the middle.

Your test bench

Meet the elevator

For the rest of the course we control one machine: a robot elevator - a carriage that rides up and down on rails, lifted by a motor. It's the perfect teacher because it has every ingredient that makes control interesting:

Gravity always pulling it down. Weight that resists speeding up and slowing down. And a crisp, testable job: go to a height, then hold it. Everything you just felt with the thruster applies directly - from here on, we get precise about it.

Going further · optional

Closed loop survives surprises, but there is a catch: every loop only acts on what the sensor read a moment ago, so there is always a tiny delay between the gust hitting and the correction arriving. Think about riding a bike with your eyes closed for half a second at a time. What happens to a closed loop if that delay gets long, or if the sensor lies to it? Hold onto that question - it is exactly where overshoot, oscillation, and the hardest tuning problems come from later in this course.

Show the thinking

A long delay makes the loop correct on stale readings, so it keeps pushing after the error is already gone and overshoots, then swings back the other way, which is oscillation. A lying sensor is worse: the loop trusts it completely, so it drives the real mechanism toward whatever wrong value the sensor reports. Both turn the loop's strength into a weakness, and both are why high gains plus delay add up to instability.

Checkpoint

Quick check

No math - just the ideas. Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
A microwave that heats for a fixed time no matter what's inside is an example of…
Open loop. It commits to an action (3:00 of heating) and never measures the result, so it can't tell whether the food is cold, hot, or on fire.
Question 2
In control terms, the gap between where you want to be and where you actually are is called the…
Error - it's the setpoint minus the measurement. Almost everything a controller does is a reaction to this single number.
Question 3
Why does a closed-loop controller handle surprises (like a gust of wind) better than an open-loop one?
It keeps measuring. Because it's constantly comparing where it is to where it wants to be, an unexpected push just shows up as error - and it corrects. Open loop never looks back, so it can't react to anything it didn't expect.

Part 2 · 01 The Hardware
The Brain & Smart I/O

One small
computer.

SystemCore is a tiny Linux computer with robot wiring built in. A real processor runs your code, a helper chip handles the plug-in ports, and one clever idea lets a single port become a PWM, digital, or analog connection with a change in software.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

Two chips, one job each.

Think of SystemCore like your phone. There is a main computer that does the thinking, and there are smaller helper parts that talk to the outside world so the main computer does not have to babysit every wire. SystemCore splits the work the same way: one chip is the brain, and a second chip runs the ports your robot plugs into.

🧠
The brain: a CM5 computerA Raspberry Pi Compute Module 5: a 4-core ARM Cortex-A76 CPU, 4 GB of RAM, a VideoCore VII GPU, and real-time Linux. This is where your robot code runs.
🔌
The helper: an RP2350 chipA separate Raspberry Pi RP2350 microcontroller. Its one job is running the reconfigurable Smart I/O, so the ports stay fast and steady while the brain thinks.
Why split the work

A full computer running Linux is great at big jobs like vision and planning, but it is not the best at flicking a wire on and off at an exact rhythm. A small dedicated chip like the RP2350 is. So the brain decides what to do, and the helper chip handles the steady ticking of the ports. You get the best of both.

The clever part

Smart I/O: one port, you pick its job

On the roboRIO today, each port has a fixed job. A PWM port is always a PWM port, a digital port is always digital, and so on. SystemCore changes that. Its "reconfigurable Smart I/O" means a single port can act as a PWM output, a Digital I/O pin, or an Analog Input, and you choose which one in software.

The multi-tool analogy

A Smart I/O port is like a multi-tool with one handle and several fold-out tools. The plug never changes, but you decide whether it acts as a screwdriver, a knife, or a file. Here it is one socket, and you tell it in code whether to be PWM, digital, or analog. That means you are not stuck with a fixed map of ports, you arrange them to fit your robot.

The rest of the connections are the standard ways robots and computers talk to each other:

🟦
USB 3.0Fast ports for things like a camera. Multiple of them.
🔗
CAN buses · CAN FDThe wiring most motors and smart sensors use. CAN FD runs at 1 Mbps.
📟
I2CA simple two-wire bus for small sensors.
🌐
EthernetA wired network link for data and setup.
CAN FD, in plain words

CAN FD is a faster, higher-bandwidth version of the CAN bus robots use today. "Higher bandwidth" means more room to send messages, so devices can report back more often without crowding the line. That leaves plenty of room for fast device updates, which helps when a robot has lots of motors and sensors all talking at once.

Still being finalized

The exact number of each port (how many USB, how many CAN buses, how many Smart I/O) is not finalized yet. Treat any count you hear as a draft until the official sources confirm it.

Going further · optional

Splitting a system into a big general chip and a small dedicated one is a pattern you see everywhere, from game consoles to cars. The big chip is flexible but busy, the small chip is simple but rock-steady at one timing-critical job. Here is one to chew on with a mentor: if the brain chip is briefly busy crunching a camera frame, why is it a good thing that the RP2350, not the brain, is the part holding a motor's PWM signal steady?

Show the thinking

A PWM signal has to keep perfect timing, every pulse on schedule, or the motor twitches or stutters. The brain chip runs many things at once and can get tied up for a moment on a big job like a camera frame, so its timing is not always exact to the microsecond. The small dedicated chip does almost nothing else, so it can hold that steady beat no matter how busy the brain gets. Handing the timing-critical job to the part that is never distracted keeps the motor smooth, which is the whole reason for splitting the work this way.

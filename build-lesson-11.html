Part 3 · 02 Power Transmission
Sprockets & Chain

Span the
gap.

Gears have to touch to mesh, so they can't move power very far. Chain solves that: it loops over two sprockets that might be a foot apart and carries the same ratio across the gap, the way a bike chain links the pedals to the rear wheel.

A chain is a flexible gear train.

The ratio works exactly like gears, driven teeth over driver teeth, but the two sprockets can sit wherever your design needs them. Change the sprocket sizes and the spacing below and watch the chain carry the power across.

Predict first
Before you run it: you leave the two sprockets at 18 and 36 teeth and drag the center distance slider to spread them farther apart. What changes?
Ratio depends only on the tooth counts, driven over driver, so moving the sprockets apart leaves it at 2:1. What changes is the chain length, which is why you must buy or build a chain to match the spacing. The sprockets also keep spinning the same direction, unlike meshed gears. In the shop

Picking and tensioning chain

FRC uses roller chain in a couple of sizes, joined into a loop with a master link. The one thing chain demands that gears don't is the right tension:

🔗
#25 chainSmall and light. The default for most FRC mechanisms.
⛓️
#35 chainBigger and stronger, for high-load jobs like a heavy drivetrain.
🔓
Master linkA removable link that closes the loop so you can install it.
↔️
Tension mattersToo loose and it skips teeth; too tight and it wears fast.
Plan for tension from the start

Chain stretches a little as it wears, so a brand-new tight chain goes slightly slack over a season. Good designs build in a way to take up that slack: a tensioner (an idler sprocket on a spring or slot), or a movable sprocket mount you can slide. A common trick is the half-link, which lets you shorten a loop by one pitch. If a chain is skipping under load, it's almost always too loose.

Going further · optional

Chain length comes in whole links, so two sprockets only mesh cleanly at certain center distances. Try this: pick a driver and driven sprocket, then ask whether you can place them exactly where your frame wants them, or whether you need a tensioner to soak up the leftover. Teams that plan this early avoid the classic mistake of cutting a chain one link too short with no way to take up the slack.

Show the thinking

Usually no, not exactly. Chain only comes in whole links, so the loop snaps to certain lengths, and the spacing your frame wants will rarely land right on one of those steps. So you either nudge the center distance to the nearest length that works out to whole links, or you leave a little extra and add a tensioner or a slotted, movable sprocket mount to soak up the leftover. Planning a tensioner from the start is the safe move, since it also takes up the slack as the chain wears and stretches over a season.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What can chain do that meshed gears can't?
Gears must touch to transfer power. Chain loops over two sprockets that can be far apart, moving the same ratio across a distance, like a bike chain.
Question 2
An 18T sprocket drives a 54T sprocket. What's the ratio?
Sprocket ratios work just like gears: driven teeth over driver teeth. 54 / 18 = 3, a 3:1 reduction. The output spins a third as fast with triple the torque.
Question 3
A chain is skipping teeth under load. Most likely cause?
A slack chain rides up and jumps over the sprocket teeth. Tighten it with a tensioner or by moving a sprocket, or remove a half-link, so it seats properly under load.

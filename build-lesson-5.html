Fasteners

Tight, but not
too tight.

A robot is held together by hundreds of bolts. Pick the right size, thread it into the right hole, and torque it just enough. Too loose and it rattles out mid-match; too tight and you strip the threads.

There's a sweet spot for tightness.

Tighten a bolt and the clamping force climbs. Stop too early and vibration backs it out. Keep going past the limit and the threads strip, the bolt spins forever and grips nothing. Hold the button to tighten, and try to stop in the green band.

Predict first
Before you run it: you pick the #10-32 bolt and hold the tighten button without ever letting go. What happens to the joint?
Torque only rises while you hold, so it sweeps up through the green snug band and keeps going. Once it crosses the red strip line, the threads shear and the bolt spins free with zero clamping force. Run the sim and hold the button to watch the needle blow past green into red and the bolt strip, then use Reset and try to release inside the green band.

Keep it from rattling loose

FRC robots vibrate hard, and a plain nut will walk right off. Two fixes: a nylock nut has a nylon ring that grips the threads, and threadlocker (blue Loctite) is a liquid that sets and holds. Use one or the other on anything that matters. And thread engagement counts: a bolt needs at least a couple of full threads biting, too short and it pulls out under load.

Going further · optional

Here is the surprising part: when you torque a bolt, you are really stretching it like a stiff rubber band, and that stretch is what creates the clamping force holding parts together. That is also why a too-short bolt or too-few threads can pull out, there is not enough thread biting to hold the stretch. A challenge to chew on: aluminum threads strip far easier than steel, so when you must thread a steel bolt into an aluminum part, what could you add to spread that load and protect the soft threads? Look up "helicoil" or "threaded insert" with a mentor.

Show the thinking

You add a steel threaded insert, either a coiled-wire Helicoil or a solid threaded insert, set into the aluminum part. The bolt then threads into hard steel instead of soft aluminum, and the insert's larger outside thread spreads the clamping load over more aluminum so it is far harder to strip. This is also the standard rescue when an aluminum hole has already stripped: drill it oversize, tap it, and install an insert. The same idea is why FRC gearboxes and motors usually have steel inserts or steel parts where bolts thread in.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does "1/4-20" tell you about a bolt?
The first number is the diameter (1/4 inch), the second is the thread pitch (20 threads per inch). The hole or nut it goes into must match both exactly.
Question 2
You keep tightening a bolt well past snug. What happens?
Past the proper torque, the threads shear off. Now the bolt spins freely with no clamping force, and you have to drill it out or replace the part. Snug, then stop.
Question 3
A bolt on a vibrating robot keeps working loose. Best fix?
A nylock nut grips the threads with a nylon insert, and threadlocker bonds them chemically. Either keeps a fastener from rattling out under FRC's constant vibration.

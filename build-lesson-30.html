Wiring Best Practices

Wiring is a
craft.

A robot takes thousands of jolts in a match. The difference between one that runs all season and one that mysteriously dies mid-match is rarely the design, it's the wiring. A solid crimp and a little strain relief are what keep a connection alive when the robot gets slammed.

Tug on it. Does it hold?

The real test of a connection is the pull test: give the wire a firm tug. A proper crimp grips the copper tightly, and strain relief (securing the wire so a yank pulls on the anchor, not the joint) keeps the force off the connection. Set the connection quality below and pull.

Twisted and unsecured, the wire pulls right out. Crimped and zip-tied, the tug lands on the anchor and the joint holds. Inspectors and good drivers both do this test, because a connection that survives a tug survives a match.

The pull test is your best friend

After every connection, give it a tug. If it moves, fix it now, on the bench, with good light and the battery unplugged, not in the pits ten minutes before a match. This single habit prevents the most common robot failures: a power lead that backs out, a CAN connector that wiggles loose, a sensor that drops out over a bump. Reliable robots aren't the ones that never get hit, they're the ones whose wiring shrugs off the hit.

Going further · optional

A crimp beats a solder joint in a robot for a reason that feels backward: solder is rigid, and right where the stiff solder ends and the bare wire begins, every vibration bends the copper in the same spot until it cracks, often hidden under the insulation. A good crimp stays a little flexible and spreads that stress out. Next-level habit: leave a small service loop of slack at each connector so a wire can flex without tugging the joint, and route power and the lighter signal wires apart so a noisy motor lead does not disturb the data. Try this on the bench: crimp one connection, then pull-test it, then wiggle it for a minute and pull again, does it still hold?

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Why crimp a connection instead of just twisting the wires?
A proper crimp squeezes the copper into a solid, gas-tight joint that survives vibration and tugs. Twisted wire loosens and eventually drops the connection.
Question 2
What does strain relief do?
Strain relief secures the wire nearby so any yank pulls on the zip tie or clamp instead of stressing the actual joint, keeping the connection intact.
Question 3
What's the simplest way to check a connection?
The pull test: a firm tug on every connection. If it moves, fix it on the bench now. It's the single best habit for a reliable robot.

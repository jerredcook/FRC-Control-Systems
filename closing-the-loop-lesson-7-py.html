System Identification

Stop guessing.
Measure it.

kS, kG, kV, kA aren't magic numbers. You pull them straight out of your own mechanism with two simple tests.

Last lesson, you typed in kG and friends like they fell from the sky.

They didn't. Every feedforward gain is a real, measurable property of your mechanism - this motor, this gearbox, this much weight. The process of measuring them is called system identification (sysid for short, or "characterization"). It turns a couple of test runs into trustworthy numbers, so your feedforward describes the robot you actually built instead of one you hoped for.

Here's the whole trick, hiding in the feedforward equation from Lesson 6:

voltage = kS·sign(v) + kG + kV·v + kA·a four physics terms (sign(v) just flips kS with the direction of travel) - and each one leaves a fingerprint we can read

Watch what happens if you move the carriage at a constant speed. Constant speed means acceleration is zero, so the kA·a term vanishes entirely. What's left is a plain straight line:

voltage = (kS + kG) + kV·velocity a line: intercept = kS + kG  ·  slope = kV

So if we run the elevator at a bunch of steady speeds and record the voltage each one needs, the data falls on a line - and the line's slope and intercept hand us the gains. That's it. That's system identification.

Try it

Run the ramp test

Press Run ramp test. The motor voltage rises slowly - slow enough that the carriage is always cruising at its steady speed for that voltage (engineers call this a quasistatic test). Each instant we plot a dot: velocity across, voltage up. Watch the cloud of dots line up.

What the line tells you

Slope is kV, intercept is the rest

Once the line fits, read it off directly. The slope - how much extra voltage each extra unit of speed costs - is kV. The intercept, where the line meets zero velocity, is the voltage the mechanism needs just to creep: that's kS (static friction) plus kG (gravity), bundled together.

But "bundled together" is a problem - feedforward needs kS and kG separately. One ramp can't split them. The fix is beautifully simple: run the ramp downward too.

up  : intercept = kG + kS
down: intercept = kG − kS friction always fights motion, so it flips sign when the direction does

Two equations, two unknowns. Add them and the friction cancels; subtract them and gravity cancels:

kG = (up + down) / 2   ·   kS = (up − down) / 2
Why it works

Going up, friction and gravity both fight you, so it takes more voltage. Going down, gravity helps while friction still resists, so it takes less. The midpoint of the two intercepts is pure gravity; half the gap between them is pure friction.

The missing gain

And kA - the step test

Notice the ramp never told us kA. That's on purpose: we kept acceleration near zero so the kA·a term would disappear and leave a clean line. To measure kA we do the opposite - a dynamic step test: slam the voltage to a fixed value and watch how hard the carriage accelerates in the first instant, before speed has a chance to build.

At that first instant velocity is still ~0, so kV and the line's intercept are already accounted for, and every leftover volt is going into acceleration:

kA = (voltage − intercept) / acceleration extra voltage above the line, divided by the acceleration it produced

Two tests, four gains: the quasistatic ramp gives you kS, kG, and kV; the dynamic step gives you kA. Together they fully describe how your mechanism turns voltage into motion.

In practice

You don't do this by hand

You could run these tests with a stopwatch and graph paper - but you won't. WPILib's SysId tool and CTRE's Tuner X automate the whole thing: a routine drives the ramp and the step for you, logs voltage, position, and velocity at high speed, then fits the line and reports kS, kG, kV, and kA. In RobotPy that routine looks like this:

A SysId characterization routine Python
self.routine = SysIdRoutine(
    SysIdRoutine.Config(),                       # default ramp rate & step voltage
    SysIdRoutine.Mechanism(
        lambda volts: self.motor.setVoltage(volts),  # 1. drive the motor
        self.log_state,                            # 2. record what it did
        self))

# Bind these to buttons, run all four, then analyze the logs:
ramp_up   = self.routine.quasistatic(SysIdRoutine.Direction.kForward)
ramp_down = self.routine.quasistatic(SysIdRoutine.Direction.kReverse)
step_up   = self.routine.dynamic(SysIdRoutine.Direction.kForward)
step_down = self.routine.dynamic(SysIdRoutine.Direction.kReverse)

Run those four commands on the real robot, hand the logs to the analyzer, and it spits out the same slope and intercept you just fit by hand - only with hundreds of data points and no eyeballing.

Going further · optional

Fitting a line by eye works, but the analyzer does it with least squares: it picks the slope and intercept that make the total of all the squared up-and-down distances from the dots to the line as small as possible. Squaring is the clever part - it punishes one wildly-off point far more than several slightly-off ones, which is why a single bad data sample can drag your whole fit. Try this: why would the same tests run on a worn-out chain or a cold battery hand you slightly different gains each time, and what does that tell you about how often a competitive team should re-characterize a mechanism?

Show the thinking

Each run samples a slightly different machine: a worn chain adds friction and slack, a cold battery sags more under load, so the voltage-versus-speed dots land in new spots and least squares hands back slightly different kS, kV, and kA every time. That means the gains are a snapshot of the hardware on that day, not fixed truths. A competitive team should re-characterize after any big mechanical change, such as a new gearbox or a rebuilt mechanism, and is wise to leave a little tuning margin and recheck across a season as parts wear in and conditions shift.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
In the ramp test, you plot voltage against velocity and the data forms a line. The slope of that line is…
kV. Slope is "extra voltage per extra unit of speed" - exactly what kV means. The intercept holds kS + kG, and kA never appears because acceleration is held near zero.
Question 2
Why run the ramp test both up and down?
A single ramp only gives you kS + kG lumped together. Going the other direction flips friction's sign, so the up-intercept is kG + kS and the down-intercept is kG − kS. Average them for kG, halve their difference for kS.
Question 3
The slow ramp test never reveals kA. Why not - and what does?
The ramp is deliberately slow so acceleration ≈ 0 and the line stays clean - which means kA never shows up. A dynamic step test creates a big, brief acceleration so kA finally has something to act on, and you read it from how hard the mechanism accelerates.

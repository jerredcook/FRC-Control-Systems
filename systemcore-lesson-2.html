Meet SystemCore

A computer
in your hand.

SystemCore is not a simple switch box. It is a real, phone-sized Linux computer built for robots: a fast processor, memory, a graphics chip, plus a built-in camera brain, motion sensor, and WiFi, all on one small board from Limelight.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

A smartphone for the robot.

The clearest way to picture SystemCore is to think of a smartphone. A phone is a real computer, with a camera, WiFi, and sensors all packed into one small slab you can hold in your hand. SystemCore is the same idea, built for a robot. It is about the size of a large phone, and it carries the robot's brain, its camera smarts, and its WiFi all in one piece.

That word computer matters. The old roboRIO was more of a controller, good at running robot code but not much else. SystemCore is a full Linux computer, so it can do heavier jobs like watching a camera feed and steering the robot at the same time, all by itself.

Made by Limelight

SystemCore is built by Limelight, the group whose vision cameras are already on many FRC robots. That background shows: they put real camera smarts right inside the board, so a robot can see and aim without bolting on a second computer.

A tour of the parts

What is inside

SystemCore is built around a small computer board called a Raspberry Pi Compute Module 5, often shortened to CM5. That is the same kind of part you find in classroom kits and hobby projects, which is part of why it can stay affordable. Here is the lineup, in plain words.

Two chips, two jobs

It helps to see SystemCore as a team of two chips. The big one (the CM5) is the smart computer that thinks, runs Linux, and watches the camera. The small helper (the RP2350) is steady and quick, and it minds the robot wiring, the wires that read sensors and fire signals out to the hardware. The smart chip plans the move; the helper chip handles the wires. Splitting the work this way lets the computer take on heavy jobs without dropping the fast, on-time signals the robot depends on.

Why it matters

A full computer, not only a controller

This is the key point to carry forward. Because SystemCore is a complete computer with its own processor, memory, graphics, and Linux, it can run vision and other heavy work on its own. With the old setup, teams often bolted on a second computer to do vision. Now that lives on the same board, along with status LEDs and a small display that show you what the robot is doing at a glance.

Going further · optional

A graphics chip, a GPU, was built for drawing pictures, but it is also great at doing the same small math on thousands of pixels at once, which is exactly what reading a camera image needs. That is why SystemCore leans on its GPU for vision. Here is one to chew on: if the same chip can crunch many pixels in parallel, what other robot job that involves a lot of repeated math might you hand to it instead of the main CPU?

Show the thinking

The key idea is that GPUs shine when the same operation runs over and over on lots of separate pieces of data at once. So good candidates are jobs with that shape: machine-learning models that recognize objects, blending readings from several cameras at the same time, or sorting through many possible robot paths to pick a good one. Jobs that are mostly one decision at a time, like reading a single sensor or running one control loop, are usually better left on the CPU because the GPU's parallel strength does not help there.

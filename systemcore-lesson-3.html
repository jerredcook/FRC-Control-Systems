SystemCore vs roboRIO

Side by
side.

A lot is new, but a lot stays exactly the same. Lining the two up shows where SystemCore folds in extra parts, and equally important, why your code and your skills carry right over.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

Fewer boxes, more built in.

The old roboRIO did one main job well, but it leaned on other parts around it. It needed a separate radio for WiFi, usually a separate vision coprocessor (a second small computer dedicated to the camera), and a Driver Station program that ran on Windows only. SystemCore pulls those extras inside itself.

A companion called MotionCore

SystemCore does not do everything alone. It pairs with a companion board called MotionCore that handles power and CAN, the wiring that carries signals and current out to the motors. Picture SystemCore as the thinking brain and MotionCore as the part that delivers power and messages to the muscles. You will meet MotionCore properly in Part 2.

The reassuring part

What stays the same

Here is the news that matters most if you already build robots: a big change to the brain does not mean throwing out what you know. The parts you have learned and the gear you already own mostly carry straight over.

⚙️
Your motor controllersCAN controllers like Kraken and TalonFX and REV still work.
🧩
Command-based codeThe same way of structuring robot programs carries over.
📐
Control theoryPID and feedforward are math; they do not change.
⌨️
Your languagesJava, C++, and Python are all still supported.
🔧
Your mechanismsDrivetrains, arms, and intakes are unchanged.
🧠
Your skillsMost of what you have learned transfers directly.
A new engine, the same controls

Think about a new bike with an upgraded frame and a smoother gear system. The pedals, the brakes, and the way you steer all work the same; you go faster and shift more easily. SystemCore is like that. The brain underneath is newer and quicker, but the way you write code and the parts you bolt to the robot stay familiar. You spend your energy learning the few new things, not relearning everything.

Going further · optional

CAN is the shared wiring that lets the brain talk to every motor controller over one pair of wires, like a group chat where each device has its own name. CAN FD is a faster version of that chat, so more messages fit in the same time. With a quicker computer and quicker wiring, the robot can check its sensors and adjust more times each second. Here is one to think about: if your robot could correct its aim twice as often, where on your robot would that smoother, faster control help the most?

Show the thinking

Faster correction helps most where small errors grow quickly or where the target is moving. A spinning shooter wheel holding an exact speed, an arm balancing at a precise angle, or a drivetrain trying to track a moving goal all benefit because the robot catches and fixes drift before it becomes a miss. It matters far less for slow, forgiving jobs like a simple intake roller or a latch that only opens and closes, where checking twice as often does not change the result. The useful question for any mechanism is whether being a beat late actually costs you anything.

Question 1
Which of these is NEW with SystemCore compared to the roboRIO?
SystemCore folds the radio and vision inside the board itself. The roboRIO needed a separate radio and usually a separate vision computer.
Question 2
Which of these stays the SAME when you switch to SystemCore?
Your motor controllers like Kraken and REV, your command-based code, and your control theory all carry over. The separate radio and Windows-only Driver Station are the old roboRIO ways, not the new ones.
Question 3
What is MotionCore?
MotionCore is the companion board that pairs with SystemCore to handle power and CAN, carrying signals and current out to the motors.

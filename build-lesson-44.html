Sizing & Design Math

Pick the right
motor and ratio.

A mechanism that is too slow, too weak, or pops a breaker was sized wrong on a screen first. Sizing is the math that picks the motor count and gear ratio before you cut metal, and a free tool called ReCalc does the heavy arithmetic for you.

Every motor lives between two limits.

Think about pedaling a bike. In a tiny gear you spin the pedals fast but barely move, and in a huge gear you crawl up a hill with all your weight on each push. A motor is the same. With nothing attached it hits its free speed, spinning as fast as it ever will. Pushing against something so heavy it cannot turn, it gives its stall torque, its hardest possible push. It can never do both at once. All real work happens somewhere in between, and the harder it pushes, the more current it pulls from the battery.

Reduction is the gear you choose on the bike

Gear reduction trades speed for torque. A 10:1 reduction spins the output about 10 times slower but with roughly 10 times the twist, minus a little friction. That is the main knob you turn to match a motor to a job. Need to lift something heavy? Add reduction. Need it to move fast? Take reduction away. You met gears back in the gears lesson; sizing is deciding which one to actually use. Adding motors is the other knob: two motors share the load, so you get more torque and more power, but together they also pull more current.

So the design question is always two numbers. What speed does this mechanism need, and what force or torque does it need? Once you know those targets, you pick a motor count and a gear ratio that hit them without stalling, without tipping the robot over, and without drawing so much current that you trip the 120 A main breaker or brown out the battery. The tool

What ReCalc actually does

You do not memorize motor curves and you do not do this arithmetic by hand. ReCalc at reca.lc has every FRC motor built in, and it is free and open source, made by and for the FRC community. You tell it the kind of mechanism and your numbers, and it reports back the answers you care about.

🧱
Pick the mechanismDrivetrain, elevator, arm, flywheel, linear, and more, each with its own page.
⌨️
Enter your numbersWeight, travel distance, target speed, the motor, and the gear ratio.
📈
Read the resultsTop speed, time to move, current draw, whether the robot tips, a suggested ratio.
🔁
Adjust and repeatChange the ratio, re-read, keep tuning until speed and current both look right.
The loop is the real skill

Nobody nails the ratio on the first try. The skill is the loop: define your requirements, plug them into ReCalc, read the speed and the current, adjust the ratio, and read again. A good ratio is one where the mechanism moves fast enough, pushes hard enough, and keeps its current low enough that the battery and breaker stay happy. When all three land in a comfortable band, you have your number, and only then do you cut metal.

Worked example

Sizing an elevator, in words

Say an elevator has to lift a 12 lb carriage about 1 meter straight up, and you want it to make that trip in under a second using two motors. What gear reduction do you pick? Here is the reasoning you would carry into ReCalc.

🐇
Too little reductionOutput spins fast but cannot make the lift; the motors strain and stall near the weight.
🐢
Too much reductionPlenty of lifting force, but it crawls, misses the one-second target, and drags current up.
🎯
A ratio in betweenLifts the weight and clears the time, with current low enough to stay off the breaker.
🔍
Let ReCalc find itTry ratios on the elevator page until the time and the current both read good.

You would open the elevator page, type in the 12 lb load, the 1 meter of travel, two of your chosen motor, and a starting ratio. ReCalc shows the time to lift and the current draw. If it is too slow, lower the reduction. If the current is scary or it stalls, raise it or add reasoning about a different motor. After a few passes the time drops under a second and the current settles into a safe range, and that ratio is the one to build. We are not claiming a single magic number here, because the right ratio depends on the exact motor, and finding it is exactly the job ReCalc is built to do.

Going further · optional

ReCalc is not the only tool that does this job. The JVN design calculator is a big spreadsheet that veteran teams use the same way, and the ILITE drivetrain simulator goes further by modeling how the robot accelerates and what the battery voltage does under load across a whole match. Flywheels have their own twist: more reduction spins up slower but recovers its speed faster after a shot, so a shooter that fires rapidly cares about recovery time as much as top speed. Here is one to take to a mentor: if your shooter has to fire five game pieces in a row, would you trade a little top speed for faster recovery between shots, and how would you check that in ReCalc?

Show the thinking

For five quick shots in a row, yes, trade a little top speed for faster recovery. Each shot dumps energy into the game piece and drops the wheel's speed, so what limits your fire rate is how fast the flywheel climbs back to its target between shots, not its absolute top speed. More reduction gives the motor more torque to re-accelerate the wheel, so it recovers quicker even though its ceiling is a touch lower. In ReCalc you check this on the flywheel page: it reports spin-up and recovery time, so you raise the ratio and watch recovery time fall, stopping once the top speed is still high enough to make the shot but the recovery is fast enough to keep up with five rapid shots.

Quick check

Question 1
A 10:1 gear reduction does what to a motor's output?
Reduction trades speed for torque. A 10:1 turns the output about 10 times slower but with roughly 10 times the twist, minus a little friction loss. That is the main knob you turn to match a motor to a job.
Question 2
What is ReCalc for?
ReCalc has every FRC motor built in. You pick the mechanism type and enter your numbers, and it reports the top speed, the time to move, the current draw, whether the robot tips, and a recommended ratio so you can adjust and re-read.
Question 3
Why does the current draw matter when you choose a gear ratio?
Pushing harder and adding motors both raise current. If the draw climbs too high it can trip the main breaker or sag the battery into a brownout, so a good ratio hits your speed and force targets while keeping current in a safe range.

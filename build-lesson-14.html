Part 3 · 05 Power Transmission
Shafts, Hex & Hubs

Don't let it
slip.

A gear is useless if it spins freely on its shaft. Something has to lock the two together so torque passes through. A hex shaft does it with its shape; a round shaft needs a key or a clamp. Get it wrong and the shaft turns while the gear stays still.

The shape can be the lock.

Push torque through three ways of mounting a gear on a shaft. A hex shaft in a hex bore can't rotate independently, the shape itself transmits torque. A round shaft held by a set screw slips once the torque gets high. A round shaft with a key holds. Turn up the torque.

Predict first
Before you run it: you switch to the round shaft held by a set screw and crank the torque up high. What do the two marks do?
A set screw only presses on a round shaft, so once the torque gets high the shaft twists past the screw and the teal shaft mark pulls ahead of the amber gear mark, that gap is the gear slipping. A hex shaft or a key carries the torque through its shape and stays locked. Run the sim, pick the set-screw mount, crank the torque, and watch the marks split apart, then switch to hex or key and see them hold together.

○Find the slip point. Ease back down to the lowest torque that still slips and hold it for 1 second. The amber tick on the torque dial is your answer key, land within 3% above it.

★Gold: just past the slip point the gear still creeps forward. Find a torque of 60% or less where the gear mark stops dead while the shaft keeps spinning.
In the shop

Why FRC runs on hex

The standard FRC shaft is 1/2-inch hex, and almost every gear, sprocket, pulley, and wheel comes with a matching hex bore. The reason is simplicity:

⬡
Hex shaftThe hexagon shape transmits torque on its own, slide a part on and it can't slip.
🔩
Set screwA screw pressing on a round shaft. Easy, but it backs out and slips under shock.
🗝️
Key & keywayA bar in matching slots that carries the torque. Strong, but more machining.
🔗
HubAn adapter that bolts a wheel or sprocket onto a shaft.
Hex won the popularity contest for a reason

You can build a perfectly good drive with round shaft and keys, plenty of teams do. But hex is forgiving: there's nothing to torque to spec, nothing to align, nothing to back out. Cut the shaft to length, slide parts on, add a retaining clip, done. For a robot built by students under a deadline, "can't be assembled wrong" is worth a lot. That's why hex is the default and set screws are treated with suspicion.

Going further · optional

Hex is forgiving, but it is not free of slop: a part has to be a hair larger than the shaft to slide on, and that tiny gap lets it rock back and forth a little, called backlash. For most jobs nobody cares, but for something that has to point precisely, like a turret or a camera mount, that wiggle adds up. Worth pondering: when would you accept a slower, fussier round shaft with a key to kill the last bit of play?

Show the thinking

Reach for a keyed round shaft when a small angular error at the part turns into a large error at the output, and when the mechanism holds a position rather than spinning freely. A turret or a camera mount is the classic case: a few thousandths of hex rock near the pivot can swing the far end of a long arm or a tight aiming solution by a visible amount. A free-spinning roller or a wheel does not care about that wiggle, so hex stays the better choice there because it is faster to build and swap. The trade you accept with a key is more machining and a part that is harder to slide on and off, bought in exchange for near-zero backlash.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
How does a hex shaft transmit torque to a gear?
The flats of the hex push directly on the matching flats of the bore. The shape itself carries the torque, so a hex-bored part can't rotate independently on a hex shaft.
Question 2
What's the weakness of a set screw on a round shaft?
A set screw grips with a tiny contact point. Under high torque or vibration it can loosen and let the shaft spin inside the part. That's why teams prefer hex or a key for anything loaded.
Question 3
Why is hex the default shaft for student-built robots?
Hex needs no keys, no set-screw torque spec, and nothing to align. Parts slide on and can't slip. Under a build-season deadline, that reliability is exactly why hex dominates.

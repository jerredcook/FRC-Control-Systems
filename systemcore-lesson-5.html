Vision, IMU & Radio, Built In

Batteries
included.

Today a team bolts on extra boxes for vision, a heading sensor, and a wireless link. SystemCore folds all three into the board. Plug in a camera and it sees, it already knows which way it is pointed, and its WiFi is built in. Fewer boxes, less wiring, lower cost.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

Three add-ons, now part of the board.

Imagine buying a bike where the lights, the bell, and the speedometer all come attached instead of being parts you buy and mount one by one. That is the idea here. Vision, a motion sensor, and a radio used to be separate purchases and separate wiring jobs. On SystemCore they ship inside the board.

Vision for everyone

Plug in a camera, and it sees

Vision is how a robot recognizes things on the field, like reading an AprilTag (a printed square marker) to figure out where it is. Today that usually needs a separate small computer called a coprocessor, which costs money and takes setup. SystemCore builds the vision processing in: plug a USB camera into the board, and the board does the work. That means every team gets vision, not only the teams who can afford and configure an extra computer.

Why "built in" matters

Each separate box you remove is one less thing to buy, mount, wire, and debug at 2 a.m. before a competition. Folding vision, the IMU, and the radio into one board means less wiring, lower cost, and vision for every team. It also shrinks the pile of parts a rookie team has to understand to get a robot moving.

The IMU is the board's sense of direction. "IMU" stands for inertial measurement unit, which is a gyro plus an accelerometer. In plain words, it knows which way the robot is facing and how it is moving, the way you can close your eyes and still feel yourself turning on a bike. That helps with heading, odometry (tracking how far you have driven), and localization (figuring out where you are). You can still add CAN sensors if you want more, but many robots will not need to.

No more radio box

The wireless link comes inside

Right now the robot's link to the Driver Station laptop runs through a separate radio box that has to be powered and wired into the robot. SystemCore replaces it with an integrated dual-band 2.4/5 GHz WiFi radio. The separate radio box and its wiring are gone, and the robot's link to the Driver Station is built right into the board.

Going further · optional

There is an optional extra for teams who want even more vision power: an M.2 A+E slot on the board that accepts a Hailo-8 AI accelerator, a small chip built for machine-learning vision. Most teams will not need it, but it is there for advanced projects like training a robot to recognize game pieces by sight. Here is one to think about with a mentor: if vision already runs on the main board, what kinds of jobs would be worth adding a dedicated AI chip for, and which jobs are fine without it?

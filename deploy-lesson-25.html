Part 6 · 03 Driving & Autonomous
Odometry

Where am I on
the field?

Add up every wheel turn and every degree of heading, loop after loop, and you can track the robot's position on the field without a GPS. That running estimate is odometry, and it's how autonomous knows where it is.

Start at a known spot, then keep score.

Give the robot a starting pose (x, y, and heading). Each loop, read how far the wheels rolled and which way the gyro points, and add that motion to the estimate. Do it 50 times a second and you always have a best guess of where the robot is. But it's only a guess: tiny errors accumulate. Drive around and watch the estimate slowly drift off the truth.

Predict first
Before you drive it: the gyro here is perfect and the wheels never slip. After you loop around the field for a while, what does the dashed odometry estimate do relative to the solid true robot?
Odometry only adds up motion, so even small rounding and timing errors accumulate loop after loop and the estimate gradually peels away from the truth. There is no absolute reference pulling it back. The dashed ghost is what the robot thinks its pose is. After enough driving it lags the real robot, that's accumulated drift. "Reset pose" snaps the estimate back onto the truth, exactly what a vision fix or a known start does.

In code

Update it every loop

Construct odometry with a start pose, feed it the gyro and wheel distances each periodic(), and read the pose back whenever you need it:

DriveSubsystem Java · WPILib
DifferentialDriveOdometry odometry = new DifferentialDriveOdometry(
    gyro.getRotation2d(), leftMeters, rightMeters,
    new Pose2d(2, 2, Rotation2d.kZero));   // known start

@Override public void periodic() {
  odometry.update(gyro.getRotation2d(),
      leftEncoder.getDistance(), rightEncoder.getDistance());
}

Pose2d pose = odometry.getPoseMeters();   // x, y, heading on the field

// snap to a known pose (match start, or a vision correction)
odometry.resetPosition(gyro.getRotation2d(), leftM, rightM, knownPose);
Drift is why vision exists

Odometry is fast and smooth but it only integrates, so wheel slip and a slightly-off gyro pile up into error over a match. Swerve uses SwerveDrivePoseEstimator, which blends odometry with camera measurements of AprilTags to erase that drift, the subject of Part 7. For now, the takeaway: encoders plus a gyro give you a live pose, and you re-anchor it whenever you get a better fix.

Going further · optional

Notice the demo's estimate drifts even though the gyro is "perfect" here; in real life wheel slip during a hard collision is a big culprit, since a spinning-but-not-gripping wheel reports distance the robot never traveled. That is why pose estimators trust the gyro for heading far more than they trust the wheels, and why a SwerveDrivePoseEstimator lets you weight each measurement by how much you believe it. Here is a question to sit with: if a defender slams your robot and a wheel skids, would your X-Y position or your heading drift more, and why?

Show the thinking

Your X-Y position drifts more, because position comes from the wheel distances, and a skidding wheel reports travel that never happened. Heading holds up far better because it comes from the gyro, which measures rotation directly and does not care whether the wheels are gripping. That is exactly why a pose estimator trusts the gyro for heading and treats wheel-based position as the weaker measurement, and why a vision fix off an AprilTag is so useful for pulling the X-Y estimate back after a hit.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What is odometry?
Odometry tracks where the robot is on the field by integrating how far the wheels rolled and how the heading changed, updated every loop from a known start.
Question 2
Why does an odometry estimate drift over a match?
Each tiny measurement error gets added to the running total and never removed, so the estimate slowly diverges. Blending in vision corrections is how teams fight the drift.
Question 3
What does odometry.getPoseMeters() give you?
A Pose2d bundles position (x, y) and orientation (heading). That's everything a path follower or auto routine needs to know where the robot is.

Measure & Mark

Measure twice,
cut once.

A robot is hundreds of parts that all have to line up. That precision starts at the measurement. A caliper reads to the thousandth of an inch, and knowing how to use one separates parts that bolt together from parts that don't.

A caliper measures far finer than a ruler.

A ruler gets you to about a sixteenth of an inch. A caliper reads to the thousandth, 0.001 inch, which is the precision a robot's holes and shafts need. Slide the jaws below to a target measurement and land it within tolerance.

Predict first
Before you run it: the target is 1.250 in with a tolerance of ±0.005. You slide the jaws to read 1.244 in and press Check. Does it pass?
The band is 1.245 to 1.255 in, the target plus or minus 0.005. A reading of 1.244 is 0.006 below the target, which is outside that window by a single thousandth, so Check marks it off. Run the sim, set the jaws to 1.244, press Check to see it fail, then nudge up to 1.245 and watch it pass.

In the shop

Tolerance, and marking a part

Why tolerance is the whole game

Two parts have to fit, so their tolerances have to agree. A 0.500 inch shaft in a 0.500 inch hole won't spin, it's a press fit. The hole needs to be a few thousandths bigger. Get tolerance right and parts slide together; get it wrong and you're filing holes at midnight. When in doubt, hold the tight tolerance only where parts actually meet, and leave the rest loose to save time.

Going further · optional

Real designs care about which way the tolerances stack. If you bolt five spacers in a row and each one can be 0.005 inch over or under, the whole stack can drift by much more than one part's worth, and suddenly nothing lines up. Try this design challenge: think of a way to lay out a row of holes so the error does not pile up the same direction every time, for example measuring every hole from one shared edge instead of measuring each from the last hole you drilled.

Show the thinking

The hint in the question is the real fix: measure every hole from one shared edge or datum, not from the hole before it. When you chain measurements hole to hole, each small error carries forward and adds onto the next, so the last hole can be way off. Measuring each hole from the same starting edge keeps every error small and independent instead of letting them stack the same direction. CAD and a drill press with a fence do this naturally, since each position is set from a fixed zero.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Why use a caliper instead of a ruler for robot parts?
A ruler stops at about a sixteenth of an inch. A caliper reads to 0.001 inch, which is the precision holes and shafts need to line up and fit together.
Question 2
A part is spec'd at 1.250 ± 0.005 in. What does the ± 0.005 mean?
Tolerance is the allowed range. ± 0.005 means 0.005 over or under is fine, so 1.245 to 1.255 in all pass. Nothing is ever perfectly on size, so you specify how close is close enough.
Question 3
Why center-punch a hole location before drilling?
A center punch makes a small dimple that catches the drill tip, so the bit bites exactly on your mark instead of skating across the surface. Mark, punch, then drill.

Shop Safety & Mindset

Keep all ten
fingers.

Before you touch a single tool, you learn to work safely. It isn't the exciting part, but it's the one that lets you come back tomorrow. Good builders make safety a habit, not an afterthought.

Safety is a routine you run every time.

Walk up to a machine and the same checks happen first, every single time, until they're automatic. Glasses on. Nothing dangling. You know where the power kill is. Run the checklist below and get yourself cleared to work.

In the shop

The rules that matter most

A few rules prevent almost every shop injury. Learn these cold, and the rest is mostly common sense:

🥽
Eye protection, alwaysSafety glasses on the moment you enter, not just when you cut. Chips fly far.
🧤
No gloves on spinning toolsDrills, lathes, mills, sanders. A glove that catches pulls your hand in.
💇
Tie back hair & sleevesAnything dangling can wrap around a rotating shaft in an instant.
🛑
Know the e-stopFind the power kill before you start. In trouble, hit it first, ask later.
🧹
Clean as you goA cluttered bench hides sharp edges and trips you near a running tool.
🙋
When unsure, stop and askNo one is ever annoyed by a safety question. They are by an ER trip.
👥
Never work aloneSomeone else in the shop, and a mentor who has cleared you, before you run any power tool.
🎧
Protect ears & lungsEar protection near loud saws and sanders; a dust mask when grinding or sanding.
The one that surprises people: gloves

Gloves feel safer, so this rule is counterintuitive. But on anything that spins, a glove is a liability: the fabric catches the tool and drags your hand straight into it before you can react. Bare hands let go. Save the gloves for handling sharp sheet metal or hot parts by hand, never for operating a rotating machine.

The mindset: slow is smooth, smooth is fast

The fastest builders aren't the ones rushing. They're calm, set up carefully, and never put a hand where a tool could be. A robot part you ruin can be remade in an hour. You can't. Respect the machines, work deliberately, and the speed comes on its own.

Going further · optional

Top teams turn safety into a system, not a vibe. Ask your mentor whether the shop runs a lockout-tagout routine (a physical lock that keeps a machine off while someone works on it) and who is trained to clear new members on each tool. Here is one to chew on: if hitting the e-stop is the right move in an emergency, why is it a poor habit to use the e-stop as your everyday off switch?

Show the thinking

An e-stop cuts power abruptly while everything is still spinning, which is hard on belts, motors, and the machine's controls, and it can leave a tool in an odd half-stopped state. Using it daily also wears out the one switch you are betting your safety on, so it might stick or fail the day you really need it. The normal off switch lets a machine spool down the way it was built to, and keeping the e-stop reserved for emergencies means your hand knows exactly what that big red button means under stress. Treat it like the fire alarm: there for the bad moment, not the everyday routine.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
When do you put your safety glasses on?
Chips and debris travel across a room, and someone else's cut can reach your eyes. Glasses on entry, off on exit, is the simplest rule that works.
Question 2
You're about to use the drill press. Should you wear gloves?
On any rotating tool, a glove is more dangerous than bare hands. If it catches, it drags your hand into the machine. Bare hands pull away cleanly.
Question 3
You're not sure how to set up a cut safely. What's the right move?
Stopping to ask is always the right call. The best builders ask constantly. Removing a guard or guessing is how people get hurt.

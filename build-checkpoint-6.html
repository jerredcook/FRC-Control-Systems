Question 1
A single motor's wire shorts out during a match. In a properly built robot, what should happen?
Power runs battery to main breaker to the Power Distribution Hub to fused branches. Each branch is fused, so a short pops only that fuse and cuts that one circuit. Layered protection is the whole point of the power chain. Review: Lesson 6.1 · The Power System
Question 2
You wire a Kraken drive motor with thin 18 AWG wire on a 60 amp breaker. The wire gets hot and the insulation starts to soften. What went wrong?
A bigger motor pulls more amps, so it needs thicker wire (a lower AWG number), around 12 AWG for most FRC motors. The breaker has to sit at or below what the wire can safely carry, or it will let the wire overheat before tripping. Review: Lesson 6.2 · Wiring Motors & Controllers
Question 3
Mid-match, half your motor controllers suddenly stop responding while the rest still work. The CAN bus is a daisy chain. What is the classic cause?
CAN is one two-wire daisy chain, so a single bad crimp or unplugged connector silences every device after it. That is why a pull test, secure crimps, and strain relief matter so much; the data is only as reliable as the weakest connection. Review: Lesson 6.3 · The Control System
Question 4
You need a clamp that snaps fully open or fully closed and holds tight while drawing no power. Should you use a motor or pneumatics, and why?
Pneumatics shines at binary jobs: all the way out or all the way in. A cylinder holds its position drawing no current, where a motor would keep pulling amps. Air is wrong only when you need to stop partway or move constantly. Keep the working pressure within the 60 psi limit. Review: Lesson 6.5 · Pneumatics
Question 5
Two batteries both read 12.8 volts at rest, but the Battery Beak shows one at 12 milliohms and the other at 28 milliohms. You are about to drive a hard pushing match. Which do you pick, and why?
Resting voltage only shows the tank is full; internal resistance is how wide the pipe is. Terminal voltage equals resting voltage minus current times internal resistance, so the higher-resistance pack sags much harder under a big drivetrain pull and is the one that browns out when the match is on the line. Review: Lesson 6.6 · Battery Science & Health

Power Budget & Brownout

Don't run out of
volts.

Every motor pulls amps from the same battery at the same time. Add it up wrong and the robot browns out, going dead for a second at the worst possible moment.

One battery, many hungry motors.

Picture the water pressure in a house. Turn on one faucet and the water still flows strong. But turn on every faucet, the shower, and the garden hose all at once and the pressure at each tap sags. Your robot's battery works the same way. Every motor draws its current from that one battery through the main breaker, and when too many of them pull hard at the same time, the voltage everywhere drops. Push it too far and the robot protects itself by shutting the motors off for a moment. That moment is called a brownout, and it loves to strike right when you are shoving an opponent off a scoring spot.

All your current flows through one 120 amp breaker

Every amp the robot uses passes through the main breaker, which is rated for 120 amps. It is thermal and time-dependent, meaning it reacts to heat over time, not to an instant number. A brief surge well over 120 amps is tolerated, but the more you exceed it the faster it trips, and a dead short trips it almost instantly. That time delay is on purpose. It lets a motor pull a hard, short burst without nuisance-tripping the whole robot. Treat the 120 amp number as a sustained limit, not a hard ceiling for one quick instant.

What you are seeing

Why the voltage sags

A single big brushless motor like a Falcon 500, a Kraken, or a NEO sips only an amp or two while spinning free with nothing in its way. But when it is fully stalled, blocked so it cannot turn, it can pull a couple hundred amps trying to muscle past the obstacle. Four to six drive motors all stalling together, like when you ram straight into a wall, is well over a thousand amps for an instant. That is a huge spike, and the battery feels every bit of it.

You never actually pull a thousand amps for long. Two things stop it. First, the battery's voltage sags as the current climbs, remember from the last lesson that terminal voltage equals resting voltage minus current times internal resistance, so a giant current pulls the voltage way down. Second, software current limits cap how much each motor is allowed to pull. Still, a hard push into a wall, or several mechanisms surging at the same time, sags the battery sharply, and that sag is what trips a brownout.

Brownout: the robot's safety valve

When battery voltage sags too low, the robot controller protects itself by cutting motor outputs. On the roboRIO this happens around 6.8 volts. When it triggers, the motors briefly stop, the robot "browns out," and then it recovers once the voltage climbs back. Think of it like the safety valve in the house water system that shuts things off before the pressure drops dangerously low. The exact brownout voltage is set by the control board and is changing for the 2027 control system, see the SystemCore preview, but the idea is what matters and that idea does not change.

In the shop

How to never brown out

A power budget means adding up the current your mechanisms can pull at the same time and making sure the battery can live with it. Drivetrain motors dominate the budget, so they get the most attention. Here is how teams keep the robot alive:

Current limits are a team sport across courses

Software current limits are the single biggest fix, and they come in two flavors you will meet in the Code and Control courses: stator limits cap the current right at the motor's windings, and supply limits cap what the motor draws from the battery. Setting them sensibly means each motor promises never to pull more than its share, so even with everything moving at once the total stays under what the battery and the 120 amp breaker can handle. You trade away a tiny bit of brute push for a robot that never goes dark.

Going further · optional

A software current limit trades a little top-end push for never browning out, and the trade is usually worth it. Here is why: a brownout can cascade. The voltage drops, motors cut, the load disappears for a blink so the voltage springs back, the motors fire again, the load slams back on, and the voltage sags right back down. The robot can stutter through several of these cycles in a single second, which feels like the robot is fighting you. A current limit smooths that out by never letting the draw spike high enough to start the cycle in the first place. Here is one to chew on with a mentor: if you lower a motor's current limit, you lose a little pushing force but gain consistency. How would you pick the limit for a drivetrain that has to push a heavy game piece without ever browning out?

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What actually causes a brownout?
When many motors pull hard at once, the battery voltage sags. If it drops too low, around 6.8 volts on the roboRIO, the controller protects itself by cutting motor outputs. That cut is the brownout.
Question 2
Why does a motor pulling a hard, short burst usually not trip the 120 amp main breaker?
The main breaker reacts to heat over time, not to an instant number. A short surge over 120 amps is tolerated, but the more and longer you exceed it the faster it trips. A dead short trips it almost instantly.
Question 3
Your robot keeps browning out when you push into the wall. What is a real fix?
Software current limits keep the motors from all pulling stall current at once, and a fresh battery with low internal resistance sags less under the same load. Both keep the voltage above the brownout line. Removing the breaker is dangerous and does not fix the sag.

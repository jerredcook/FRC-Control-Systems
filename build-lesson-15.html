Gearboxes

Stack the
stages.

A single gear pair gives you one ratio in a fixed space. A gearbox packs several reduction stages into one small unit, and the ratios multiply. That's how you get a 100:1 reduction in a package the size of a soda can.

Each stage multiplies the last.

A planetary gearbox bolts straight onto a motor and stacks stages, each its own little reduction. The total ratio is every stage multiplied together. Add and configure stages below, and watch the output slow down and the torque climb with each one.

A 6000 rpm motor goes in; each stage divides the speed and multiplies the torque. The speed bars shrink stage by stage. Three 5:1 stages stacked make 125:1, a huge reduction in a tiny in-line package.

In the shop

Why planetary gearboxes win

FRC teams reach for planetary gearboxes like the MAXPlanetary and VersaPlanetary because they pack a lot of reduction into almost no space:

🪐
PlanetaryGears orbit a central sun gear; compact, strong, and in-line with the motor.
🧱
Stackable stagesSnap on cartridges (3:1, 4:1, 5:1) to build up almost any ratio.
✖️
Ratios multiplyTotal = stage1 × stage2 × stage3. 4 × 4 × 3 = 48:1.
🔌
Motor mounts onThe motor bolts to one end; your shaft comes out the other.
Pick the ratio, then the stages

You usually start from the ratio a mechanism needs, then choose stages that multiply to it. Want 25:1? That's a 5:1 and a 5:1. Want 60:1? Try 5 × 4 × 3. There's often more than one way to hit a number, and you pick based on what cartridges you have and how much torque each stage can take, earlier stages spin faster and handle less torque, later stages are the opposite. A gearbox is just the gear-ratio idea from Lesson 3.1, packaged and stacked.

Going further · optional

A target ratio rarely comes out as a clean stack of stages. Say a mechanism wants 17:1, but your cartridges only come in 3:1, 4:1, and 5:1, no combination multiplies to exactly 17. Real teams get the gearbox close, then make up the rest with a sprocket or pulley reduction on the way out (a 16T driving a 36T adds about 2.25:1). Try working backward from a few odd target numbers and see how a gearbox plus one belt stage can hit almost anything.

Show the thinking

Take the 17:1 target. A 4:1 cartridge gets you to 4, and you need the belt or chain stage to supply the remaining 17 divided by 4, about 4.25:1. A 12T driving a 50T pulley is 50/12, almost exactly 4.17, landing you at 16.7:1, close enough for most jobs; a 12T to 54T gets 4.5 and lands at 18:1 if you want to err high. The method is always the same: pick the cartridge stack that gets a little under the target, divide the target by what you have, then chase that leftover number with a sprocket pair, since you can fine-tune the final ratio one tooth at a time in a way fixed cartridges cannot.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
A gearbox has a 4:1 stage and a 3:1 stage. What's the total ratio?
Stages multiply: 4 × 3 = 12, a 12:1 reduction. Each stage's output feeds the next, so the reductions compound rather than add.
Question 2
What's the main advantage of a planetary gearbox?
Planetary gearboxes stack big reductions into a compact unit that mounts straight on the motor. That's why they're the default for FRC mechanisms.
Question 3
You need a 25:1 reduction from stackable 3:1, 4:1, and 5:1 stages. What works?
5 × 5 = 25. You build a target ratio by choosing stages whose product hits it. Often several combinations work, and you pick based on torque and the cartridges on hand.

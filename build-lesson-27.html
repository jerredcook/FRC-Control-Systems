The Power System

From battery
to everything.

Every motor, sensor, and computer on the robot draws from one 12-volt battery. Power flows through a strict chain, protected at every level, so that when something shorts out, it blows a fuse instead of the whole robot.

Battery, breaker, distribution, loads.

The power chain is always the same: the battery feeds the main breaker (one big 120-amp switch for the whole robot), which feeds the Power Distribution Hub, which fans out to every motor controller and the electronics on its own fused branch. Energize it, then trip a fault and watch the protection work.

Overload one branch and just that branch's fuse pops, the rest of the robot keeps running. Short the main and the whole robot shuts down to protect itself. That layered protection is the entire point of the power chain.

Protection is layered for a reason

A wire that shorts can draw hundreds of amps and start a fire or melt insulation in seconds. The fuse on its branch blows first, cutting just that circuit while everything else keeps running. The main breaker is the last line of defense for a really big fault, or your emergency off switch. Wiring without proper fusing isn't just against the rules, it's genuinely dangerous, which is why inspectors check every branch.

The battery itself is the biggest hazard

That little pack looks harmless, but it can pour hundreds of amps into anything that bridges its two terminals. A dropped wrench across them can heat red-hot and burn you in a second, no breaker in between. Keep metal tools away from the terminals, cover them when the battery isn't connected, and lift the heavy pack with two hands. Connect it last, with the main breaker off.

Going further · optional

The PDH does more than split power, it can report how many amps each branch is pulling back to your code over the data bus. That turns the power system into a sensor: software can watch a motor's current and notice it is climbing, which usually means the mechanism is jammed or stalling, and back off before anything overheats. This is real motor protection that goes beyond the breaker, and it is safer because it acts in software well before a fuse ever needs to blow. What might a sudden current spike on the intake branch be telling you?

Show the thinking

A sudden current spike means the intake motor is being forced to work much harder than normal, which usually means it is jammed or stalled. The most common cause is a game piece stuck or wedged so the rollers cannot turn, and a fully blocked motor pulls many times its running draw, which is exactly what the spike shows. Your code can watch for that and react, for example stop or reverse the intake to clear the jam, before the motor heats up or the breaker trips. A spike could also come from a mechanical bind or a piece of debris, so it is a cue to look, not a guaranteed diagnosis.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What's the correct order of the power chain?
Power flows battery → main breaker → Power Distribution Hub → each fused branch → its load. Every level adds protection between the battery and the device.
Question 2
A single motor's wire shorts out. What should happen?
Each branch is fused, so a short pops just that fuse and cuts only that circuit. The rest of the robot is unaffected, that's why distribution is fused per-branch.
Question 3
What is the main breaker for?
The 120-amp main breaker sits right after the battery as the robot's master switch and the last line of protection for a major overload.

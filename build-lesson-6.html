Aluminum Extrusion

The robot's
skeleton.

Almost every FRC robot is built from aluminum box tube, light, strong, and easy to cut and drill. Pick the right profile and you get a stiff, lightweight frame. Pick wrong and it's heavy or it flexes.

Box tube, in a few standard sizes.

The structural backbone of a robot is hollow aluminum tube, usually with a regular hole pattern down each face so parts can bolt on anywhere. Different profiles trade weight against stiffness. Compare them below, and see how a profile's height drives its stiffness.

In the shop

Why aluminum, and the hole pattern

Aluminum hits the sweet spot: about a third the weight of steel, plenty strong for a robot, and soft enough to cut and drill with shop tools. The standard hole pattern is the quiet superpower:

🪶
Light and strong6061 aluminum is roughly 1/3 the weight of steel at the strength a robot needs.
🕳️
Pre-drilled patternA regular grid of holes means parts bolt on anywhere, no new drilling.
📏
Taller = stifferOrient a profile so its tall side resists the bending load.
🔲
Wall thickness1/16" wall is the common default; thicker only where loads are high.
Orientation is half the battle

A 1x2 tube is much stiffer in one direction than the other. Lay it flat and it sags; stand it tall and it barely flexes, same tube, same weight. When you design a frame, point each tube's tall dimension into the load it carries. Getting orientation right often matters more than picking a heavier profile.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Why is aluminum the default material for FRC structure?
Aluminum is about a third the weight of steel while still being strong enough for a robot, and it's soft enough to machine with shop tools. That balance is why nearly every frame is aluminum.
Question 2
What's the advantage of the standard hole pattern on the tube?
The pre-drilled grid lets you attach brackets, gussets, and mechanisms at regular spots without measuring and drilling each time. It speeds up the build enormously.
Question 3
A 1x2 tube will carry a bending load. How should you orient it?
Bending stiffness rises with the cube of the height in the load direction. Standing the 2-inch dimension up makes the same tube far stiffer than laying it flat, for no extra weight.

The roboRIO retires

One board to
replace them all.

For the 2027 season, the roboRIO and the wireless radio both step aside. In their place comes SystemCore: one smaller, more powerful board, meant to cost less than the roboRIO, that folds the brain, the radio, and the camera smarts into a single slab.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

A new brain for the robot.

Every FRC robot needs a main controller, the part that runs your code and tells the motors what to do. For years that part has been the roboRIO. Starting in January 2027, a new board called SystemCore takes its place. It is built by Limelight, the same group behind the vision cameras many teams already use, and it is aimed to cost less than a roboRIO did.

This is not a small tweak. People who have followed FRC for a long time are calling it the biggest control-system change since the cRIO, the controller that came before the roboRIO. So it helps to see where SystemCore fits in the family line.

The family line

The robot brain has changed twice before. First came the cRIO. Then the roboRIO arrived in 2015 and ran the show for over a decade. Now SystemCore arrives for the 2027 season. Each step traded an older, bulkier setup for something smaller and more capable, and SystemCore is the biggest jump yet.

The big changeover

Out with two boxes, in with one

Here is the part that surprises people. In 2027 you will not wire a roboRIO and a SystemCore together on the same robot, you use one or the other. SystemCore is the new standard, and every 2027 FRC team gets one in the Kit of Parts, so no team is left behind. The roboRIO and the separate wireless radio give way to it. Exactly how long the old roboRIO stays legal during the changeover is still being worked out, so always check the official rules.

Why fold everything into one board

Think of an old computer lab: a pile of bulky desktop towers, a separate router on the wall, and webcams clipped to each screen. A school that swaps all of that for one modern all-in-one machine ends up with less clutter, fewer cables, and more speed for less money. SystemCore does the same trick for the robot. It rolls the controller, the radio, and the vision smarts into one slab that runs a modern version of Linux, so there are fewer separate boxes to wire, power, and worry about.

Going further · optional

The robot brain has changed roughly once a decade: cRIO, then roboRIO in 2015, now SystemCore in 2027. Each jump waited until cheaper, faster computer parts were common enough to build around. Here is one to think about: SystemCore is built around the same kind of small computer board found in hobbyist projects and classrooms. If those parts keep getting cheaper and faster every year, what would you want the robot brain after SystemCore to finally be able to do on its own?

Show the thinking

There is no single right answer here, the point is to think about the trend. A good answer follows the pattern: each new brain took over a job that used to need a separate box. So you might want the next one to handle all vision on its own, learn to spot game pieces on the fly, plan its own path across the field, or recover from a fault without a person resetting it. The honest catch is that more power on the robot is only useful if the rules allow it and your code can keep up, so the limit is often what the game asks for, not the chip.

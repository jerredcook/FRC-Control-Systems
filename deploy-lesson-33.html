Simulation

Test without
a robot.

The robot is shared, half-built, or on the practice field. Your code doesn't have to wait. WPILib runs the exact same program against a physics model of your mechanism, so you can test logic and tune gains on your laptop.

Same code. The sensor just comes from a model.

In simulation, your subsystem runs untouched, but instead of a real motor and encoder, a physics model takes the voltage you command and computes how the mechanism would move. That motion becomes the sensor value your code reads back. The loop closes exactly as on the robot. Command this simulated flywheel and watch it spin up.

There is no motor here. The spinning rotor and the measured RPM both come from the sim's physics, and your PID closes the loop against them just like it would on the real flywheel. Inertia and spin-up time are modeled, so the feel is real.

In code

simulationPeriodic feeds the model

Your subsystem code is unchanged. You add a simulationPeriodic() that pushes your motor's voltage into the model, advances the physics, and writes the result back as the sensor reading:

Shooter (sim hooks) Java · WPILib
private final FlywheelSim sim = new FlywheelSim(
    LinearSystemId.createFlywheelSystem(DCMotor.getKrakenX60(1), 0.004, 1.0),
    DCMotor.getKrakenX60(1));

@Override public void simulationPeriodic() {
  sim.setInputVoltage(motor.getMotorVoltage().getValueAsDouble());
  sim.update(0.020);                       // advance physics 20 ms
  motorSim.setRotorVelocity(                  // write the result back as the sensor
      sim.getAngularVelocityRPM() / 60.0);
}
Bugs are cheaper to find in sim

A wrong sign, a unit slip, an auto that drives into a wall, you can hit all of them on your laptop, where a crash costs nothing and you can step through it. WPILib ships models for elevators, arms, drivetrains, and flywheels. Teams that simulate write most of their logic before the robot is even wired, then arrive at the field already tuned.

Going further · optional

A model is a guess about reality, so it is never perfect. The numbers you pass FlywheelSim, the rotor inertia and the gearing, are estimates, and if they are off your sim will spin up faster or slower than the real flywheel. Try tuning your gains in sim, then comparing the real spin-up curve from your logs and adjusting the model until they match. Question to chew on: if your auto works flawlessly in sim but drifts on the real robot, is the bug in your code or in your model, and how would you tell?

Question 1
Your camera spots AprilTag 7 and reports a tx of about 14 degrees. What does that number tell your roboRIO, and where did the work to compute it happen?
A coprocessor does the image processing and publishes simple numbers like the fiducial ID, tx (yaw), and ty over NetworkTables. tx is the horizontal angle from the camera center to the target. The roboRIO never touches a pixel.
Question 2
A team aims at a tag by feeding tx into a controller with a setpoint of 0. They raise kP a lot and now the robot swings past the tag and hunts back and forth. What is the most likely cause?
Vision aiming is the same PID loop from the Control course, with tx as the error and 0 as the setpoint. Camera latency means the robot acts on slightly old data, so a high kP overshoots and oscillates. The fixes are a gentler kP, a touch of D, and a tolerance band.
Question 3
A pose estimator runs odometry every loop and folds in a camera fix whenever a tag is seen. Why does the tag sighting count as an absolute correction while odometry alone keeps drifting?
Because tags are at surveyed locations, a sighting pins the robot to an absolute spot and erases accumulated drift. Odometry, from Lesson 6.3, only integrates wheel and gyro motion, so its errors pile up until vision corrects them.
Question 4
In a SwerveDrivePoseEstimator, which call runs every loop and which runs only sometimes?
update() advances the odometry part each loop from the gyro and module positions. addVisionMeasurement() is the extra call you make only when the camera reports a tag, blending that absolute fix into the running estimate.
Question 5
A superstructure is modeled as a state machine with IDLE, INTAKING, LOADED, and SHOOTING. The driver presses the shoot button while the robot is in IDLE. What happens, and why is that the point?
Shooting is only reachable from LOADED. With no transition defined from IDLE on shoot, the event is rejected. A state machine makes illegal combinations like an empty shot unrepresentable, which is the safety it buys over a pile of separate booleans.

The Design Process

Decide on
purpose.

The best robots aren't the cleverest, they're the ones whose team decided clearly what to build and why. Before any metal gets cut, good teams understand the game, list what matters, dream up options, and choose between them on purpose instead of by whoever argued loudest.

Score your options, don't just argue.

When three concepts are on the table, a decision matrix settles it fairly: rate each concept on the things that matter, weight those things by importance, and add it up. Slide the weights below to match what you think wins games, and watch which concept comes out ahead.

Predict first
Three concepts are scored: one is Simple and Sturdy, one is Fast and Fancy, one is Balanced. You drag the Scoring weight all the way up and the others down. Which concept wins?
The total is each concept's rating times the weight on every criterion. Pile all the weight onto Scoring and the concept that scores highest there, Fast and Fancy, pulls ahead. Change what you value and you change the winner, which is the whole point of the matrix. Run the sim, max out Scoring, then max out Reliability instead and watch Simple and Sturdy take over.

Reliability quietly wins championships

A simple robot that scores a little every single match almost always beats a brilliant one that breaks down half the time. That's why "reliability" and "buildability" belong in the matrix right next to "scoring", and why everything you learned about clean wiring and solid joints matters here too. Pick the concept your team can actually finish, debug, and drive well, not the one that looks best on a whiteboard.

Going further · optional

A decision matrix is honest only if you set the weights before you score the concepts. If you already have a favorite and quietly tweak the weights until it wins, the matrix becomes a way to dress up a gut call as math. A stronger habit some teams use is to agree on the weights as a group first, then have different people score each concept and compare, the spots where scores disagree are exactly where the team needs to test or prototype before committing.

Show the thinking

The honest way to run a matrix is to lock the criteria and weights first, ideally from the game itself: how many points each thing scores, how often you will do it, and how risky it is to build. Then score every concept against those fixed weights, and treat the result as a guide to dig deeper, not a verdict to obey. Where scorers disagree or two concepts come out close, that is your signal to prototype and measure rather than argue, because real test data settles it better than another round of opinions. The matrix is most useful for exposing where you do not actually know enough yet, which is exactly what a prototype is for.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What's the first step in designing a robot?
You can't design for a game you don't understand. Read the manual, learn how points are scored, and write down the requirements before sketching anything.
Question 2
What does a decision matrix do?
It rates every concept on the things that matter, weighted by importance, and totals them. The choice becomes a defensible number instead of the loudest opinion.
Question 3
Why include reliability and buildability in the criteria?
Consistency wins. A robot you can actually finish and that scores every match outperforms a brilliant design that spends half its matches broken in the pit.

Part 7 · 02 Design & CAD
CAD with Onshape

Draw it
in 3D first.

CAD (computer-aided design) lets you build the entire robot on a screen before touching a saw. You start by drawing a flat sketch, then pull it into a solid 3D part. Onshape does this for free in a web browser, with your whole team editing the same model at once.

Sketch in 2D, then extrude.

Every CAD part starts as a sketch, a 2D outline, which you turn into a solid by extruding it, pushing the flat shape out to a thickness. Drag the depth below to extrude the plate, and toggle a bolt hole to see how the sketch decides the part.

At zero depth it's just a drawing. Pull it out and the same outline becomes a real plate you could machine. Change the sketch (add the hole) and the part changes with it, that link between sketch and solid is the heart of CAD.

In the shop

Sketch, feature, part, assembly

Those four words build up from a single line to a whole robot:

✏️
SketchA 2D outline on a flat plane, the shape of your part.
📦
FeatureAn operation like extrude or revolve that makes the sketch 3D.
🔩
PartOne finished 3D piece, ready to be made or bought.
🤖
AssemblyMany parts mated together; the whole robot, virtually.
Build the robot twice: once on screen, once for real

In an assembly you mate every part together exactly where it goes, so you find the collisions, the missing bolt clearance, the gearbox that won't fit, all on the computer, where fixing it costs a mouse drag instead of a sheet of aluminum. You can even check the robot's weight and balance before it exists. It's the ultimate version of "measure twice, cut once" from the very first lesson: design twice, build once.

Going further · optional

The real power of CAD shows up when you drive a sketch with named variables instead of fixed numbers. Define one variable like plate thickness or hole spacing, then reuse it everywhere, change it once and every part that depends on it updates together. Onshape also keeps a full version history, so your team can branch a design, try a risky idea, and roll back if it does not work, the same safety net that version control gives the programmers in the Deploy course. Try building a bracket whose hole pattern is driven by a single spacing variable and watch what happens when you change it.

Learn it for real: FRCDesign.org

This lesson is your orientation. Our training uses Onshape (free in the browser), and the place to actually build CAD skill is FRCDesign.org, an official Onshape education partner. Its free, FRC-specific course takes you from your first sketch to modeling a whole robot in Onshape. Come back here for the why; go there for the hands-on.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What is CAD used for?
CAD lets you model every part and the whole robot on screen, catching fit and clearance problems before any material is cut.
Question 2
How do you turn a 2D sketch into a solid 3D part?
Extruding pulls the 2D sketch out to a depth, turning the outline into a solid part. It's the most common CAD feature.
Question 3
What is an assembly?
An assembly places every part in its real position so you can check fit, motion, and weight, building the entire robot on screen first.

Linkages & Four-Bars

Reach out,
stay level.

A plain arm tilts whatever it carries as it swings, a tray would spill. A four-bar linkage uses a clever bit of geometry to extend reach while keeping the end effector at exactly the same angle the whole way. It's free, and it's everywhere.

Two parallel arms make a parallelogram.

A four-bar has two arms of equal length, pinned to a fixed base and to a moving coupler. Keep the arms parallel and the four links form a parallelogram, so the coupler can never tilt. Swing it below, and compare against a single arm that just rotates everything.

Predict first
Before you run it: you switch the linkage to Single arm and then swing the arm from straight up down toward horizontal. What does the tray and its game piece do?
A single arm rigidly carries its end effector, so the tray rotates by the same angle as the arm and the piece would roll right off. A four-bar holds level only because its equal parallel arms keep the four links a parallelogram. Run the sim on Single arm, swing it, and watch the tilt readout climb away from 0 degrees.

○Switch to Single arm. The tilt rule is tilt = 90 - arm angle. Work out the tilt at 30° in your head, then drag the slider there to check. It should read 60°.

★Gold: run the rule backwards. Park the single arm so the tilt reads exactly -45°. Work out the arm angle first, then check yourself.
In the shop

Why teams love the four-bar

The whole trick is geometry, no sensors, no control loop. As long as the arms stay parallel and equal, the coupler holds its angle for free:

▱
ParallelogramEqual, parallel arms keep the four links a parallelogram that can't tilt.
🧺
Level platformA tray, intake, or shooter on the coupler stays flat through the whole motion.
↗️
Reach out and upThe end effector moves along an arc, gaining height and reach at once.
🆓
No control neededThe angle is fixed by the linkage, not by software. One less thing to tune.
When you want the opposite

Sometimes you want the end effector to change angle as it reaches, to scoop low and tip up high, for instance. Then you deliberately use a non-parallel four-bar with unequal arms, and the coupler rotates a controlled amount through the motion. The parallel version is just the special case that holds level. Either way, the angle comes from the bar lengths you choose, which is why a four-bar is really a design you draw, not a thing you tune.

🔧 In the shop · 10 minutes

Build a four-bar out of cardboard: cut two arm strips the exact same length plus a base strip and a coupler strip, then pin the four corners with paper fasteners or pushpins so every joint can swing. Draw an arrow on the coupler and sweep the linkage through its full travel. Pass test: the arrow must point the same direction the whole way. If it tilts, the pin holes on your two arms are not the same distance apart, measure hole to hole and re-punch until they match, then re-pin one arm a hole shorter on purpose and watch the tilt come back. This one is already a no-shop build, cereal-box cardboard and four pins do the job.

Going further · optional

A four-bar gives a level platform for free, but it carves out a fixed arc, the end effector can only reach the points along that curve, not anywhere it likes. Compare that with an elevator plus a wrist, which can place a tool at almost any height and angle but needs more parts and more control. Worth chewing on: when is a four-bar's single clean motion exactly enough, and when does locking into one arc cost you a position you really needed?

Show the thinking

A four-bar is exactly enough when the task is move a piece between two or three fixed spots that all happen to fall on one arc, like pulling an intake from the floor up to a single scoring height while keeping it level. You design the arc to pass through the spots you care about, and you get level travel and rigid two-sided support with one motor. It costs you when the game wants placement at many heights or angles that do not share a curve, such as scoring on three different levels plus a hang: the arc that hits two of them will miss the third, and no amount of tuning bends a fixed-geometry arc to reach an off-curve point. That is the moment an elevator with a wrist, which trades parts and control for reach, earns its keep.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does a parallel four-bar linkage do?
A parallel four-bar forms a parallelogram, so the coupler (and whatever rides on it) keeps the same angle through the whole swing, staying level while it reaches out and up.
Question 2
Why does the platform stay level on a four-bar but tilt on a single arm?
It's pure geometry. A parallelogram's opposite sides stay parallel, so the coupler can't rotate. A single arm rigidly carries its end effector, so the end effector turns with the arm.
Question 3
How do you set the angle a four-bar's end effector holds?
The behavior is built into the geometry. Equal parallel arms hold level; deliberately unequal arms tilt by a set amount. You design it on paper, not in software.

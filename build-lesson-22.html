The Frame / Chassis

The box it all
bolts to.

The frame is the foundation every mechanism mounts to. It has to be rigid, the right size, and stable. The big trap is height: pile a heavy mechanism up high and the robot tips over the first time it stops fast.

Keep the weight low and the base wide.

A robot tips when its center of mass swings out past a wheel. Accelerate or stop hard and inertia tries to topple it. The defenses are simple: a low center of mass and a wide wheelbase. Push the sliders and try to keep this robot on its wheels.

Predict first
Before you run it: you leave the wheelbase and acceleration where they are and raise the CoM height slider toward the top. What happens to the robot's stability?
Raising the center of mass lengthens the lever that the inertia pull acts through, so the same nudge tips it past the front wheel sooner. A tall, narrow robot goes over with a gentle stop; a low, wide one shrugs it off. Run the sim, slide the CoM up, and watch the stability readout slip from stable to tipping at the same acceleration.

In the shop

Designing a good frame

Beyond stability, the frame has to be stiff and the right size. A few principles guide nearly every chassis:

⬇️
Low center of massPut heavy things (battery, motors) down low to resist tipping.
↔️
Wide wheelbaseA bigger footprint means the CoM has farther to travel before it tips.
🔺
Rigid & triangulatedGussets and cross-braces keep the frame from racking (Part 2.4).
📐
Frame perimeterThe footprint that bumpers wrap and that the rules size limits apply to.
The battery is your friend down low

The single heaviest thing on the robot is usually the battery, so where you put it matters a lot. Mount it low and centered and it pulls the center of mass right down where you want it. Teams that stack a tall arm or elevator pay for it by widening the base or weighting the bottom. There's always a tradeoff between reach (tall) and stability (low and wide), and the frame is where you make that call.

Going further · optional

Design challenge: tipping is not only about the static base, it is about how fast you change speed. Software can cap how hard the drivetrain accelerates and brakes, a slew-rate limit, so a tall robot stays planted without you widening the frame at all. Where would you rather spend your weight budget: a wider, heavier base that never tips, or a slimmer one that leans on code to stay upright? Real teams weigh exactly that.

Show the thinking

There is no single right answer, it depends on how tall and top-heavy the robot is and how much weight budget you have to spare. A wider, heavier base never tips and needs no clever code, but it eats weight you could have spent on mechanisms and it can make the robot slower to turn and easier to push around. A slimmer base with a slew-rate limit stays planted in normal driving and frees that weight, but it leans on the software being right, and a sensor fault, a hard collision, or a partly disabled robot can take the protection away. The common middle path is to set the wheelbase wide enough that the robot survives a bump on its own, then add the slew limit on top as insurance and to keep the ride smooth, rather than betting the whole match on either one alone.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What causes a robot to tip over?
Tipping happens when the center of mass moves outside the wheelbase. Accelerating or stopping fast adds inertia that pulls the CoM toward the edge, and past a wheel it topples.
Question 2
Which two changes make a robot harder to tip?
A low CoM and a wide base both increase how far the center of mass has to travel before it passes a wheel. Tall and narrow is exactly the recipe for tipping.
Question 3
Where should the heavy battery usually go?
The battery is often the heaviest part, so mounting it low and centered drops the overall center of mass, making the robot much more stable.

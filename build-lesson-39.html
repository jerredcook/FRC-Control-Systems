Maintenance & Spares

Keep it
alive.

A competition is a brutal day: a dozen matches of full-speed collisions, vibration, and quick fixes between rounds. The robot that wins isn't always the best one, it's often the one that keeps working when others break. That comes from a calm pit crew, a few key spares, and a checklist run before every single match.

Run the pre-match checklist.

Between matches you have minutes, not hours. A short, fixed checklist catches the things that quietly kill robots, a low battery, a loose bolt, a wiggling connector, before they cost you a match. Tick each item to get this robot match-ready.

Reliability is a habit, not luck

Everything in this whole course points here: clean wiring that survives a tug, solid joints, serviceable layout, a robot done early enough to shake out the bugs. On competition day that work pays off as a robot that just keeps running while others limp to the pit. Bring spares of the parts you know can fail, fix problems on the bench between matches, and run the checklist religiously. Consistency is the quiet superpower of great teams.

🔧 In the shop · 10 minutes

Write a six-item pre-ride checklist for your bike where every item is a touch or a tug, not a glance: squeeze both tires, pull each brake lever, tug the wheel quick-releases, twist the bars, check the seat clamp, spin the pedals. Run it twice in a row. Pass test: the second run takes under two minutes and you never stop to think about what comes next. If an item makes you pause, rewrite it as one physical action, that is exactly how pit checklists get fast. On a team night, run the robot version: tug the battery connector and put a wrench on a few frame bolts.

Going further · optional

Here is why a "fresh" battery wins close matches even when both packs read a similar voltage at rest. Every battery has internal resistance, and the harder the robot pulls current, the more voltage gets lost inside the battery itself, so the number sags under load. An older or barely-charged pack has higher resistance and sags further, which is exactly when your drivetrain bogs down in a push. Good teams log and retire packs that sag too much, the same way you would notice a bike tire that slowly goes soft. Ask your team how they track which batteries are still strong.

Show the thinking

Most teams number every battery and use a load tester or analyzer, commonly a Battery Beak, that briefly pulls current and reports the pack's internal resistance and condition. A fresh pack reads low resistance, often around 10 to 15 milliohms, and an aging one creeps up toward 20, 30, or more, which is the narrow pipe that makes voltage sag under load. They log those readings over the season and retire packs whose resistance keeps climbing or that sag too far in the test, rotating fresh, rested batteries into matches and tired ones out.

Question 1
What should you do before every match?
A short pre-match checklist catches a low battery, loose bolt, or wiggling connector before it fails on the field.
Question 2
Why swap to a fresh battery each match?
A fully charged battery holds voltage under load, so the robot keeps its full power through the match, often the margin in a close game.

Why carry spare motors, wheels, and controllers?
Things break at competition. Spares of the parts you know can fail let you repair fast and get back on the field.

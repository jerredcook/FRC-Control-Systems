Battery Management

Win on
fresh cells.

Matches are won and lost on battery discipline. A team that manages a fleet of healthy batteries has a real edge over one that grabs whatever cell is lying around, and it is mostly habits anyone can learn.

Think of your batteries like a relay team.

A relay squad never sends the same runner out exhausted twice in a row. They rest, recover, and keep track of who is fresh. Your batteries work the same way. A battery fresh off the robot is warm and partly spent, so it needs to cool and recharge before it races again. Manage a whole fleet of them well and you start every match with a strong leg, while a careless team is sending out a tired runner and wondering why they fade at the end.

Charging is hot, heavy work, treat it with respect

FRC batteries are heavy and store a lot of energy, so handling and charging always happen with a mentor watching the charging station. Use a proper smart charger (commonly around 6 amps) and never fast-charge a battery beyond the rating printed on it. A battery fresh off the robot is warm; let it rest and cool first, then charge it fully. Never charge a battery while it is still on the robot, that is against the rules and a real fire and shock hazard. If a battery is hot, swollen, cracked, or leaking, stop and get a mentor.

In the pit

What healthy looks like

Here is the trap: a tired battery can still read a perfectly normal voltage when nothing is pulling on it. Resting voltage lies, the same lesson from earlier in this unit. The number only tells the truth once the battery is under load, because that is when a weak cell sags. So teams do not trust the resting reading alone.

Instead they use a battery analyzer (often called a Battery Beak) that briefly loads the battery and reports its internal resistance and overall condition. A fresh battery has low internal resistance and holds its voltage under load. As a battery ages, that resistance creeps up and its capacity falls, which is the analyzer's way of telling you the battery is tired. A tired battery is what causes a mid-match brownout, the robot stuttering or rebooting right when you need it. When a battery starts testing poorly, you retire it. One weak cell can lose you a match.

Rotation and the logbook

Label every battery with a number and keep a log of which match each one ran. Then rotate them so each battery gets time to cool and fully recharge before it goes out again. A good rhythm is to never use the same battery two matches in a row. The logbook does two jobs: it forces the cooldown gap so a warm, half-spent battery never gets rushed back into service, and over a season it lets you spot the cell that keeps showing up weak on the analyzer, so you can retire it before it costs you.

On the robot

Connections and the rules

The battery feeds the robot through a big Anderson SB connector and heavy 6 AWG leads that run to the main breaker. Those fat wires and that chunky connector exist for a reason: a loose or corroded connection adds resistance, which means more voltage sag under load and, worse, a real fire risk as a bad joint heats up. Crimped lugs beat loose ones, and the terminals should be protected and insulated so nothing can short across them.

Inspectors check the electrical setup before you compete, and a few ideas stay true every year even as the exact rule numbers change:

The competition-day habit

It comes down to one routine you repeat every match: a fully charged, rested, healthy battery goes in for every single match. Between matches you swap to a fresh one, and the battery you pulled goes straight to the charger and gets written in the log. That is the whole discipline. A team that runs this loop without thinking about it almost never gets caught by a dead battery, and that reliability quietly wins matches all weekend.

Going further · optional

Top teams turn the analyzer numbers into a season-long story. If you write down each battery's internal resistance every time you test it, you can watch the trend climb over months and predict when a cell is about to go bad, retiring it on your schedule instead of in the middle of an elimination match. Some teams even let cold batteries warm to room temperature before a match, since a cold battery sags more under load. Here is one to chew on with a mentor: if two batteries read the same resting voltage but one has noticeably higher internal resistance on the Beak, which one would you trust in the finals, and how would you explain that choice to a teammate?

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
A battery reads a normal resting voltage. Why isn't that enough to trust it?
Resting voltage lies. A worn battery can show a normal number with nothing pulling on it, then sag badly the moment the robot draws current. A battery analyzer like the Beak loads the cell and reads its internal resistance, which is the honest test.
Question 2
A battery came off the robot warm and you want it ready again. What's the safe move?
A battery off the robot is warm and partly spent, so let it cool and rest first, then charge it fully on a proper smart charger. Never charge a battery on the robot (against the rules and unsafe), never fast-charge beyond its rating, and always have a mentor overseeing the charging station.
Question 3
Why bother labeling, logging, and rotating your batteries?
Rotation gives every battery a cooldown and a full recharge between matches, so you never send out a warm, half-spent cell. The log also exposes the battery that keeps showing up weak on the analyzer, so you can retire it before it causes a mid-match brownout.

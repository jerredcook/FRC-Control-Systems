Arms & Wrists

Gravity hits
sideways.

An arm is the easiest mechanism to build and one of the trickiest to hold in place, because gravity's pull on it changes with the angle. It fights hardest when the arm sticks straight out, and not at all when it points straight up or down.

The holding torque follows a cosine.

Hold an arm at an angle and the motor has to fight gravity's twist on it. That twist depends on the horizontal reach of the load: maximum when the arm is level (sticking straight out), zero when it's vertical (load directly over the pivot). Swing the arm and watch the torque it takes to hold.

In the shop

Taming an arm

Because the load on the motor swings from zero to maximum as the arm moves, arms need a little extra care, in the structure and in the code:

📉
Torque ∝ cos(angle)Max holding torque when horizontal, zero when vertical. Size the gearing for the worst case.
🛑
Hard stopsPhysical limits so the arm can't swing past where it should and break something.
🪀
CounterbalanceA gas spring or rubber band can offload gravity so the motor does less work.
🧠
Feedforward in codeThe Control course's ArmFeedforward adds kG·cos(angle) to hold against gravity automatically.
This is where building meets coding

That cosine curve is exactly why the Control course's ArmFeedforward has a kG term multiplied by cos(angle): the code predicts the gravity torque from the arm's angle and feeds in just enough motor effort to cancel it. On the build side, you size the gearbox for the horizontal worst case, add hard stops, and maybe a counterbalance so the motor isn't fighting gravity alone. A good arm is designed and tuned together.

Going further · optional

The gravity torque also depends on where the weight sits along the arm, not the angle alone, and that gives you a free design lever. A heavy gripper at the very tip pulls hardest because its weight acts at the longest distance from the pivot, the same reason a backpack feels heavier held at arm's length than against your chest. So pulling mass inboard, or balancing it with a counterweight on the opposite side of the pivot, can shrink the worst-case torque before you ever touch the gearbox. Try sketching where you would add a counterweight to cancel a heavy end load.

Show the thinking

Put the counterweight on the opposite side of the pivot from the gripper so its torque pulls the arm the other way and cancels the load's torque. What matters is weight times distance, not weight alone, so a small mass far out on a short stub behind the pivot can balance a heavy gripper at the tip, the same way a seesaw balances a big kid close to the center against a small kid out at the end. Mounting it far back lets you keep the added weight light, which protects your total robot weight budget. The honest catch is that a counterweight cancels gravity but adds rotating mass, so the arm becomes slower to start and stop, and many teams instead use a constant-force gas spring or surgical tubing to get the same gravity cancellation without the extra inertia.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
At what angle does gravity fight an arm the hardest?
Horizontal is the worst case: the load's full weight acts at the longest horizontal distance from the pivot, so the holding torque peaks. Vertical puts the load over the pivot, requiring none.
Question 2
How does the holding torque change as the arm rotates from horizontal to vertical?
Torque is proportional to cos(angle from horizontal). It's at maximum when level and drops to zero when the arm is vertical and the load sits right over the pivot.
Question 3
How does robot code automatically hold an arm against gravity?
The Control course's ArmFeedforward computes the gravity torque from the angle (kG times the cosine) and feeds in exactly that much motor effort, so the arm holds steady at any angle.

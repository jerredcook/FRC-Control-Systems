Intakes & Rollers

Grab it off
the floor.

An intake is how a robot picks game pieces up off the ground: spinning squishy wheels that squeeze the piece a little and pull it in. Too little squeeze and it slips; too much and it jams. There's a sweet spot.

Compression is the whole trick.

The rollers sit a bit closer to the floor than the game piece is tall, so they compress it and grip. The spinning surface then drags the piece in. Set the compression and roller speed, then feed a piece and see if the intake grabs it.

Predict first
Before you run it: you crank the Compression slider all the way to the top, then press Feed game piece. What happens?
There is a sweet spot. A little compression grips and pulls the piece in, but squeeze too hard and the piece can't even fit through the gap, so it jams at the mouth. Too little compression, or too slow a roller, lets it slip back out instead. Run the sim, max the compression, feed a piece, and watch it jam.

In the shop

Building an intake that works

Intakes look simple but are finicky. A few choices make the difference between a reliable grab and a frustrating one:

🫧
Compliant wheelsSquishy wheels conform to the game piece and grip without crushing it.
🤏
CompressionSet the gap smaller than the piece so it's squeezed, but it can still enter.
🌀
Surface speedFast enough to drag the piece in, not so fast it bounces off.
🛡️
Over or under bumperThe intake reaches over the bumper or scoops from below, depending on the game.
Prototype the intake first, always

You can't calculate the perfect compression and speed; you find them by testing. Smart teams build a rough intake out of scrap on day one and feed real game pieces through it, tweaking the gap, wheel type, and speed until it grabs every time. An intake that works on paper but fumbles on the field loses matches, so it's usually the first thing a team prototypes and the last thing they stop tuning.

🔧 In the shop · 10 minutes

Set two stacks of books on a table with a gap between them a little smaller than a tennis ball, push the ball through, and close the gap about a centimeter at a time. Pass test: you can name two gap sizes, the one where the ball squeezes through with a firm grip on it, and the one where it jams and will not enter, the sim's green band and jam zone in real life. If every gap feels the same, use a squishier ball, a foam ball or a partly deflated one shows the sweet spot best. If your team has real game pieces, run the same test by pressing one into the robot's intake gap by hand with the robot powered off.

Going further · optional

Most reliable intakes do not stop at one roller, they hand the piece off to a second set of wheels deeper inside so it can never drift back out, a bit like a conveyor passing a box from one belt to the next. A design worth sketching: where does the piece go the instant after the roller grabs it, and what keeps it there while the robot drives and bumps around? Many teams also tune the two sides at slightly different speeds to spin a piece into the right orientation as it enters. Catching is easy, controlling what happens next is the real craft.

Show the thinking

The instant after the first roller grabs the piece, it should be driven into a defined pocket where a hard stop or a backstop wall sets its final position, not left sitting against the spinning intake. A second set of wheels or a belt past the entry keeps a light squeeze on the piece so driving, bumps, and tipping cannot shake it loose, and a sensor at the stop tells the code the piece has fully seated so the rollers can ease off. Running the two sides at slightly different speeds works because a faster wheel on one edge spins the piece about its center, the way pushing one side of a coin on a table makes it rotate, which lets you square up an odd entry angle before the piece reaches the stop. Good looks like every piece ending in the same spot and orientation regardless of how it came in.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
How does a roller intake grip a game piece?
The roller sits closer than the piece is tall, so it squeezes the piece. That compression creates grip, and the spinning surface pulls the piece into the robot.
Question 2
What happens with too little compression?
Without enough squeeze there's no grip, the roller spins against the piece and it slips out. Too much compression is the opposite problem: the piece can't fit and jams.
Question 3
What's the best way to dial in an intake's compression and speed?
There's no formula for the perfect grab. You build a rough intake, feed real pieces through it, and adjust the gap, wheels, and speed until it works every time.

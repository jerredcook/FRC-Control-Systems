Dashboards & Telemetry

Log it, then
read it.

When something goes wrong in a match, you can't pause and inspect. So you record every signal that matters and study the timeline afterward. Plotting the setpoint next to the measured value turns "it didn't work" into a precise answer.

A match is over in seconds. The log lasts forever.

Two tools, two jobs. Elastic is the live driver dashboard, a quick glance during the match. AdvantageScope reads the recorded log afterward, so you can scrub the timeline and see exactly what every signal did. Drag the cursor across this recorded shooter sequence and watch the values.

Predict first
Before you scrub it: at the moments where teal (measured RPM) dips below amber (setpoint RPM), what do you expect the purple current and red battery traces to be doing?
A shot drags the flywheel down, so the controller pulls hard to recover: current spikes up and the battery sags under that draw, which is exactly why the dip happens. Reading the four signals together tells the story a live glance cannot. Scrub the cursor to a teal dip and watch the purple current spike and the red battery sag line up with it.

In code

Record everything, automatically

One call starts a full match log. Then publish each signal you care about beside its setpoint, and RobotPy records the whole timeline for you to replay:

robot.py Python · RobotPy
def robotInit(self):
    wpilib.DataLogManager.start()                       # record to a .wpilog file
    DriverStation.startDataLog(wpilib.DataLogManager.getLog())

class Shooter(commands2.Subsystem):
    def periodic(self):
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("shooter/setpointRpm", self.setpoint_rpm)
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("shooter/measuredRpm", self.measured_rpm)
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("shooter/currentAmps", self.current_amps)

# review the .wpilog in AdvantageScope; watch it live in Elastic
Log the setpoint next to the measurement

A measured value alone tells you what happened, not whether it was right. Logging setpoint_rpm beside measured_rpm lets you see the gap, the exact thing a control loop is trying to close. "The shooter never reached speed" stops being a guess and becomes a line you can point at. When in doubt, log more; storage is cheap and lost data is gone forever.

Going further · optional

Logging is not free: every signal you record takes space and a little time on the roboRIO, and flooding NetworkTables with too many fast updates can actually slow your loop. Try logging at a sensible rate and being picky about what truly helps you debug. A real gotcha to think about: when AdvantageScope shows the shooter dipping right as the battery sags, which one caused which? Logging the timestamps carefully is what lets you tell cause from coincidence.

Show the thinking

Timestamps tell you the order of events, and a cause has to come before its effect. If the voltage sag starts a few milliseconds before the shooter speed dips, the battery is the likely cause; if the dip comes first, something else is dragging both down. Two signals moving together is correlation, and that alone does not prove one caused the other, so line them up on the same time axis and look at which moved first. Be careful that all your signals share one clock, because logging from different sources at different rates can fake a lead that is not real.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What's the difference between Elastic and AdvantageScope?
Elastic gives the drive team a glance during the match. AdvantageScope reads the .wpilog after, so you can scrub the timeline and analyze exactly what every signal did.
Question 2
Why log the setpoint alongside the measured value?
The measurement alone can't tell you if it was correct. Side by side with the setpoint, the error jumps out, which is exactly what you need to diagnose a control problem.
Question 3
What does DataLogManager.start() do?
It opens a log on the roboRIO and records published signals throughout the match, giving you a complete timeline to replay in AdvantageScope.

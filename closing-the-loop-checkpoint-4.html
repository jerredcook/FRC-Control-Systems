Question 1
You are tuning a real elevator start to finish. Which order gives a stable, easy result?
Feedforward before feedback, always. Measured kS, kG, kV, kA supply most of the voltage, so kP stays small and stable while it only trims the residual. Then profile the move and validate under load.
Question 2
After SysId on a well-built mechanism, your kP comes out surprisingly small. Is something wrong?
When feedforward (kS, kG, kV, kA) carries the load, the feedback term has little left to do. A modest kP that trims residual error is exactly the goal, and it stays easy to tune and stable.
Question 3
You reuse the elevator's workflow on an arm, but the arm sags worst when it is near horizontal and barely sags when near vertical. Why, and what is the right gravity model?
An arm fights full weight at horizontal (cos 0 = 1) and almost none straight up (cos 90 = 0). Arm_Cosine scales kG by cos(angle). The same end-to-end workflow as the elevator applies; only the gravity type changes.
Question 4
A flywheel shooter holds a target speed, not a position. Compared with the elevator's position loop, which gain does most of the work, and what about kG?
Velocity control is kV's domain: kV times target speed predicts the holding voltage. A flywheel has no gravity term to fight, so kG is not used. kP only trims small errors.
Question 5
On that same flywheel, a teammate keeps cranking kP to make recovery faster after a shot, but the speed starts hunting and oscillating. What is the better mental model for a velocity loop?
In a velocity loop, kV supplies the bulk and a small kP trims. A large kP amplifies tiny velocity errors and the loop chases its own tail. After a shot, kV holds the base voltage while a modest kP drives recovery.

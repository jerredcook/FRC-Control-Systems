Hardware lab

Close the loop
on a real sensor.

A strip of black tape, a $60 robot, and one number to tune. In this lab the XRP's reflectance sensor measures how far off the line the robot has drifted, and a P controller you tune live steers it back. This is Part 7's big idea, sense then correct, on hardware you can hold.

What you need

Three things and a strip of tape

  • An XRP with the line-follower (reflectance) board mounted at the front, sensors facing the floor.
  • A laptop with RobotPy and WPILib installed, which you set up back in Part 1.
  • Charged batteries in the XRP. A tired pack makes the motors weak and your tuning misleading.
  • A line to follow: a meter or two of black electrical tape on a light floor, whiteboard, or poster board, with gentle curves. A closed loop is the most fun.

The XRP is the same low-cost robot the course capstone uses. If you have not met it yet, the short version: it is officially supported by WPILib, so the same RobotPy subsystems and commands you have written all course drive it directly.

The big idea

Error in, steering out

In lesson 29 you closed a loop on a camera. Today the sensor costs a few dollars and stares at the floor.

The reflectance board shines infrared light down and measures the bounce. A bright floor bounces the light back, so the sensor reads near 0.0. Black tape swallows the light, so it reads near 1.0. There are two of these sensors side by side at the front of the XRP, and the tape line rides in the gap between them.

That gives us a feedback loop with one signed number as the error: right reading - left reading. Drift left and the tape slides under the right sensor, so the error goes positive. Drift right and it goes negative. Centered, both sensors see mostly floor and the error is near zero. The controller is one line: rotation = kP * error.

Think of riding a bike along the painted edge line of a path. You glance down, see you have drifted off the line, and steer back, a small drift gets a small correction, a big drift gets a bigger one. How hard you steer per centimeter of drift is your personal kP. Too timid and you wander off on curves. Too aggressive and you weave back and forth across the line. Today you get to tune that number on a real robot, live.

Step 1 · Track & connect

Lay the line, join the robot's WiFi

First the track: stick your tape to a light surface in a loop with gentle curves. Avoid corners sharper than about 90 degrees for now, a creeping robot can learn sharp turns later.

Then the connection, the same setup the capstone uses:

  1. Install RobotPy and WPILib, if you have not already (Part 1).
  2. Make a new project from the RobotPy XRP example.
  3. Power on the XRP and connect your laptop to the XRP's WiFi network.
  4. Run the program in simulation with robotpy sim. This connects to the XRP over WiFi.
  5. The Simulation GUI opens. You will enable the robot from here in Step 4.

Remember the XRP's one mental model: your code runs in simulation on your laptop, and a WebSocket link over the XRP's own WiFi carries motor commands out and sensor readings back, many times per second.

What you should see

The Simulation GUI opens with the Robot State panel showing Disabled. If your terminal scrolls connection errors instead, your laptop is still on your regular WiFi, not the XRP's network. Switch networks and run robotpy sim again.

Step 2 · Read the sensor

Get numbers out of the floor

Before closing any loop, look at the raw measurement. This Drivetrain is the same XRP subsystem shape you know, motors on channels 0 and 1, encoders on DIO 4 to 7, the gyro, plus one new class: XRPReflectanceSensor. Its periodic() publishes both readings to the dashboard every loop. Note that RobotPy keeps the WPILib method names like getLeftReflectanceValue.

drivetrain.py python
from wpilib.xrp import XRPMotor, XRPGyro, XRPReflectanceSensor
from wpilib import Encoder, SmartDashboard
import math
import commands2


class Drivetrain(commands2.Subsystem):
    WHEEL_DIAMETER_M = 0.060   # 60 mm wheels
    COUNTS_PER_REV = 585.0     # from the XRP example

    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        # XRP drive motors live on channels 0 (left) and 1 (right)
        self.left = XRPMotor(0)
        self.right = XRPMotor(1)
        # Wheel encoders are wired to these DIO channels
        self.left_enc = Encoder(4, 5)
        self.right_enc = Encoder(6, 7)
        self.gyro = XRPGyro()
        # New for this lab: the two downward-facing reflectance sensors
        self.line = XRPReflectanceSensor()

        meters_per_count = (math.pi * self.WHEEL_DIAMETER_M) / self.COUNTS_PER_REV
        self.left_enc.setDistancePerPulse(meters_per_count)
        self.right_enc.setDistancePerPulse(meters_per_count)
        self.right.setInverted(True)   # the right side is mirrored

    def arcade_drive(self, speed, rotation):
        self.left.set(speed + rotation)
        self.right.set(speed - rotation)

    # Reflectance: 0.0 = bright floor, 1.0 = black tape
    def left_line(self):
        return self.line.getLeftReflectanceValue()

    def right_line(self):
        return self.line.getRightReflectanceValue()

    def periodic(self):
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("Line left", self.left_line())
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("Line right", self.right_line())

Run robotpy sim again, then in the Simulation GUI open NetworkTables → SmartDashboard. Pick the XRP up and slide it around the tape by hand. You do not even need to enable the robot, sensors report while disabled.

What you should see

Over the bare floor both numbers sit low, often under 0.2. Slide the robot until one sensor crosses the tape and that number jumps toward 1.0 while the other stays low. If neither number ever moves, jump to the troubleshooting table below.

Step 3 · One number: the error

Two readings become one signed error

A controller wants a single number that says how far off you are and in which direction. Subtracting one reading from the other gives exactly that. Add this method to Drivetrain:

Add inside drivetrain.py python
    def line_error(self):
        # Positive when the tape is under the right sensor (robot drifted left),
        # negative when it is under the left (robot drifted right).
        # Near zero when the line is centered between them.
        return self.right_line() - self.left_line()

And publish it, so you can watch the error live. Add one line to periodic():

Add inside periodic() python
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("Line error", self.line_error())
What you should see

Slide the robot slowly across the tape by hand and watch Line error: it swings positive as the tape passes under the right sensor, through zero when the line is centered, then negative under the left sensor. That sweep from plus to minus is your whole control story in one number.

Step 4 · Close the loop

The P controller, one line of math

Now the payoff. Drive forward at a steady base speed and steer by kP * error. We read kP from the dashboard every loop, that is your live tuning knob. Making follow_line() the autonomous command means no joystick setup: put the robot on the line, pick Autonomous, click Enable.

robotcontainer.py python
import commands2.cmd as cmd
from wpilib import SmartDashboard

from drivetrain import Drivetrain


class RobotContainer:
    def __init__(self):
        self.drivetrain = Drivetrain()
        # Publish the knob once so it shows up in the sim GUI, editable
        SmartDashboard.putNumber("Line kP", 0.4)

    def follow_line(self):
        def steer():
            error = self.drivetrain.line_error()
            kp = SmartDashboard.getNumber("Line kP", 0.4)
            self.drivetrain.arcade_drive(0.35, kp * error)  # base speed 0.35

        return cmd.runEnd(
            steer,
            lambda: self.drivetrain.arcade_drive(0, 0),
            self.drivetrain,
        )

    def getAutonomousCommand(self):
        return self.follow_line()

Set the XRP down so the tape runs between its two sensors, pointing along the line. In the Simulation GUI pick Autonomous and click Enable. Click Disable to stop it, the runEnd end function stops the motors for you.

What you should see

The XRP creeps forward and visibly steers to keep the tape between its sensors, nudging left and right as the line curves. If it confidently steers away from the tape instead, your robot's directions are flipped relative to ours. That is row two of the troubleshooting table: swap the subtraction in line_error().

Step 5 · Tune kP live

Turn the knob while it drives

Here is the part no simulator can give you. Line kP is sitting in the Simulation GUI under NetworkTables → SmartDashboard, and your command reads it every 20 ms. Change the number while the robot is driving and the loop's behavior changes under your fingers.

  1. Set Line kP to 0.1. Watch the robot wander: it corrects too gently and slides off on the first real curve.
  2. Step it up: 0.3, 0.5, 0.8. Each bump makes the steering snappier and the curves cleaner.
  3. Keep going until it misbehaves: around 2.0 or so the robot starts to fishtail, whipping across the tape and back, swinging wider each pass.
  4. Back off until the weave just disappears. That value, just below the wobble, is your tuned kP for this robot, this tape, this floor.
  5. Write your number down. Then swap in tired batteries or a faster base speed and see how the best kP shifts.
What you should see

A personality change at each step: sluggish and lost at low kP, calm and locked-on in the middle, twitchy and oscillating up high. That progression is exactly the kP tuning story from Closing the Loop, except now the plot is a robot on your floor instead of a curve on a screen.

When it misbehaves

Going further · optional

Once the loop holds the line, push it. Follow the tape's edge with a single sensor by making the error reading - 0.5, so the robot balances on the boundary between dark and light. Slow down in the turns by shrinking the base speed as the size of the error grows, then race for the fastest clean lap. Feeling brave? Add a small D term that reacts to how fast the error is changing and watch the weave damp out at kP values that used to fishtail. Question to chew on: your tuned kP stops working when you double the base speed. Why?

Show the thinking

At double the speed, the robot covers twice the distance between corrections, so by the time a correction lands, the error has grown further than before. The same kP now produces corrections that are effectively late, and late corrections overshoot, which is the seed of oscillation. You either lower kP, slow down, or add a D term to anticipate. Fast systems need gentler or smarter gains, and that trade-off follows you all the way up to a full-size FRC drivetrain.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
In this lab's P controller, what exactly is the error?
Both sensors stare at the floor, reading near 0 over bright surface and near 1 over black tape. Right minus left packs everything the controller needs into one signed number: positive means the tape slid under the right sensor, negative means the left, zero means centered. The loop steers with rotation = kp * error to drive that number back to zero.
Question 2
You raise Line kP from 0.5 to 3.0 and the XRP starts whipping left and right across the tape, swinging wider each pass. What is happening?
A too-big kP overcorrects: drift a little right, jerk hard left, sail past the line, jerk back even harder. It is like steering a bike with a death grip, every wobble becomes a bigger wobble. Lower kP until the weave stops; the best value sits just below where the fishtailing begins.
Question 3
You enable the follower and the XRP steers away from the tape instead of back onto it. What is the most likely cause?
Steering confidently in the wrong direction means the loop is closed with the wrong sign, maybe the sensor board faces the other way or a motor inversion differs from ours. Flip the subtraction once and it hugs the line. Darker tape is exactly what the sensor wants, and a kP of zero would drive straight ahead, not turn away.

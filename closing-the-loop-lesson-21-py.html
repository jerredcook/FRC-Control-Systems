The Flywheel

Hold a speed,
not a place.

A shooter doesn't go somewhere - it spins at a target RPM and recovers the instant a game piece steals its energy. Welcome to velocity control, where kV runs the show.

Same loop, different target.

The elevator and arm controlled position. A flywheel controls velocity - you don't care where it is, only how fast it's spinning. The control law barely changes: it's still feedforward plus feedback. But the balance flips. For velocity, kV - volts per unit of speed - does almost all the work, and kP shrinks to a whisper.

There's no kG (gravity doesn't slow a wheel), and big kP is actually harmful here - it makes the speed hunt and oscillate. Spin it up below, then steal its energy with Shoot and watch it recover.

In code

kV leads, kP follows

The setup is the on-motor loop you already know - just a VelocityVoltage request and gains weighted toward kV. No kG, and a deliberately small kP:

flywheel.py Python · Phoenix 6
cfg.slot0.k_s = 0.10   # volts to overcome friction and start spinning
cfg.slot0.k_v = 0.12   # the workhorse: volts per rotation/sec
cfg.slot0.k_p = 0.18   # small - big kP makes the speed hunt
# no gravity_type: a flywheel doesn't fight gravity

self.request = VelocityVoltage(0).with_slot(0)

def set_speed(self, rps: float) -> None:
    self.motor.set_control(self.request.with_velocity(rps))

def at_speed(self, rps: float) -> bool:
    return abs(self.motor.get_velocity().value - rps) < 1.0
Wait for "at speed" before you shoot

The whole game is timing. Spin up, confirm at_speed(), then feed a game piece - fire while it's still ramping and the shot goes short. After each shot the speed dips; kV holds the base and kP closes the gap so you're ready for the next one fast. That recovery time is what separates a good shooter from a great one.

From the field

Why a flywheel fights back

A flywheel has one job: hold a repeatable speed, because the shot rides on it. That makes velocity control fussier than almost anything else on the robot, and it leans hard on two things most mechanisms never worry about: how cleanly and quickly you can measure speed, and how finely you can tune the loop.

The measurement problem. Velocity isn't sensed directly; it's computed from how fast the encoder counts change, and a coarse or noisy speed signal forces an ugly trade. Filter it heavily and the number comes out smooth but late, so the loop reacts a beat behind and recovery drags. Filter it lightly and it's jumpy, so kP amplifies the jitter and the speed hunts up and down. You get stuck in the middle, never quite clean and never quite fast.

The granularity problem. If the speed signal is coarse and the loop is configured in big steps, small gain changes either do nothing or tip the whole thing into oscillation. There's no fine adjustment left to find the sweet spot, so tuning turns into guesswork.

We ran a NEO on a SPARK MAX, and that combo reads velocity off the NEO's built-in encoder through the controller's own filtering, which is relatively coarse and already smoothed. For most mechanisms that's fine. For a shooter chasing a tight, repeatable RPM it's exactly the lag-versus-noise wall above, and it's why a NEO flywheel can be maddening to dial in.

Hardware note: where the granularity comes from

A Kraken or Falcon (TalonFX) measures speed from its integrated rotor sensor at high resolution and runs the closed loop at 1 kHz on the motor controller itself, so the velocity signal is both cleaner and lower latency. You set kS / kV / kA feedforward in real units and lean on kV, with a light kP to recover between shots. With torque-current (FOC) control you can command torque straight to the rotor for an even smoother spin-up. That finer resolution is what actually lets you nail a steady RPM. If you've fought a SPARK MAX flywheel that won't hold speed, a TalonFX-based motor is the common upgrade path, and the reason is granularity, not magic.

flywheel.py · the CTRE angle Python · Phoenix 6
# torque-current FOC: command torque straight to the rotor,
# the tightest, fastest-recovering way to hold a flywheel speed
self.request = VelocityTorqueCurrentFOC(0).with_slot(0)

# gains are in AMPS now, not volts, and run at 1 kHz on the motor (illustrative)
cfg.slot0.k_s = 4.0    # amps to break static friction
cfg.slot0.k_v = 0.10   # amps per rotation/sec to hold speed against drag
cfg.slot0.k_p = 5.0    # amps per rps of error, the fine high-rate correction

Whatever the hardware, the tuning order doesn't change: lean on feedforward first (kV, or kS in current mode), keep kP small, wait for at_speed() before you fire, and leave room in the routine for the speed to recover between shots. Better hardware just gives you more room to get all four right.

Going further · optional

There is a sneaky reason a flywheel recovers faster than your gut expects: the same spinning mass that makes it slow to start also makes it a tiny energy bank. Right after a shot, the wheel still carries most of its stored spin, so feedforward plus a gentle kP only has to top off a small dip rather than rebuild from zero, like a heavy bike flywheel that barely flinches when you tap the brake. That points at a real design tension: a heavier wheel dips less per shot but takes longer to spin up the first time. If your match plan is rapid-fire scoring, would you reach for a heavier wheel or a lighter one, and what does that cost you?

Show the thinking

For rapid fire, lean heavier. The extra inertia means each shot pulls only a small dip out of the wheel, so it holds speed shot to shot and you wait less between them. The cost is the first spin-up: more inertia takes longer and more energy to reach speed, so the wheel must be running before the first shot, and the same inertia makes any commanded speed change sluggish. The usual compromise is enough mass to ride through a burst plus strong feedforward so recovery rides the stored spin rather than rebuilding it.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
For velocity control, which gain does most of the work?
kV directly predicts the voltage needed to sustain a given speed, so it carries a velocity loop. kP only trims small errors, and there's no kG because gravity doesn't resist a spinning wheel.
Question 2
Why keep kP small on a flywheel?
With kV doing the heavy lifting, a big kP overreacts to tiny errors and sets the speed oscillating. A gentle kP cleans up disturbances without destabilizing the loop.
Question 3
After a game piece dips the speed, what brings it back up?
kV keeps supplying the steady-state voltage, and kP sees the sudden velocity error and adds extra volts to recover. That's why you wait for at_speed() again before the next shot.

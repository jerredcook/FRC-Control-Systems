Power Tools

Match the
speed.

A power tool does in seconds what a hand tool does in minutes, which also means it makes mistakes in seconds. The first skill is matching the tool's speed to the material. Too fast burns; too slow chatters.

Different materials want different speeds.

Drill a hole in steel at wood speed and you'll burn the bit dull. Drill plastic too fast and it melts and grabs. Each material has a sweet spot. Pick a material below and dial the drill speed into the green band.

In the shop

The shop's power tools

A handful of machines do most of the work. Before you run any of them for the first time, a mentor has to train you on that machine and stay nearby, and you never run a power tool when you are alone in the shop. Once you are cleared, each has the same two rules: secure the part, and let the tool reach speed before it touches the work:

🛠️
Drill pressAccurate, square holes. Clamp the part; never hold it by hand.
🪚
BandsawFast straight and curved cuts in stock. Keep fingers clear of the blade line.
🔌
Hand drill / driverFor drilling and driving in place; two hands, steady.
🌀
Belt / disc sanderDeburr and shape edges fast. It removes skin just as fast.
Clamp it. Always clamp it.

The most common power-tool injury is a part that grabs and spins. A drill bit catches the workpiece and whips it around like a propeller, with your hand right there. Clamp every part down so the tool fights the clamp, not your fingers. If you can't clamp it, it's too small to machine safely, find another way.

Let it reach speed, then ease in

Start the tool and let it spin up before it meets the material, then feed slowly and steadily. Forcing a tool or stalling it is how bits snap and motors burn. For metal, a drop of cutting fluid keeps things cool and the edge sharp. Steady pressure, not brute force.

Going further · optional

The "right speed" you dial in by feel actually comes from a formula machinists use: surface speed, how fast the cutting edge travels, should stay roughly constant for a given material, so a bigger drill bit has to spin slower than a small one. With a mentor running the machine, ask them to show you a speeds-and-feeds chart and notice that doubling the bit diameter roughly halves the recommended rpm. Why would a half-inch bit and a tiny one-eighth bit ever want such different speeds in the same aluminum?

Show the thinking

What actually matters is how fast the cutting edge sweeps through the metal, the surface speed, and that depends on both rpm and how far the edge sits from the center. The rim of a half-inch bit travels four times as far per turn as the rim of a one-eighth bit, so at the same rpm the big bit's edge is moving four times faster and would overheat. To keep the edge speed in the range aluminum likes, you slow the big bit down and let the small one spin fast. It is the same reason the outside of a bike wheel covers more ground per spin than a point near the hub.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
You drill steel at the same high speed you'd use for wood. What happens?
Harder materials need slower speeds. Too fast generates heat that dulls the bit and can work-harden the steel, making it even tougher to drill. Slow down for steel.
Question 2
Polycarbonate (clear plastic) drilled too fast tends to…
Plastic has a low melting point, so excess speed and heat melt it. It re-welds behind the bit (webbing) and can grab. Moderate speed and steady feed keep it clean.
Question 3
Before drilling a part on the drill press, you should…
Always clamp. If the bit grabs an unclamped part, it spins it into a blur right where your hand is. Clamping makes the tool fight the clamp instead of you.

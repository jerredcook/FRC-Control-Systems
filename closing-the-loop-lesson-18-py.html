Predict first
You run the ramp test, then fit a straight line through the dots of voltage against velocity. What do the line's slope and intercept tell you?
On a slow ramp the mechanism is barely accelerating, so kA drops out and voltage is mostly kV times velocity plus a fixed offset. That makes the fit a straight line: its slope is kV and its intercept, the voltage at zero speed, is kS plus kG. Tuner X fits exactly this line across hundreds of samples. The code

A SysId routine in Phoenix 6

The routine drives the motor with a plain VoltageOut request while SignalLogger captures position, velocity, and voltage in the background. You bind four commands - quasistatic and dynamic, each direction - and run them on the real robot:

A Phoenix 6 SysId routine Python
from wpilib.sysid import SysIdRoutine, SysIdRoutineLog
from phoenix6 import SignalLogger
from phoenix6.controls import VoltageOut
from wpimath.units import volts

self.sys_id_req = VoltageOut(0)

self.routine = SysIdRoutine(
    SysIdRoutine.Config(
        stepVoltage=4.0,                                  # step voltage for the dynamic test
        recordState=lambda state: SignalLogger.write_string(
            "sysid-state", SysIdRoutineLog.stateEnumToString(state)),
    ),
    SysIdRoutine.Mechanism(
        lambda v: self.motor.set_control(self.sys_id_req.with_output(v)),  # drive the motor
        lambda log: None,                                # SignalLogger already records the signals
        self,
    ),
)

def quasistatic(self, direction):
    return self.routine.quasistatic(direction)

def dynamic(self, direction):
    return self.routine.dynamic(direction)

Call SignalLogger.start(), run the four commands, then open the .hoot log in Tuner X and let its SysId tool fit the gains. The output drops straight into the slot0 you built two lessons ago:

Going further · optional

SysId offers two test shapes, and the names tell you why. The quasistatic ramp creeps up so slowly that acceleration is near zero, which isolates kS and kV cleanly, like riding a bike at a steady crawl to feel exactly how hard you must pedal to hold each speed. The dynamic step slams full voltage so the mechanism accelerates hard, and that burst is what exposes kA. Worth chewing on: why can't a slow ramp ever measure kA well, no matter how careful you are?

Show the thinking

kA is the volts needed per unit of acceleration, so the only way to see it in the data is to make the mechanism accelerate. A slow ramp holds acceleration near zero on purpose, so the kA term contributes almost nothing to the voltage and the fit has nothing to grab onto. Pushing the ramp faster to force some acceleration turns it into a step test, so the clean answer is to let the dynamic step do that job. This is why both tests exist: the ramp pins down kS and kV at near-zero acceleration, and the step adds the hard acceleration that reveals kA.

Question 1
What is SysId actually measuring?
SysId runs a ramp and a step, then fits the voltage/velocity/acceleration data to recover kS, kV, kA, and (for gravity-loaded mechanisms) kG - the model of how your hardware turns voltage into motion.
Question 2
How is the test data captured on Phoenix 6?
SignalLogger streams every device signal to a .hoot log. Tuner X opens that log, runs the line-fit you just did by hand, and reports the gains - no manual graphing.
Question 3
Once SysId gives you kS, kV, kA, kG, where do they go?
The feedforward gains go straight into slot0. With them in place, the onboard loop already does most of the work, so you only need a modest kP (and maybe kD) to clean up tracking.

Part 7 · 01 Seeing & Thinking
Vision & AprilTags

Give the robot
eyes.

A camera spots AprilTags, the printed markers scattered around the field, and hands your code a simple answer: which tag, and how far off-center it is. From that, the robot can aim and even locate itself.

The hard part runs on a separate computer.

A coprocessor, a Raspberry Pi running PhotonVision, or a Limelight, does the heavy image processing and publishes the results over NetworkTables. Your roboRIO just reads them: the tag's ID, its yaw (how far left or right of center, called tx), its pitch, and how big it appears. Pan the camera and watch the numbers.

Predict first
Before you pan it: you slide the Pan camera control until the AprilTag sits right on the center crosshair. What does the tx (yaw) readout show at that moment?
tx is the angle from the camera's center to the target, so centering the tag drives tx toward 0. That is exactly the error you will turn to zero with a PID next lesson. Pan the camera and watch tx shrink to 0 as the tag crosses the crosshair, then grow again as you pan past it.

In code

Read the target off the camera

You ask the camera for its latest result, check whether it sees anything, then pull the numbers off the best target:

VisionSubsystem Java · PhotonVision
PhotonCamera camera = new PhotonCamera("frontCam");

var result = camera.getLatestResult();
if (result.hasTargets()) {
  var target = result.getBestTarget();
  int    id   = target.getFiducialId();   // which AprilTag
  double tx   = target.getYaw();          // degrees left/right of center
  double ty   = target.getPitch();        // degrees up/down
  double area = target.getArea();         // how big it looks (closeness)
}

// Limelight is the same idea over NetworkTables: tx, ty, tv, tid
An AprilTag is a known address on the field

Each tag has a printed ID and a published, fixed location on the field. So seeing tag 7 isn't just "a target over there", it's a survey marker the robot can trust. That's what makes the next two lessons possible: aim at a tag, and use its known spot to correct your pose. The roboRIO never touches a pixel; it just reads tx, ty, and id.

Going further · optional

The best target is whichever tag is most centered, but that is not always the one you want. If two tags are in view and you only trust tag 7, filter the results yourself: loop over result.getTargets() and pick the one whose getFiducialId() matches, instead of taking getBestTarget() blindly. Here is a question to chew on: what should your code do for the split second when the camera sees no tag at all, hold the last reading, or treat it as "no answer"?

Show the thinking

Treat it as "no answer" and stop acting on vision. Check result.hasTargets() every loop, and when it is false, fall back to a safe default like holding still or driving on the odometry pose instead of the camera. Holding the last reading is dangerous, because a tag that left view a second ago may now be in a completely different spot, and your robot would confidently aim at where it used to be. The honest move is to report "no lock" and let the rest of the robot decide what to do without vision.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What is an AprilTag?
AprilTags are fiducial markers placed at surveyed spots on the field. Detecting one gives you both its ID and a trusted reference point to aim at or locate from.
Question 2
Where does the actual image processing happen?
A dedicated coprocessor does the vision work and sends simple numbers (tx, ty, id) over NetworkTables. The roboRIO just reads them, keeping its loop fast.
Question 3
What does tx (the target's yaw) represent?
tx is the horizontal angle from the camera's centerline to the target. Drive it to zero and the robot is pointed straight at the tag, which is exactly the next lesson.

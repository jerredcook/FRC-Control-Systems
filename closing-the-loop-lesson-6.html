Lesson 06 Modeling
Feedforward

Knowing the
answer in advance.

PID reacts to error. Feedforward predicts the voltage a move needs before any error even appears.

Every controller so far has been reactive.

PID waits for an error, then responds. That's powerful, but it's always a step behind - it can only correct a mistake after the mistake happens. Feedforward flips it around: if you know the physics of your mechanism, you can compute most of the voltage a move needs in advance, before any error shows up.

Take gravity. Your elevator needs the same voltage just to hang in place, every time. Why make the controller rediscover that through accumulated error on every move, when you can tell it the answer up front?

voltage = kS·sign(v) + kG + kV·v + kA·a a model of exactly what voltage each part of a move requires

Four terms, each a piece of physics: kS the static friction to break free and start moving (the sign(v) beside it just means +1 when moving one way and −1 the other, since friction always pushes back against whichever way you're going), kG the constant pull of gravity, kV the voltage to sustain a given speed, and kA the extra to accelerate the mass. Feedforward computes this predicted voltage from where you want to go, and leaves PID to mop up only the small leftover - disturbances, a sagging battery, a game piece you didn't model.

Why it works

Feedforward does the heavy lifting

Remember the droop from Lesson 2 - the gap pure P couldn't close because it needed leftover error to fight gravity? And how Lesson 3's integral erased it, slowly, by accumulating that error over time? Feedforward erases it a third way: instantly, and in advance. kG hands the motor exactly the gravity-holding voltage from the first instant, so there's nothing for the error to compensate for. No droop, no waiting, no windup to manage.

Watch the feedforward readout: with feedforward on it sits at the gravity-holding voltage the entire time, doing the steady work - while PID's contribution stays near zero unless something disturbs the carriage.

Feedforward + feedback, together

Feedforward isn't a replacement for PID - it's a head start. The predicted voltage gets the mechanism most of the way; PID cleans up the rest: friction you under-modeled, a battery sagging, an unexpected load. A strong feedforward with a light PID on top is the gold standard for nearly every FRC mechanism - and exactly the recipe your final lessons build toward.

In Java

Predict, then correct

By hand, feedforward is one term added before the feedback. For a simple hold, that term is just kG:

Feedforward + feedback, by hand Java
double kG = 0.45;   // volts needed just to hold against gravity

public void periodic() {
  double error = setpoint - encoder.getPosition();
  double feedforward = kG;             // predict the constant gravity load
  double feedback    = kP * error;     // PID corrects only the leftover
  motor.setVoltage(feedforward + feedback);
}

WPILib's ElevatorFeedforward packages all four physics terms. You pair it with a plain PID and add their outputs:

Feedforward + PID, with WPILib Java
private final ElevatorFeedforward ff = new ElevatorFeedforward(kS, kG, kV, kA);
private final PIDController pid = new PIDController(kP, 0.0, kD);

public void periodic() {
  double ffVolts  = ff.calculate(velocity, nextVelocity);   // discrete form, for a moving profile
  double pidVolts = pid.calculate(encoder.getPosition(), setpoint);
  motor.setVoltage(ffVolts + pidVolts);
}
Under the hood - where kV and kA come alive

For a static hold, feed velocity 0 and feedforward returns just kG. The velocity and acceleration terms only earn their keep when the setpoint is actually moving - fed a planned trajectory. The single-argument calculate(velocity) gives the steady-state voltage to hold a speed; to follow a setpoint that is actively moving, newer WPILib also offers the discrete calculate(currentVelocity, nextVelocity), which accounts for the change between loops. Generating that smooth moving trajectory is motion profiling, which we reach at the end of the course - where kV and kA finally shine.

Going further · optional

Gravity feedforward for an elevator is one constant kG, but think about an arm instead of an elevator: an arm hanging straight down needs almost no holding voltage, while an arm held straight out sideways needs the most, and at any angle in between it needs something proportional to how far it is tipped over. So an arm's gravity term is not a constant - it scales with the cosine of the arm angle, which is exactly why WPILib has a separate ArmFeedforward whose kG gets multiplied by cos(angle). Here is the question to sit with: why does a leaning bookshelf push hardest against your hand when it is halfway to falling, not when it has barely started to tip?

Show the thinking

Gravity's turning effect is the weight times the horizontal distance from the pivot out to the center of mass, not the full weight. Standing nearly upright, the shelf's center of mass sits almost over its base, so that horizontal distance is tiny and it barely leans on your hand. As it tips, the center of mass swings out to the side and that horizontal lever arm grows, so the shelf pushes harder the farther it leans past upright, reaching the most when its mass hangs straight out to the side. That same geometry is why an arm's gravity term follows cos(angle), since cosine measures how far the mass sticks out sideways from the pivot.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does feedforward do that feedback can't?
Feedback only reacts to error after it appears. Feedforward uses a model of the mechanism to supply most of the voltage up front, so the error never grows large in the first place.
Question 2
With correct kG and no integral at all, why does the elevator hold with zero steady-state error?
In Lesson 2 the droop existed because P needed error to generate the holding voltage. Feedforward provides that voltage outright, so the error can sit at zero - no integral required.
Question 3
Which feedforward terms only matter when the setpoint is moving?
kG (gravity) and kS (static friction at the start) apply even for a hold. kV and kA respond to commanded speed and acceleration, so they need a moving trajectory to act on - which is what motion profiling provides.

Proportional Control

How hard,
exactly?

One number turns "push harder when you're far" into a controller you can actually write.

Last lesson ended on a question.

You ran a feedback loop with your own hand and watched a controller "push harder when the gap is bigger." But how much harder? Proportional control gives the simplest possible answer: make the push directly proportional to the error.

output = kP × error where error = setpoint − measurement

That's the whole idea. kP is the gain - the dial that sets how aggressively the controller reacts. A big kP slams the motor at the smallest gap; a small kP nudges gently. Everything else this lesson is just consequences of that one choice.

The catch

It never quite arrives

Did you notice the carriage settles below the target? Turn kP down to 1 and the gap is enormous; turn it up and the gap shrinks but stubbornly refuses to close. This isn't a bug - it's the defining limitation of proportional control, and the reason is worth understanding completely.

To hold the carriage up, the motor has to keep pushing against gravity - forever. But with proportional control, the only way to get a push is to have some error. Zero error would mean zero output, and the carriage would fall. So it settles at exactly the height where the leftover error produces just enough voltage to balance gravity. That permanent gap is called steady-state error, or droop.

Watch the voltage trace: it settles at the gravity-holding value, not at zero. The droop is the price of that hold. Mathematically it's exactly g / kP - bigger gain shrinks it, but you'll pay for that gain in overshoot.

You can't win with P alone

Small kP → big droop. Big kP → overshoot and oscillation. There is no single gain that's both tight and smooth. That exact tension is why the next two lessons exist.

In Python

Your first controller

Everything you just watched is three lines of arithmetic, run about fifty times a second inside the subsystem's periodic() method:

Proportional control, by hand Python
# runs ~50x per second inside the subsystem's periodic() method
def periodic(self):
    setpoint    = 1.20                       # target height, meters
    measurement = self.encoder.getPosition()
    error       = setpoint - measurement       # the gap

    kP     = 4.0                            # gain - volts per meter of error
    output = kP * error                        # push harder when the gap is bigger

    self.motor.setVoltage(output)

Read it top to bottom: ask the encoder where the carriage is, subtract that from where you want it to get the error, multiply by the gain, and send the result to the motor as a voltage. That's a complete proportional controller. The whole loop is "measure, compare, act" - exactly the shape from Lesson 1.

You won't hand-roll this forever, though. WPILib ships a PIDController that does the same arithmetic - you give it the gains and call calculate:

The same thing, with WPILib Python
from wpimath.controller import PIDController

# kP = 4.0, then kI = 0.0 and kD = 0.0 - pure proportional, for now
self.controller = PIDController(4.0, 0.0, 0.0)

def periodic(self):
    output = self.controller.calculate(self.encoder.getPosition(), 1.20)
    self.motor.setVoltage(output)

The constructor takes the three gains in order - kP, kI, kD. We're leaving the last two at zero because they're the next two lessons. calculate(measurement, setpoint) returns the same kP × error you wrote by hand. Real robot code also clamps that output to the battery's reach (about ±12 V) and only drives the motor when the robot is enabled, guard rails we leave off here to keep the loop bare.

Under the hood - gains have units

Because output is in volts and error is in meters, kP is volts per meter. That's why a gain that works on one mechanism won't transfer to another - and why guessing gains gets old fast. Lesson 7 (system identification) is how you measure the right numbers instead of fishing for them.

Going further · optional

The lesson says droop equals g / kP, but notice that depends on gravity being a constant pull. Try this thought experiment: what is the steady-state error of a pure-P controller driving a flywheel up to a target speed with no gravity and only friction? The answer is not zero, because friction still demands some voltage to hold speed, so P still needs leftover error to make it. The deeper rule is that pure proportional control leaves an offset whenever the system needs a nonzero output to sit still, which control theory calls a type-0 system.

Show the thinking

It is not zero. With no gravity but real friction, the flywheel still needs some steady voltage to hold its target speed against drag, and a pure-P controller only makes voltage out of error, so it has to sit at a small leftover error to produce that holding voltage. The error shrinks as you raise kP but never reaches zero, which is the type-0 offset the lesson describes. Adding an integral term is what finally drives it to zero, because the integral can supply that holding voltage on its own.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
With pure proportional control, raising kP will…
Droop is g/kP - a bigger gain makes it smaller, but it only reaches zero if kP were infinite (which would make the system wildly unstable). P alone can't fully close the gap.
Question 2
Why does the elevator settle below the target under proportional control?
Output is kP × error. Holding against gravity needs a nonzero voltage, which needs a nonzero error - so the carriage parks exactly where the leftover error supplies the holding voltage.
Question 3
You push kP very high and the carriage overshoots and oscillates. This shows…
Exactly the proportional bind: you can dial out the droop or dial out the overshoot, but not both with kP alone. The fix is adding other terms, which is where we're headed.

Integral control

Proportional parks below the line forever. The Integral term gives the controller a memory - it adds up the error over time and keeps pushing until the gap is truly gone. You'll meet kI and watch the droop disappear.

Brackets, Gussets & Joining

One bolt is a
hinge.

Tubes and plates are just parts until they're joined into a rigid frame. A gusset ties two tubes at a corner, but only if it's bolted right. One bolt lets the joint pivot; spread two or more and it locks solid.

In the shop

How a frame comes together

Tubes are joined with gussets and brackets, flat plates that span a corner and bolt into both pieces. A few rules make the difference between a rigid robot and a wobbly one:

📐
GussetA plate over a joint that ties two tubes together and spreads the load.
🔩
Two bolts minimumPer tube, spread apart, so the joint can't pivot.
🪛
Tapped vs throughTap threads into the tube, or run a bolt through to a nut on the back.
⚙️
Rivets & nutsertsFor thin walls or when you can't reach the back of the tube.
Triangles don't move

The strongest frames are built from triangles. A four-sided box can rack (lean over into a parallelogram), but add a diagonal and it's locked, because a triangle can't change shape without its sides changing length. When a frame feels flexy, the fix is almost always a gusset or a diagonal that turns a wobbly rectangle into a rigid triangle.

Going further · optional

Two bolts stop a tube from pivoting, but how far apart they sit matters as much as how many there are. Spread the same two bolts farther apart and each one carries less force when the joint is twisted, the same reason a wider stance helps you resist being pushed over. A design challenge: given a fixed gusset and four bolt holes, where would you place your bolts to fight a twisting load best, bunched near the corner or pushed out toward the ends of each tube? Think about which placement gives the longest lever between the bolts.

Show the thinking

Push the bolts out toward the ends of the tube, as far apart as the gusset allows. A twisting load tries to rotate the tube, and the bolts resist by pulling against each other across that gap, so a wider gap is a longer lever and each bolt carries less force for the same twist. Bunched near the corner, the bolts sit on a short lever and have to fight much harder, so they loosen or tear out sooner. It is the same reason a wide stance is harder to tip over than feet close together.

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Why does a tube need at least two bolts into a gusset, not one?
A single bolt is a pivot point, the tube can swing around it. Two bolts spread apart fix the angle, so the joint stays rigid under load.
Question 2
What does a gusset do?
A gusset is a plate bolted across a corner. It connects the two tubes and distributes the load across several bolts so the joint stays stiff.
Question 3
A square frame leans over into a parallelogram under load. The fix?
A rectangle can rack into a parallelogram, but a triangle can't change shape without changing a side's length. A diagonal brace or corner gusset locks the frame.

Encoders & Position

Raw counts to
real units.

An encoder tells you how much the motor turned. A gear ratio and a little geometry turn that into the inches and degrees your code actually reasons about.

The motor spins fast; the mechanism moves slow.

An encoder counts rotations of the motor shaft. But your code cares about how high the elevator is, or what angle the arm points, not how many times a motor spun. A gearbox sits in between, so you divide by the gear ratio to get mechanism rotations, then multiply by the geometry (a drum's circumference, or 360 degrees) to get real units. Turn the motor and watch the whole chain.

Predict first
Before you run it: you hold the motor rotations fixed and drag the gear ratio higher (say 10:1 up to 40:1). What happens to the height in inches?
You divide motor rotations by the gear ratio, so a bigger ratio gives fewer mechanism rotations from the same motor turns, and the height shrinks. A high ratio trades speed for finer, stronger motion. Run it, hold the motor rotations, and drag the ratio up to watch the height drop.

In code

Let the config do the division

You could divide by the gear ratio everywhere by hand, or tell the controller about it once. In Phoenix 6, sensor_to_mechanism_ratio bakes the reduction in, so get_position() already reports mechanism rotations:

Reading position in real units Python · phoenix6
import math
from phoenix6.configs import TalonFXConfiguration

cfg = TalonFXConfiguration()
cfg.feedback.sensor_to_mechanism_ratio = 10.0   # 10 motor turns = 1 mechanism turn
motor.configurator.apply(cfg)

# get_position().value is now in MECHANISM rotations, already divided by 10
mech_rotations = motor.get_position().value
height_inches = mech_rotations * (math.pi * drum_diameter_inches)

# define "zero" wherever the mechanism sits right now
motor.set_position(0)
Position and velocity both convert

Once sensor_to_mechanism_ratio is set, get_velocity() comes back in mechanism rotations per second too, and so do the gains and Motion Magic targets you saw in the Control course. Set the ratio once and every number downstream speaks the mechanism's language instead of the motor's. That one config line removes a whole category of unit bugs.

Going further · optional

A motor's built-in encoder counts turns of the motor, so it only knows the mechanism's position if the gearbox never slips and you tell it where zero is at startup. That is why arms often add a second sensor, an absolute encoder on the mechanism itself, that reports the true angle even right after a power cycle. Question to sit with: if you call set_position(0) at boot but the arm was resting at 30 degrees, what does your code now believe, and how could an absolute encoder seed the right starting value instead?

Show the thinking

Your code now believes the arm is at 0 when it is really at 30 degrees, so every reading is off by 30 and any move to a setpoint lands 30 degrees low, which can drive the arm into a hard stop. The relative encoder only counts change from wherever you called zero, so it has no way to know the true starting angle on its own. An absolute encoder fixes this because it reports the real mechanism angle even right after power-on. At boot you read the absolute encoder, convert its 30 degrees into the matching motor-encoder count, and call set_position with that value instead of 0, so the relative encoder starts out agreeing with reality.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
The raw encoder reads 40 motor rotations through a 10:1 gearbox. How many times did the mechanism turn?
The gearbox reduces 10 motor turns into 1 mechanism turn, so 40 / 10 = 4 mechanism rotations. Mechanism rotations = motor rotations ÷ gear ratio.
Question 2
What does sensor_to_mechanism_ratio do for you?
It tells Phoenix 6 the gear ratio once, so position and velocity come back already converted to mechanism rotations. No more dividing by hand everywhere you read the sensor.
Question 3
To turn mechanism rotations into linear inches on an elevator, you multiply by…
Each rotation of the drum pulls in one circumference of cable, so inches = mechanism rotations × (π × drum diameter). Geometry is the last step from rotations to real-world distance.

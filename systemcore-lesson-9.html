What carries over,
and getting ready.

Here is the reassuring truth to end on: most of what this academy taught you still holds in 2027. The board, the wiring, and the Driver Station change, but your fundamentals travel with you. Let us sort what stays from what shifts, then plan how to get ready.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

Most of your toolbox travels with you to 2027.

When a tool you depend on gets replaced, the first question is always: how much do I have to relearn? For SystemCore the answer is, happily, not much. The core skills, the theory, the code structure, the mechanisms, all carry straight over. Think of it like swapping the frame on your bike: the way you pedal, brake, and steer is the same, even though the frame underneath is new.

So what actually changes?

The biggest shifts are the board itself, the wiring, the now built-in radio, vision, and IMU, CAN FD on the bus, the new cross-platform Driver Station, real-time Linux underneath, MotionCore, and changes to power distribution that are still being finalized. Real changes, but they sit underneath the skills you already have. You are learning a new frame, not learning to ride all over again.

A few things genuinely go away

Some older tools do not make the jump to 2027. LabVIEW is not supported on SystemCore. The Shuffleboard and SmartDashboard dashboards, PathWeaver, and RobotBuilder are removed, you would use Elastic or AdvantageScope for dashboards, and PathPlanner or Choreo for paths. And in Java the library packages get renamed from edu.wpi.first to org.wpilib; the project importer updates that for you. None of these change the ideas you learned, they are tool swaps.

Going further · optional

Notice the pattern across this course: the things that change are mostly hardware and tooling, while the things that stay are the ideas, control theory, program structure, how a mechanism is built. That is true far beyond robotics. Here is one to think through with a mentor or your team: when the next big platform change lands, after SystemCore, which of your skills do you expect to carry over, and how would you spot that in advance?

Show the thinking

The skills that carry over are the ones about ideas rather than a specific board or app: understanding control theory, structuring a program clearly, reading documentation, debugging, and knowing how a mechanism should move. What tends to change is the exact hardware, the wiring, and the names of buttons in the tools. A useful way to spot the difference in advance is to ask whether a skill is about why something works or only about where to click. If it explains a reason, it usually survives the next change, and a platform new enough to still be in alpha is exactly when leaning on those durable ideas pays off.

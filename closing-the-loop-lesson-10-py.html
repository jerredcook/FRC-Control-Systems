Units, Gears & Conversions

Rotations in,
meters out.

Your motor counts in turns. Your elevator moves in meters. The gearbox sits in between - and getting this conversion wrong is the single most common real-robot bug.

A motor doesn't know what a meter is.

A TalonFX reports one thing: how many times its shaft has spun - rotations. But you think in meters (elevator height) or degrees (arm angle). Two things stand between the motor and the mechanism, and you have to account for both:

gear ratio - the gearbox spins the motor many times per one output turn
circumference - one output turn pays out one drum's worth of cable

Miss either one and your robot will think it's at 2 meters when it's at 20 centimeters. Let's make the conversion something you can see.

See it

From motor turns to height

Drag motor rotations and watch the chain: the gearbox divides the turns down, the drum turns those into cable, and the carriage rises. Then change the gear ratio and the drum size and watch how many motor turns one meter now costs.

Predict first
Before you run it: you raise the gear ratio from 9:1 toward 20:1 and keep the drum the same size. How many motor rotations does one meter of height now take?
The gearbox divides motor turns down before the drum, so a bigger ratio means the motor must spin more times for each output turn, and each output turn still pays out one drum circumference. More motor turns per meter buys finer control but a slower mechanism. Run it, drag the gear ratio up, and watch the motor rotations needed for the same height climb.

A bigger gear ratio means more motor turns per meter - finer control, but a slower mechanism. This trade-off is yours to choose when you design the robot.

The math, once

The conversion chain

Every position conversion is the same two steps. Motor rotations divide by the gear ratio to get output rotations; output rotations multiply by the drum's circumference to get distance:

meters = ( motor_rotations ÷ G ) × π · diameter G = gear ratio  ·  circumference = π · diameter (use meters!)

To go the other way - turning a target height into the motor rotations to command - you just run it backwards: multiply by G, divide by circumference. An arm is even simpler: there's no drum, so output rotations convert straight to degrees (× 360).

The classic bug

Mixing units. A drum diameter in inches with a height in meters, or radians where the code wants rotations. Pick one unit system, convert everything to it once at the boundary, and never mix. Inches → meters: multiply by 0.0254.

In Phoenix 6

Let the motor do the conversion

You could scatter / G * circumference math all over your code - or you can tell the TalonFX the ratio once, and then ask it for position in mechanism units directly. Phoenix 6 has a config field for exactly this:

Configure the ratio once Python · Phoenix 6
cfg = TalonFXConfiguration()
cfg.feedback.sensor_to_mechanism_ratio = 9.0   # gear ratio: 9 motor turns per output turn
motor.configurator.apply(cfg)

# now get_position() returns OUTPUT rotations, gearbox already removed:
output_rotations = motor.get_position().value
meters = output_rotations * (math.pi * drum_diameter_meters)

With the ratio configured, the gearbox disappears from your day-to-day code - the motor reports output turns, and you only have the drum left to handle. One source of bugs, gone.

Going further · optional

A real edge case: a winch that wraps cable in layers, like wrapping string around a pencil until it gets fatter. As cable piles up on the drum, the effective diameter grows, so one turn pays out more cable near the top than at the bottom and a single fixed circumference is slightly wrong. Most teams accept the small error, but some model the changing radius. Can you reason out whether this makes the elevator read a bit high or a bit low as it climbs?

Show the thinking

It reads a bit low as it climbs. The math multiplies turns by a fixed circumference, but once cable layers build up the drum is fatter than that, so each turn actually pays out more cable than the formula credits. The true height climbs faster than the computed number, so the reading lags behind the real position. The fix, if you care about the small error, is to model the radius growing with the number of wraps.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
The motor reports 36 rotations through a 12 : 1 gearbox. How many output rotations is that?
3. The gearbox spins the motor 12 times for every 1 output turn, so you divide: 36 ÷ 12 = 3 output rotations.
Question 2
To turn output rotations into a linear distance, you multiply by…
One full output turn pays out exactly one circumference of cable, so distance = output rotations × (π × diameter). Just keep the diameter in the same units you want the distance in.
Question 3
What does sensor_to_mechanism_ratio do in Phoenix 6?
You give the TalonFX the gear ratio once, and from then on its position and velocity come back in mechanism (output) units - so you don't divide by the gear ratio by hand all over your code.

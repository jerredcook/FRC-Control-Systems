Gyros & the IMU

Which way is
forward?

A gyro gives the robot a sense of direction, the heading. It is the one number that lets "push the stick away from you" always mean "go down-field," no matter which way the robot is spun.

An encoder measures a mechanism. A gyro measures the whole robot.

An IMU (a Pigeon 2 or a navX) reports the robot's yaw: how far it has rotated, in degrees. Two things trip people up. The reading is continuous, so it keeps climbing past 360 as you keep spinning, and it only means something relative to a zero you choose. Spin the robot and watch both readings move.

Predict first
Before you run it: you hold the robot spinning one way and keep going well past 360 degrees. What does the continuous getYaw() reading do?
getYaw() is continuous, so it keeps adding up every degree you turn and never wraps, which is why the full-turns counter climbs. The wrapped -180..180 reading is the one that folds back around. Run it, hold the spin, and watch continuous keep growing while the wrapped value snaps from 180 to -180.

In code

Read the heading, set the zero

An IMU lives on the CAN bus like a motor. You read its yaw, often as a Rotation2d for kinematics, and you zero it at the start of a match to define down-field:

Pigeon 2 Python · phoenix6
from phoenix6.hardware import Pigeon2

gyro = Pigeon2(20)

heading_deg = gyro.get_yaw().value          # continuous, CCW positive
heading = gyro.get_rotation2d()              # for kinematics / field-relative

# define "down-field" as zero at the start of the match
gyro.set_yaw(0)

# navX is the same idea, just a different class:
# from navx import AHRS; navx = AHRS(AHRS.NavXComType.kMXP_SPI); navx.getRotation2d()
Continuous vs wrapped: let the math handle it

Raw yaw climbs forever (370°, 720°...), which is perfect for tracking total rotation. When you need "am I pointing at 90°?", use a Rotation2d; its trig wraps the angle for you, so 370° and 10° compare as equal. Don't hand-roll angle subtraction and forget the wrap, that is a classic source of a drivetrain that spins the long way around.

Going further · optional

Most IMUs read yaw, pitch, and roll. After zeroing on flat ground, try logging pitch and roll to NetworkTables, then drive partway up a ramp and watch them change. Teams use that exact reading to auto-balance on a charge station: feed the tilt angle into a controller that drives the robot until the angle returns to zero. What heading, pitch, or roll value would mean "perfectly level"?

Show the thinking

Level means pitch and roll are both 0, since those measure tilt away from flat. Heading (yaw) does not matter for level, because the robot can sit flat at any heading by spinning in place on flat ground. So an auto-balance controller drives pitch and roll back toward 0, and a small deadband of a degree or two around 0 keeps the robot from twitching back and forth forever once it is close enough.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does a gyro / IMU measure?
An IMU senses rotation, giving you yaw (heading). Encoders handle "how far did this mechanism move"; the gyro handles "which way is the robot facing."
Question 2
You spin the robot two and a half times to the left. What does the continuous get_yaw() read (CCW positive)?
Continuous yaw accumulates: 2.5 turns × 360 = 900°. That's great for counting total rotation. If you want it folded into -180..180, use the wrapped form or a Rotation2d.
Question 3
Field-relative driving ("forward is always down-field") depends on what?
Field-relative drive takes your joystick command and rotates it by the negative of the robot's heading, so "away from you" maps to down-field no matter how the robot is turned. No gyro, no field-relative.

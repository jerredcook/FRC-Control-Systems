Design for Manufacture

Design what
you can build.

A perfect CAD model is worthless if your shop can't make the part. Design for manufacture, DFM, means making choices that fit your real tools and skills: standard stock, sensible tolerances, and simple shapes. The best design is the one that actually gets built well, on time.

Which one is easier to make?

Two designs can do the same job, but one drops onto the bandsaw in five minutes and the other needs a CNC mill and a perfect setup. For each pair below, pick the version your shop will thank you for.

The easy-to-make choice usually wins twice: it gets built faster and it's easier to fix or replace at competition. Designing for your shop is designing for reliability.

In the shop

What makes a part easy to make

DFM is mostly a handful of habits that keep parts on the simple side:

📐
Standard stockDesign around tube and plate sizes you already buy.
🕳️
Holes on a gridLine up holes to a pattern so they're fast and repeatable to drill.
📏
Sensible tolerancesOnly ask for tight precision where it truly matters.
🟦
Simple shapesFlat plates and straight cuts beat complex 3D forms.
Design for the shop you actually have

A part that needs a five-axis mill is useless if all you own is a drill press and a bandsaw. Great FRC designers know exactly what their team can make, and they design right up to that edge and no further. Every unnecessary tight tolerance, oddball angle, or custom thickness is time you don't have in a six-week season, and a part that's harder to remake when it breaks at an event. Simple, standard, and buildable wins.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does "design for manufacture" mean?
DFM means choosing geometry, materials, and tolerances that fit your real tools and time, so the part gets built well and on schedule.
Question 2
When should you call for a very tight tolerance?
Tight tolerances are slow and hard to hit. Reserve them for the few spots that need them and leave everything else generous.
Question 3
Why design around standard stock and simple shapes?
Standard, simple parts cut fast, fit the tools you have, and can be remade quickly at an event, which is reliability you can build.

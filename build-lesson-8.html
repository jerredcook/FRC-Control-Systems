COTS vs Custom

Buy it, or
build it?

You don't fabricate everything from scratch. Vendors sell proven parts off the shelf, and the kit of parts gives you a head start. The skill is knowing which parts to buy and which to make yourself.

Buy the hard stuff. Build the unique stuff.

A part that's standard, high-stress, or precision is usually better bought, someone has already perfected it. A part that's specific to your robot has to be custom. Decide for each part below: COTS (commercial off-the-shelf) or custom-built?

The rookie trap: building everything

New teams often try to machine every part to save money, and run out of time before the robot works. The opposite trap is buying so much that nothing fits together. The healthy middle: buy the proven, high-stress, precision parts (gearboxes, swerve modules, wheels, bearings) so they just work, and spend your shop time on the parts unique to your design. Reliability you can buy is reliability you don't have to debug.

Going further · optional

The buy-versus-build call is not only about this part, it is about your team's whole schedule. Veteran teams think about the "critical path", the longest chain of tasks that must finish before the robot can drive, and they buy COTS for anything sitting on that path so a slow custom part never holds up the entire build. Here is one to weigh: a custom version might be lighter and fit better, but if it takes two extra weeks you may never test-drive it. How would you decide when "good and done now" beats "perfect and late"?

Show the thinking

A good answer weighs the part against the calendar, not against an ideal. Ask whether the part sits on the critical path: if a slow custom version delays driving and testing, "good and done now" almost always wins, because a robot you can practice with beats a perfect one you never drive. Save the custom, perfect-fit work for parts off the critical path or for the few places where the gain is large and you truly have the time. The honest tie-breaker is practice time, the team that drives sooner gets more reps, and reps win matches more often than a slightly lighter bracket.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What does COTS stand for?
COTS means commercial off-the-shelf: a part a vendor already makes and sells. You buy it ready to use instead of fabricating it yourself.
Question 2
Which kind of part is usually best to buy rather than build?
Gearboxes, swerve modules, wheels, and bearings are precision, high-stress parts that vendors have already perfected. Buying them means reliability you don't have to engineer yourself.
Question 3
When is custom fabrication the right call?
If no vendor sells what you need, because it's specific to your design or the game, you build it. Custom is for the parts that make your robot yours.

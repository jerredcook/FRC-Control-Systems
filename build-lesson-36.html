Planning the Season

Six weeks,
one robot.

Build season is a sprint with a hard deadline. The teams that do well aren't the ones who build the most, they're the ones who plan the time: design early, build in parallel, and above all leave room at the end to put it together and learn to drive it.

Spend the six weeks wisely.

You get roughly six weeks. Split them across design, fabricate, integrate (assembling and wiring everything together), and practice (driving and debugging). Slide the weeks and watch the plan get judged, the classic trap is spending all six on building.

A season plan is mostly about protecting the end of it:

🧩

🔗

🎮
Protect practiceA driver needs weeks, not days, to get good.
The robot that practices beats the robot that's fancier

The single most common rookie mistake is building right up to the deadline, then showing up with a robot nobody has driven. Subsystems that worked alone fight each other once combined, and a driver who's never practiced will fumble. A simpler robot with two weeks of driver practice routinely beats a fancier one finished the night before. Plan the season so the robot is done early enough to learn, that connects everything: the Control and Deploy courses only pay off if there's time to tune and practice with them.

Going further · optional

Building subsystems in parallel only saves time if you find the critical path, the longest chain of tasks that have to happen in order. Practice cannot start until the robot is integrated, integration cannot start until the slowest subsystem is done, so that slowest subsystem secretly sets your whole schedule. Pour extra people onto it and start the others a little later if you must. It is like a group bike ride where the whole team only moves as fast as the slowest rider: speeding up everyone else does not help until you help them. Which subsystem is on your critical path, and what would it take to shorten it?

Show the thinking

The answer depends on your robot, but the critical path is usually the most complex subsystem or the one whose parts arrive last, often the drivetrain, since nothing can be integrated or driven until it rolls. To find it, list each subsystem with how long it takes and what it waits on, then trace the longest chain that has to happen in order. You shorten it by attacking that chain only: order its parts first, put your most experienced people on it, simplify its design, or run a fallback version in parallel. Speeding up anything off the critical path buys you nothing.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What's the most common season-planning mistake?
A robot still being assembled in the final days never gets driven. Leaving no integration or practice time is the classic trap.
Question 2
Why build subsystems in parallel?
With a hard deadline, the drivetrain, intake, and arm teams have to work at the same time. Serial building runs out of weeks.
Question 3
Why protect driver practice time?
Driving well takes weeks of practice. The time to learn the robot is often worth more than another fancy mechanism.

Part 6 · 05 Electrical & Pneumatics
Pneumatics

Power with
air.

Not everything needs a motor. Compressed air gives you fast, strong, snap-action motion that holds its position with no current at all, perfect for on-off jobs like clamping a game piece or popping out a hook. It's a whole little system: compressor, tank, valve, cylinder.

Fill the tank, fire the cylinder.

A compressor squeezes air into a tank for storage. When you flip the solenoid valve, stored air rushes into the cylinder and shoves the piston out, fast and hard. Run the compressor to build pressure, then fire the cylinder and watch the tank drain and refill.

Compressed air is stored energy, treat it like a live wire

A charged tank stays dangerous even after the robot is switched off. Always bleed the tank down to zero pressure before you touch a fitting or a line. Keep fingers out of a cylinder's path, it snaps out hard enough to crush or pinch. Wear eye protection, and have a mentor check any air system before you charge it. FRC rules cap the working pressure at 60 psi, never run it higher than your team's regulator is set to.

Predict first
The tank starts at 0 psi. Before you run the compressor at all, you press Fire cylinder. What happens?
The cylinder is pushed by air stored in the tank, and an empty tank has none to give, so firing on 0 psi does almost nothing. Run the compressor first to fill the tank, then fire and watch the piston snap out, and notice each shot drains the tank a little. Each shot dumps pressure from the tank; the compressor builds it back up. Fire too fast with the compressor off and the tank runs dry and the cylinder gets weak, the same reason real robots watch their air supply during a match.

🎯 Missions clear them with the sim above
○Fill the tank to full (120 psi), switch the compressor off, then fire one shot. Watch the tank readout, each full-force shot costs about 6 psi.
○Keep the compressor off and keep firing until the tank is nearly empty. The piston stops snapping out and only crawls. No stored air, no push.
○Match play: get the tank into the 40 to 60 psi band, then land 6 shots without leaving it. Run the compressor in short bursts between shots to top back up.
★Gold: each shot really costs about 6.5 psi, so one full tank holds 12 shots before dipping under 40 psi. Prove it: fill to full, compressor off, land all 12, then stop firing while the tank still reads 40 or more.
In the shop

The air system, end to end

Pneumatics is its own little plumbing system bolted onto the robot. The pieces, in order of the airflow:

💨
CompressorSqueezes air to build pressure; runs until the tank is full.
🛢️
Tank + regulatorStores air and steps it down to a safe working pressure.
🎚️
Solenoid valveThe electric switch that sends air to extend or retract.
⬆️
CylinderAir pushes the piston; fast, strong, holds with no current.
When to reach for air instead of a motor

Pneumatics shines at binary jobs: all the way out or all the way in, clamp or release, up or down. It's snappy, it's strong, and once extended it holds position drawing zero power, where a motor would have to keep pulling current. The trade-off is you only get two positions and a limited air supply per match, so it's wrong for anything that needs to stop partway or move constantly. Pick air for the on-off actions and motors for everything that needs control, and on the code side a cylinder is just a DoubleSolenoid you set to kForward or kReverse.

🔧 In the shop · 10 minutes

With your mentor, confirm the robot is off and the air system is bled to zero, then trace the whole air path with one finger, naming each part out loud: compressor, tank, regulator, solenoid valve, cylinder. Pass test: every gauge reads 0 psi before you touch anything, and you can point to the regulator and say what working pressure it is set to. If you cannot name a piece or find the regulator, walk the path again with your mentor until you can do it solo, and never loosen a fitting unless the gauges read zero. No shop? Pump a bike tire to full pressure, then push the pump handle down again and feel the stored air shove it back, that shove is exactly what fires a cylinder.

Going further · optional

The cylinder you saw is single-acting if air pushes it one way and a spring returns it, but most FRC cylinders are double-acting: the solenoid sends air to either end, so air does the work in both directions and the piston holds firmly at each stop. Here's one to chew on with a mentor: a fatter cylinder pushes with more force at the same 60 psi (force is roughly pressure times piston area), but it also gulps more air per shot, so you get fewer firings before the tank sags. How would you pick a cylinder size for a clamp that has to fire fifty times in a match?

Show the thinking

Start from the force the clamp actually needs, then pick the smallest bore that delivers it at your working pressure, since force is roughly pressure times piston area. A smaller cylinder uses less air per shot, so it gives you more firings before the stored air sags, which matters a lot at fifty shots a match. If the smallest cylinder that meets the force still uses too much air, you can add storage tanks, raise the working pressure within the legal limit, or shorten the stroke, rather than jump to a fatter bore. The pick is a balance: enough force with margin, but no bigger than that, so you do not waste air you need to last the whole match.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What's the order of the pneumatic system?
The compressor builds pressure into the tank, the solenoid valve directs that stored air, and the cylinder turns it into motion. Airflow always runs in that order.
Question 2
What is pneumatics best suited for?
Air gives you two positions, out or in, with a strong snap and zero holding current. It's ideal for clamps and hooks, but wrong for anything needing to stop partway or move continuously.
Question 3
You keep firing the cylinder with the compressor off. What happens?
Each shot spends stored air. With no compressor refilling the tank, pressure drops and the cylinder loses force, which is why teams watch their air supply during a match.

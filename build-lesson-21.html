Shooters & End Effectors

Launch it home.

The end effector is the business end of the robot, the part that actually scores. A flywheel shooter spins a wheel up, feeds a game piece into it, and flings it across the field. Dial in the speed and angle, and put it in the goal.

Wheel speed plus launch angle equals a trajectory.

A flywheel shooter throws a game piece at roughly the wheel's surface speed: faster wheel, faster shot. The hood sets the launch angle. Together they decide where the piece lands. Set the RPM and angle below, watch the predicted arc, and shoot for the goal.

Predict first
Before you run it: you leave the launch angle alone and lower the Flywheel RPM. What happens to the predicted path, the dashed line showing where the piece will land?
A faster flywheel hands the piece more launch speed, so it flies farther. Lower the RPM and the piece leaves slower, so the arc lands shorter. The angle sets how high the arc climbs, but the RPM sets how far it carries. Run the sim, drop the RPM, and watch the dashed path pull in toward the robot.

The business end

Shooters are the flashiest end effector, but the family is bigger. What they share is that they're the part that finally does the scoring:

A shot is only as good as the RPM holding steady

Range depends directly on flywheel speed, so if the RPM sags between shots, every shot lands somewhere different. That's exactly the problem the Control course's flywheel lesson tackles: a velocity loop with strong feedforward (kV) that holds the wheel at a rock-steady RPM and recovers fast after each piece. The mechanical build gives you a shooter; the control loop is what makes it consistent. Building and coding meet here too.

Going further · optional

A flywheel does not slow the shot down for short range, it keeps spinning fast, so close-in shots need a higher hood angle to lob the piece instead. Here is one to chew on: if you add a second wheel above the piece spinning the opposite way, you can put heavy backspin on it, and backspin makes the shot far more forgiving on the goal. Why might a little backspin turn a hard bounce-out into a soft roll-in?

Show the thinking

Backspin means the top of the piece is rotating backward, toward the shooter, as it flies. When a piece with backspin hits the far rim or the back wall of the goal, the spin grabs that surface and kills forward energy instead of springing off it, so a shot that would have bounced straight back out drops down and rolls in. The spin also makes the flight more consistent by smoothing out air effects, so the piece lands in nearly the same place shot after shot. It widens the margin for error: the piece can come in a little hard or a little off and still deaden against the goal rather than rejecting, which is why so many top shooters run a counter-rotating top wheel to add backspin.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
How does a flywheel shooter launch a game piece?
The spinning flywheel contacts the piece and accelerates it to near the wheel's surface speed, then releases it. Faster wheel, faster and farther the shot.
Question 2
What two things together determine where a shot lands?
Like any projectile, the trajectory comes from how fast it leaves (RPM) and at what angle (the hood). Tune both to put the piece in the goal.
Question 3
Why does a shooter need its flywheel RPM held steady?
Shot distance is set by exit speed. If the RPM dips after feeding a piece, the next shot goes short. A velocity control loop with feedforward keeps it steady, the Control course's flywheel lesson.

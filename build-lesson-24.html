Wheels & Traction

Push without
the spin.

How hard a robot can push or accelerate isn't set by its motors, it's set by its grip. Add all the power you want; past the traction limit the wheels just spin. Traction comes from two things: how grippy the tread is and how much weight is on it.

Traction equals grip times weight.

The most a wheel can push before it slips is its grip (the tread's coefficient of friction) times the weight pressing it down. Command more force than that and the wheel breaks loose and spins. Set the tread and weight, then push harder and harder against the wall.

In the shop

Getting grip

Traction decides push battles, acceleration, and whether you can climb a ramp. You buy it with the right wheels and earn it with weight:

🛞
TreadHigh-grip rubber or treaded wheels grip; slick plastic ones slide.
🫳
DurometerSofter wheels conform to the carpet for more grip; harder ones last longer.
⚖️
Weight on the wheelsThe driven wheels carry more of the robot's weight, the more they can push.

Power isn't gripBeyond traction, extra motor power just spins the wheels.
This is why pushing matches come down to design

When two robots shove each other, the one with more traction wins, no matter who has stronger motors. So teams chase grip: grippy tread, soft durometer, and putting as much weight as possible over the drive wheels. There's a balance, though, more grip also means more scrub when turning (Lesson 5.2), so a robot tuned to push hard can be harder to spin. Like everything on a drivetrain, it's a tradeoff you choose on purpose.

Going further · optional

Traction has a hidden ceiling: weight transfer. When you stop hard, weight shifts to the front wheels and the rears go light, just like how the back wheel of your bike can skip up if you grab only the front brake. So the wheels that can grip the most change moment to moment. Advanced teams also weigh grip against wear: a sticky soft tread wins push battles but can wear down over a whole event, while a harder wheel grips a little less and lasts the season. Which would you pick for a robot built around shoving?

Show the thinking

For a robot built to shove, lean toward the grippier soft tread, because push battles are decided by traction and you do not want to give that up. The wear problem is manageable: tread is cheap and quick to swap, so plan to re-tread between matches or carry spare wheels rather than trade away grip. The one place a harder wheel earns a look is if your driven wheels rarely get a break and you cannot service them, but for a dedicated pusher, grip wins.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What sets the maximum force a wheel can push before slipping?
Traction limit = coefficient of friction × weight on the wheel. That's the most it can push or accelerate. More motor power past that point just spins the wheel.
Question 2
You command more drive force than the wheels' traction. What happens?
Once you exceed traction, the wheel spins on the carpet. You can't push harder than your grip allows, no matter how much power you add.
Question 3
Two robots are shoving each other. Which one wins?
Pushing matches come down to traction, not motor power. Grippier tread, softer durometer, and more weight over the drive wheels all add traction and win the shove.

Power, MotionCore & CAN

Power and
motors.

The brain needs power, and the motors need wiring. That is the job of MotionCore, a companion module that handles power and CAN distribution. One short cable links the two boards. Some of the FRC power story is settled, and an important part of it is not yet.

Alpha preview · this will change

SystemCore is still in alpha and beta testing. The details here are based on what FIRST and WPILib have shared so far and they are not final, so confirm anything important against the official sources on the course page before you rely on it.

Meet MotionCore, the power-and-motors partner.

SystemCore is the brain, but a brain does not hand out power or run a tangle of motor wiring. That is MotionCore's job. MotionCore is a companion module, primarily a power and CAN distribution device that makes it easy to connect motors and smart sensors. In FTC, MotionCore also handles encoder input and actuator support. Think of SystemCore as the rider and MotionCore as the bike's frame and chain that actually move things.

The bridge

One cable links the two boards

SystemCore connects to MotionCore through a "MotionCore bridge port." It uses a 4-pin Molex Micro-Fit+ connector, and that single cable does two jobs at once: it carries power to SystemCore, and it carries CAN FD communication between the two boards. So one short, sturdy plug both feeds the brain and lets the two halves talk.

Two jobs, one plug

It helps to picture a bike with a single cable that both powers your headlight and lets it talk to the speedometer. Here the bridge cable powers SystemCore and carries the data between SystemCore and MotionCore. The two devices talk over CAN FD at 1 Mbps, which is fast enough to leave plenty of room for quick device updates, the same CAN FD idea from the brain lesson.

Not settled yet

The FRC power story is still open

Here is the honest part. For FRC specifically, the full power-wiring picture is not finalized. Be careful about taking any of these as final until the official sources say so.

Still being decided for FRC

Several power questions are not settled yet for FRC: exactly what the battery connects to, whether today's Power Distribution Hub (the PDH) stays in the picture, what the input voltage will be, where the brownout threshold (the low-voltage point where the system protects itself) will land, and even MotionCore's exact role. FIRST has so far described MotionCore as aimed mainly at the FTC program, so how FRC teams will distribute power is not locked in. Do not assume answers to these. Watch the official sources for updates.

What still holds true

The battery fundamentals do not change. There is still one 12 V battery, it still needs fusing to stay safe, and a brownout is still what happens when the voltage sags too low under heavy load. The Build course already teaches these ideas, so the concepts from build-lesson-41, build-lesson-42, and build-lesson-43 still apply even while the SystemCore wiring details get finalized.

Going further · optional

Notice how the design separates "thinking" (SystemCore) from "muscle and power" (MotionCore), with one bridge cable between them. That split shows up in lots of machines, from electric bikes to cars, because it keeps the delicate computer away from the heavy current. Here is one to discuss with a mentor: until the FRC power wiring is locked in, what is the safest way for a team to plan a robot, design around the parts that are confirmed, or wait for every detail first? Why?

Show the thinking

Waiting for every detail usually loses you time you cannot get back, so the safer path is to build on what is already confirmed and leave room for what is not. A thoughtful answer leans on the parts that are not changing, the single 12 V battery, the need for fusing, and the basic brownout idea, and it designs the parts that touch the open questions to be easy to adjust later. For FRC the power-wiring picture is genuinely unsettled, including how the battery connects, whether the PDH stays, and even MotionCore's exact role, so the smart move is to keep those choices flexible and check the official sources before committing.

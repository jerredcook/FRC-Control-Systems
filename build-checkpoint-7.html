Question 1
A new game is released. Your team wants to start cutting metal that night to get a head start. What is the right first move instead?
You cannot design for a game you do not understand. Good teams understand the game, list requirements, brainstorm concepts, then choose, before any metal gets cut. Skipping that is how teams rebuild in week five.
Question 2
Three concepts are on the table and the loudest voice is winning the argument. A teammate suggests a decision matrix. What does it actually give you?
A decision matrix rates each concept on weighted criteria and totals them, turning the choice into a defensible number instead of the loudest opinion. Set the weights before you score, or it merely dresses up a gut call.
Question 3
You finish a beautiful CAD assembly of the whole robot. Why is mating every part together in the assembly worth the effort?
An assembly lets you build the robot on screen first, catching interferences and clearance problems where fixing them costs a drag instead of a sheet of aluminum. It is the design-twice, build-once version of measure twice, cut once.
Question 4
You are unsure whether your intake will actually grab this year's game piece. What is the fastest, cheapest way to find out before committing real material?
Prototype the one thing you are least sure about, at the cheapest rung that answers it. A shape-or-reach question is cardboard; a real-load question may need wood or metal. Twenty minutes of cardboard can save a week of aluminum rework.
Question 5
Your shop has a bandsaw and a drill press but no CNC mill. One bracket design needs a precise milled pocket; an equally good design is a flat plate with holes on a grid. Which do you draw, and what principle is that?
Design for manufacture means choosing geometry, stock, and tolerances that fit your real tools and time. A part needing a machine you do not own is useless, and the simple, standard part is also faster to remake when it breaks at an event. That ties straight back to reliability winning matches.

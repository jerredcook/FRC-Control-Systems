Belts & Pulleys

Quiet and
clean.

A timing belt does chain's job with none of the noise, grease, or stretch. The toothed belt grips toothed pulleys so it can't slip. The one catch: a belt comes in a fixed length, so the pulley spacing has to match it.

The belt length fixes the spacing.

Pick your two pulleys and a belt, and the center distance between them is determined, you can't just slide them anywhere like a tensioned chain. Choose a belt and pulleys below: the spacing follows, and a belt that's too short simply won't fit.

Bigger belt, pulleys farther apart. Shrink the belt or grow the pulleys too far and they'd have to overlap, the belt won't reach, and the demo flags it. In real life you choose the belt and the layout together.

In the shop

Belt vs chain

Belts and chain do the same job. Belts win on smoothness and cleanliness; chain wins on flexibility of layout. Most modern FRC mechanisms use timing belts:

🤫
Quiet & cleanNo metal-on-metal clatter, and no oil to fling around.
🦷
Toothed = no slipGT2 and HTD belts mesh with pulley teeth, so they can't slip like a flat belt.
📏
Fixed lengthBelts come in set tooth counts, so the center distance is constrained.
🔧
Less maintenanceNo lubrication and almost no stretch, set it and forget it.
Design the layout and the belt together

Because belts come in discrete lengths, you can't pick the pulley positions first and hope a belt fits. Teams design the two together: choose a belt tooth count, and place the pulleys at the exact center distance it needs (often with one pulley on a small adjustable slot for fine-tuning). Chain is more forgiving of layout because you can add a tensioner or a half-link, which is why chain still shows up where the geometry is awkward.

Going further · optional

Here is a puzzle teams hit constantly: a 30T and a 36T pulley with a 100T belt land at one exact spacing, but your frame needs them 2mm farther apart. What do you change? You can step up to the next belt length, swap a pulley tooth count, or add a tensioning idler. See if you can find which moves help and which fight you, that hunt for a belt and pulley combo that fits a real layout is most of belt drive design.

Checkpoint

Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
What's a timing belt's main advantage over chain?
A toothed belt runs quiet, throws no oil, and barely stretches, so it needs almost no maintenance. Chain clatters and needs lubrication and tensioning.
Question 2
Why is a belt drive's center distance constrained?
A belt is a closed loop with a set number of teeth. For given pulleys, only one center distance makes that belt fit, so you design the layout around the belt length.
Question 3
Why doesn't a GT2 timing belt slip like an old flat belt?
Timing belts are toothed and run on toothed pulleys. The teeth interlock, so the belt drives positively without slipping, just like chain and sprockets.

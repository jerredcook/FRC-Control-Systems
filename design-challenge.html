How this grades your design

It uses the standard first-order motor model. A NEO gives about 2.6 Nm at stall, spins 5880 rpm free, and its current climbs in a straight line from 1.8 A free to 105 A at stall, so the harder each motor pushes, the more amps it pulls. We charge 10 percent for gear friction and pad travel times 15 percent for spin-up, then check three things: does it move, does it hit the time or speed target, and does each motor stay under its 40 A breaker. Real designs deserve ReCalc's exact curves; this page keeps every step of the arithmetic visible so you can follow it. New to sizing? Do the sizing lesson first. Quick check

Pick an answer to see why.

Question 1
Your lift design fails with "too slow". Which change actually speeds up the rope, assuming the motor still has torque to spare?
Rope speed is motor speed divided by the reduction, times the drum radius. Shrinking the reduction or growing the drum both raise the rope speed, exactly like shifting a bike into a higher gear. But both also demand more torque from the motor, which raises the current, so you re-check those two numbers before calling it fixed.
Question 2
The verdict says "breaker trip risk: 62 A per motor on a 40 A breaker". What actually fixes it?
On the NEO's line, current tracks torque: about 1.8 A when free, 105 A at stall. Anything that shrinks the torque each motor must produce, more reduction, sharing the load across more motors, or a smaller drum, slides you back down that line. Less reduction does the opposite: it loads each motor harder, which pushes it away from free speed and pulls even more current.
Question 3
A NEO is listed at 5880 rpm free speed and 2.6 Nm stall torque. What do those two numbers mean for a real mechanism?
Free speed happens at zero load and stall torque happens at zero speed, so a motor never delivers both at once. Every real job sits somewhere on the line between them, like pedaling a bike: spinning easy and fast, or grinding slow and hard, never both. Sizing means using gears to park the motor at a comfortable spot on that line.

Question 1
You have a 1x2 aluminum tube to use as a long arm that bends under load. To make it stiffest against bending, you should orient it so the:
Bending stiffness rises with the cube of the height in the load direction, so the tall 2 inch dimension does far more work. Same tube, same weight, but orienting it tall is much stiffer than laying it flat.
Question 2
You need a clear guard panel that will take repeated hits from a game piece without shattering. Which material?
Polycarbonate bends and springs back, so it survives impacts. Acrylic looks identical but is brittle and cracks on the first hard hit. Aluminum would take a permanent dent rather than recover.
Question 3
Your team can buy a proven swerve module or machine its gears from scratch. The module sits on the critical path to a driving robot. The veteran call is to:
COTS means commercial off-the-shelf, a part a vendor already perfected. Buy the proven, high-stress precision parts on the critical path, and spend shop time on the geometry unique to your design. Good and done beats perfect and late.
Question 4
A tube bolted to a gusset with a single bolt keeps swinging out of square under load. What is happening, and the fix?
A single bolt is a pivot the tube can swing around. Two bolts spread as far apart as the gusset allows fix the angle and give a wider lever, so each bolt carries less of the twisting load and the joint stays rigid.
Question 5
You drill a 0.250 in hole in a polycarbonate bellypan with a sharp square inside corner right next to it. Connecting back to tolerance and stress, the better design is to:
A sharp inside corner concentrates stress, which is where a crack starts and runs. A fillet gives the load a wider path and drops the peak stress. A press fit in plastic only adds more stress, the opposite of what you want here.

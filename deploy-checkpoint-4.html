Question 1
Your shooter spins up to the right speed early in a match but comes up short late in the match when the battery has drained. You currently call set(0.6). What is the better fix?
set(percent) delivers a fraction of whatever the battery is at, so output sags as the battery drains. setVoltage(volts) asks for real volts and compensates for sag, giving repeatable behavior from the first match to the last. Review: Lesson 4.1 · Motors & Controllers
Question 2
An elevator runs through a 12:1 gearbox. The motor encoder reads 24 rotations. How do you turn that into mechanism rotations, and what config makes getPosition() do it for you?
A 12:1 reduction means the motor turns 12 times per mechanism turn, so you divide: 24 / 12 = 2 mechanism rotations. Setting SensorToMechanismRatio bakes that reduction in once, so getPosition() and getVelocity() report mechanism units and you stop doing the math everywhere. Review: Lesson 4.2 · Encoders & Position
Question 3
After every power-on your arm's relative encoder reads 0 wherever it happens to be sitting, so "level" is in a different spot each boot. What is the standard fix?
Relative encoders forget their position at power-on. Homing drives the mechanism to a fixed physical reference, a limit switch read with DigitalInput.get(), and sets a known zero there, so the readings mean the same thing every match. An absolute encoder avoids homing by reporting true angle after a power cycle. Review: Lesson 4.4 · Switches & Digital Sensors
Question 4
A driver complains the robot creeps when the sticks are centered. You read the stick with getLeftY() and pass it straight to the drive. What two fixes address the complaint and the feel?
A released stick does not rest at exactly 0, so a deadband (for example MathUtil.applyDeadband) treats near-center as 0 and stops the creep. getLeftY() returns negative when you push forward, so negate it so push-forward means drive-forward. Squaring the input is an optional extra for finer control near center. Review: Lesson 4.5 · The Gamepad
Question 5
Back in Part 3, subsystems update in periodic() every 20 ms. You want a live tunable shooter speed you can change from the dashboard without redeploying. Using NetworkTables, what is the catch?
NetworkTables is a shared key-value table that flows both ways: telemetry goes robot to dashboard, tuning goes dashboard to robot. A value you want to tune live must be read with getNumber(), not written with putNumber() every loop, or your loop keeps stamping over the change you made. Review: Lesson 4.6 · NetworkTables & Dashboards
